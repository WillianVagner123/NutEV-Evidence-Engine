Abstract 
Background: The Mediterranean diet is widely recognized for its cardiovascular benefits, 
but its specific effects on atherogenic indices remain unclear—particularly in individuals 
seeking a weight-loss dietary program, where excess fat mass may mitigate the diet’s 
protective effects.  
Methods: To explore this relationship, a cross-sectional study was conducted involving 
10,286 participants enrolled in a weight-loss dietary program. Anthropometric and 
biochemical data were collected, and adherence to the Mediterranean diet was assessed 
using the 14-item Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener questionnaire. Lipid profiles—
including total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides—were analyzed to calculate the 
following atherogenic indices: atherogenic index of plasma, Castelli risk indices I and II, 
lipoprotein combine index, atherogenic coefficient, and atherogenic combined index. 
Results: Multivariate linear regression models—adjusted for sex, age, body mass index, 
smoking, physical activity, sociodemographic factors, and use of lipid-lowering 
medications—showed that each one-point increase in the Mediterranean diet adherence 
score was significantly associated with reductions in the atherogenic index of plasma 
(−0.003, 95%CI: 0.006, −0.000), the atherogenic coefficient (−0.013, 95%CI: −0.024, 
−0.001), Castelli risk index I (−0.013, 95%CI: −0.024, −0.001), and the atherogenic 
combined index (−0.004, 95%CI: −0.007, −0.000), with a marginal association observed 
for Castelli risk index II (−0.009, 95%CI: −0.019, 0.000). Significant associations were also 
observed for total cholesterol (−3.453 mg/dl, 95%CI: –5.911, –0.995), LDL (−3.225 mg/dl, 
95%CI: –5.402, –1.048), and HDL concentrations (+0.255 mg/dl, 95%CI: 0.098, 0.412). 
However, except for HDL, these associations lost statistical significance after adjusting for 
body fat percentage. Significant interactions between Mediterranean diet adherence score 
and body fat percentage were observed for several atherogenic indices, including the

atherogenic coefficient, Castelli risk index I, Castelli risk index II, and the atherogenic

favorable lipid profiles and atherogenic indices, these benefits are modulated by body 
composition, particularly fat mass. These findings highlight the importance of integrated 
dietary strategies that combine nutritional quality with body fat reduction to support 
cardiovascular prevention.

Introduction 
In 2021, the global number of cardiovascular disease (CVD) cases had reached 523 
million, nearly doubling from 271 million in 1990 [1]. This gradual yet substantial increase 
has made CVD the leading cause of morbidity, disability, and mortality worldwide [2]. 
According to the European Society of Cardiology, CVDs account for approximately 45% of 
all deaths in Europe and 34% of total mortality in Italy [3]. In addition to the serious human 
consequences, there is a significant economic burden: current estimates place the indirect 
annual costs of $237 billion in 2015, with projections rising to $368 billion by 2035 [4]. 
CVDs are multifactorial conditions resulting from a complex interplay of genetic, lifestyle, 
and environmental factors [1]. A key pathophysiological mechanism underlying CVD is 
atherosclerosis, a progressive process characterized by the buildup of plaques in arterial 
walls that can begin as early as childhood Although initially asymptomatic, atherosclerotic 
plaques may eventually grow large enough to impair blood flow, leading to major clinical 
outcomes such as coronary artery disease, cerebrovascular disease, peripheral artery 
disease, and aortic atherosclerosis which collectively represent the four major 
manifestations of CVD [5]. 
Traditionally, the assessment of atherosclerotic risk has relied on clinical history and 
standard lipid parameters such as total cholesterol, LDL-C, HDL-C, and triglycerides. 
Although these markers remain fundamental for cardiovascular risk stratification, they may 
not fully capture the complexity of lipid-related atherogenesis [6]. In recent years, several 
composite lipid parameters, collectively known as atherogenic indices (AI), have been 
proposed to provide a more integrated view of lipid-related risk [7]. These indices, 
including the Atherogenic Index of Plasma (AIP), Castelli’s Risk Indices (CRI-I and CRI-II), 
the Atherogenic Coefficient (AC), the Lipoprotein Combine Index (LCI), and Atherogenic 
Combined Index (ACI) combine multiple lipid variables into single measures. Emerging

evidence suggests that atherogenic indices may complement traditional lipid parameters 
and provide additional information on cardiovascular risk [8-10]. For example, one study 
reported that AIP, LCI, AC, CRI-I, and CRI-II were stronger predictors of the coronary 
artery aneurysm risk than conventional lipid parameters [8]. Another study found that AIP 
better predicted mortality risk than individual cholesterol measures in older adults [10]. 
Additionally, AIP has been shown to predict long-term cardiovascular outcomes in 
community-based cohorts independently of traditional lipid parameters [9]. 
Numerous modifiable factors contribute to the development of atherosclerosis, among 
which diet plays a central role [11]. In particular, the Mediterranean diet, characterized by 
high consumption of fruits, vegetables, legumes, whole grains, fish, low-fat dairy products, 
nuts, seeds, and extra virgin olive oil as the main source of fat, has been widely 
recognized for its cardioprotective properties [12, 13]. Several meta-analyses have 
demonstrated that adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with reduced CVD 
risk and improvements in cardiovascular risk factors [12-14], including reductions in body 
fat [14], total cholesterol levels [15], vascular function [16], systemic inflammation and the 
progression of subclinical atherosclerosis [17]. However, its specific impact on lipid-derived 
atherogenic indices remains unclear, as no studies to date have directly examined this 
association. This question is particularly relevant in individuals at elevated cardiovascular 
risk, such as those following a weight-loss dietary intervention. In these individuals, excess 
adiposity contributes to chronic inflammation, insulin resistance, and disturbances in lipid 
metabolism through multiple interrelated mechanisms, including increased release of free 
fatty acids, elevated proinflammatory cytokine production, and enhanced hepatic VLDL-C 
synthesis, which may collectively attenuate the protective effects of the Mediterranean diet 
[18, 19]. It was therefore hypothesized that greater adherence to the Mediterranean diet 
would be associated with more favorable atherogenic indices and lipid profiles in

individuals seeking a weight-loss dietary program. Furthermore, body fat was 
hypothesized to play a key role in this relationship by acting either as a mediator or an 
effect modifier, potentially attenuating the cardioprotective effects of the Mediterranean 
diet in individuals with higher adiposity. 
Therefore, the aim of this study was to evaluate the relationship between adherence to the 
Mediterranean diet and AI and lipid profiles in a large cohort of individuals seeking a 
weight-loss dietary program, while accounting for body fat, thereby providing novel 
evidence on the role of adiposity in modulating diet–atherogenic risk relationships.

Material and methods 
Study design and sample selection 
A total of 10,286 participants were recruited from those attending the International Center 
for the Assessment of Nutritional Status (ICANS) at the University of Milan from 
September 2010 to September 2022. Enrollment was restricted to individuals aged over 18 
who did not meet any of the exclusion criteria: diabetes, cardiovascular disease, cancer 
within the past 5 years, hepatitis C virus infection, hepatitis B virus infection, liver cirrhosis, 
neurological disease, gastrointestinal disease, cardiac failure, renal failure, pulmonary 
insufficiency and use of drugs known to cause lipodystrophy. At recruitment, all 
participants underwent a series of assessments, starting with a medical interview to 
evaluate their medical history, current drug therapies, smoking habits, and physical 
activity. Physical activity was assessed by asking participants whether they practiced any 
structured activity and, if so, how many hours per week they spent on it. Individuals 
reporting ≥2 hours per week of structured physical activity were classified as active [20]. 
Additionally, participants underwent a nutritional evaluation, during which anthropometric 
measurements were taken, and a fasting blood sample was collected. Finally, all 
participants were instructed to complete a validated questionnaire to assess their 
adherence to the Mediterranean diet. The study was conducted in accordance with the 
guidelines of the Helsinki Declaration, and each participant read and signed a written 
informed consent. The Ethics Committee of the University of Milan approved the study 
procedures (study protocol No. 23/2016). 
Anthropometric measurements 
All anthropometric data were collected based on the international guidelines. Before 
undergoing all measurements, participants were asked to wear only underwear and socks.

Weight was measured using a column scale with a precision of 0.1 kg while height was 
assessed using a stadiometer and rounded to the nearest 0.5 cm. Body Mass Index (BMI) 
was then calculated by dividing weight (kg) by the square of height (m²) and categorized 
following World Health Organization (WHO) standards: underweight (BMI <18.5 kg/m²), 
normal weight (BMI 18.5–25 kg/m²), overweight (BMI 25–29.9 kg/m²), or obese (BMI ≥30 
kg/m²). Waist circumference was measured with a non-elastic tape placed horizontally 
between the lower border of the rib cage and the iliac crest. Additionally, skinfold thickness 
at the triceps, biceps, subscapular, and suprailiac sites was recorded using a plicometer 
with a precision of 0.2 mm. For each site, the measurements were averaged from three 
consecutive readings. Following international standards, skinfolds were measured on the 
non-dominant side of each participant. Body density (ρ) was estimated using the equations 
from Durnin and Womersley [21], which account for both gender and age. Body fat 
percentage was calculated using Siri’s formula: Body fat % = (4.95 / ρ - 4.50) × 100. 
Adherence to the Mediterranean diet 
The 14-item MEDAS questionnaire was employed to evaluate adherence to the 
Mediterranean Diet, a short dietary screening tool validated in the PREMIDED trial [22]. 
This questionnaire consists of 14 questions designed to assess the frequency of 
consumption of foods typical of both Mediterranean and Western diets. A point is given for 
each food item that aligns with the Mediterranean diet criterion. Briefly, 1-point was 
attributed for each of the following: (1) olive oil as the main cooking fat; (2) olive oil ≥ 4 
tablespoons/day; (3) vegetables ≥ 2 servings/day; (4) fruit ≥ 3 servings/day; (5) red or 
processed meat < 1 serving/day; (6) butter or cream or margarine < 1/day; (7) sugar-
sweetened beverages < 1/day; (8) wine ≥ 3 glasses/week; (9) legumes ≥ 3 servings/week; 
(10) fish/seafood ≥ 3 servings/week; (11) commercial sweets and confectionery < 3/week;

(atherogenic indices and lipid profile parameters) was assessed using a pre-specified 
sequence of linear regression models defined a priori based on biological plausibility: 
Model 1 (M1): Unadjusted 
Model 2 (M2): Adjusted for sex (binary: 0 = female, 1 = male) and age (continuous, in 
years) 
Model 3 (M3): M2 plus adjustments for BMI (continuous, kg/m²), smoking status

Model 4 (M4): M3 plus body fat percentage (continuous) 
Model 5 (M5): Given the presence of significant interactions between MEDAS score and 
body fat percentage for several outcomes, the interaction term (MEDAS score#body fat) 
was included in the model when statistically significant

and the outcomes of interest. Model 2 evaluates this association accounting for sex and 
age. Model 3 further adjusts for BMI, sociodemographic factors, and lipid-lowering 
treatment. Model 4 incorporates body composition into the analysis. This model 
investigates whether fat mass acts as a mediator of the association between adherence to 
the Mediterranean diet and the outcomes of interest. Finally, Model 5 assesses the effect 
of adherence to the Mediterranean diet in relation to body composition, while controlling for

all other potential confounders. This model investigates whether fat mass acts instead as a 
modifier of the association between adherence to the Mediterranean diet and the 
outcomes of interest. Collinearity was assessed using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) 
for each predictor and for the overall model; all VIF values were <5, indicating negligible 
multicollinearity. Homoscedasticity and potential violations of the linearity assumption 
between continuous predictors and the dependent variable were assessed both 
graphically, using residual-versus-fitted plots, and through statistical tests. The Breusch-
Pagan test was applied to check for heteroscedasticity, and robust confidence intervals 
were calculated for all models where heteroscedasticity was detected. Multivariable 
fractional polynomials were used to model potential non-linear associations between 
continuous predictors and the outcome. Several transformations of age and BMI were 
selected depending on the model. However, there was only a modest gain in the linearity 
of continuous predictors when transformations were applied; therefore, to preserve 
interpretability and readability, all continuous covariates were kept in their original, 
untransformed form. The significance of interactions was formally tested using Wald tests, 
and all interactions reported in the manuscript are statistically supported. To address 
potential overadjustment, additional analyses were performed using a more parsimonious 
model that included only variables remaining significant (Supplementary tables S1-S2). 
Results were consistent with those of the main analyses, suggesting that overadjustment 
was unlikely. In addition, we evaluated the interaction between MEDAS and sex. Analyses 
showed that the MEDAS#body fat and MEDAS#sex interactions were both significant 
when tested separately, with identical R² values, suggesting that they explained the same 
proportion of variability. Moreover, when the two interaction terms were included 
simultaneously in the same model, both lost statistical significances, indicating redundancy 
between the modifiers. In light of this, and considering that body fat showed a greater 
number of significant interactions and is a continuous and modifiable variable, we decided

Discussion 
In the present study, adherence to the Mediterranean diet among individuals seeking a 
weight-loss program was associated with lower atherogenic indices and a more favorable 
lipid profile—specifically, lower levels of LDL-C and total cholesterol, and higher HDL-C 
concentrations. Although the effect sizes observed in the models were numerically small, 
their cumulative impact across the full range of adherence to the Mediterranean diet is 
clinically meaningful. When considered over the entire MEDAS scale, these associations 
translate into substantial reductions in atherogenic indices, supporting the biological 
relevance of the findings at both the individual and population levels. Interestingly, for 
several atherogenic indices and lipid parameters, a significant interaction between 
Mediterranean diet adherence and fat mass was identified, indicating that adiposity may 
act as an effect modifier. For AIP specifically, the associations disappeared after adjusting 
for fat mass, suggesting that adiposity may mediate this relationship 
Some previous studies have examined the relationship between diet and atherogenic 
indices. In a study of 150 women aged 18 to 45 years diagnosed with PCOS, higher 
adherence to a diet rich in phytochemicals—measured by the Dietary Phytochemical Index 
(DPI)—was associated with reduced AIP levels [25] . In another study involving 8,870 
adults, Ravansar et al. reported a positive association between higher Dietary 
Inflammatory Index (DII) scores and increased levels of AIP, CRI-I, CRI-II, LCI, and AC 
[26]. Furthermore, the Isfahan Cohort Study, conducted on 2,256 adults, found that higher 
scores on the Healthy Lifestyle Score (HLS) were significantly associated with lower levels 
of AIP, CRI-I, CRI-II, ACI, and AC [27]. To the best of our knowledge, only one study—
conducted on a small sample of 51 participants—has reported that the combination of the 
Mediterranean diet and physical activity was associated with a reduction in AIP [28].

This study, based on a large cohort of participants, further contributes to the literature by 
demonstrating that adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with more favorable 
atherogenic indices—beyond AIP—and that this effect appears to be mediated by an 
overall improvement in lipid homeostasis. Specifically, lower levels of total and LDL-C 
cholesterol, and higher HDL-C concentrations were observed in participants with greater 
adherence to the Mediterranean diet, consistent with findings from several literature 
reviews [29].  
Although the exact mechanisms remain partially unclear, several synergistic effects among 
the various components of the Mediterranean diet may explain its beneficial impact on lipid 
metabolism and atherosclerotic risk. This dietary pattern is rich in unsaturated fatty acids—
particularly omega-3 and omega-9—mainly derived from extra virgin olive oil, nuts, seeds, 
and oily fish. These healthy fats help lower plasma triglycerides and LDL-C cholesterol 
while increasing HDL-C cholesterol levels [30, 31]. The Mediterranean diet is also high in 
soluble fiber from fruits, vegetables, legumes, and whole grains, which contributes to 
reductions in total and LDL-C cholesterol through fermentation by gut microbiota and 
enhanced bile acid excretion [32, 33]. Moderate red wine consumption adds polyphenols, 
particularly resveratrol, which may prevent LDL-C oxidation, improve endothelial function, 
and reduce inflammatory responses involved in atherogenesis [34], while ethanol has 
been associated with increased HDL-C levels [35]. Importantly, the Mediterranean diet 
limits foods high in saturated fats—such as red meat, full-fat dairy products, and ultra-
processed foods—which are linked to adverse lipid profiles, higher LDL-C/HDL-C ratios, 
and increased oxidative stress and inflammation [36, 37], as well as excessive caloric 
intake and fat accumulation that exacerbate cardiovascular risk [38, 39]. 
Interestingly, for atherogenic indices and lipid parameters, such as AC, CRI-I, CRI-II, ACI, 
total cholesterol, and LDL-C, a significant interaction between adherence to the

Mediterranean diet and fat mass was observed. Specifically, the strength of the 
association between adherence to the Mediterranean diet and these markers decreased 
as fat mass increased. These findings suggest a modifying effect of adipose tissue, 
whereby excess body fat may progressively diminish the beneficial effects of the 
Mediterranean diet. This hypothesis is supported by previous studies. In the observational 
ATTICA study, adherence to the Mediterranean diet was only modestly associated with 
lower total cholesterol levels among individuals with overweight or obesity [40]. Similar 
results have been reported in interventional trials, where, in the absence of caloric 
restriction or weight loss, the Mediterranean diet failed to improve metabolic and lipid 
profiles [40-42]. Overall, this evidence suggests that the Mediterranean diet should be 
combined with weight reduction strategies in individuals with overweight or obesity. This 
finding may be partly explained by the metabolic activity of adipose tissue, especially 
visceral fat (VAT), which releases free fatty acids, adipokines, and inflammatory cytokines. 
These substances can impair lipid metabolism by increasing hepatic triglyceride 
production, reducing HDL-C levels, and promoting LDL-C oxidation [19, 43-46]. As a 
result, the excess release of these mediators may counteract the beneficial effects of the 
Mediterranean diet on lipid and atherogenic profiles in individuals with higher body fat. 
Importantly, excess adiposity is also associated with increased insulin resistance, which 
represents a key metabolic mechanism linking fat accumulation to adverse lipid 
alterations. Insulin resistance can further exacerbate hypertriglyceridemia and low HDL-C-
cholesterol, thereby potentially counteracting the beneficial effects of the Mediterranean 
diet on lipid and atherogenic profiles in individuals with higher body fat. It is also plausible 
that the modifying effect of adiposity reflects underlying sex-related differences in body 
composition. Women and men differ substantially in total and regional fat distribution, and 
these differences are known to influence lipid metabolism and atherogenic profiles. In line 
with this biological framework, the interaction between MEDAS and sex was found to

explain essentially the same variability as the interaction between MEDAS and body fat; 
moreover, when both interaction terms were included simultaneously in the same model, 
neither retained statistical significance. This redundancy suggests that sex-related 
differences in the association between Mediterranean diet adherence and lipid outcomes 
are largely mediated by differences in body fat, supporting adiposity as the biological 
driver. 
For AIP only, the association with adherence to the Mediterranean diet disappeared after 
adjusting for fat mass, and no interaction was observed. This suggests that adipose tissue 
acts as a mediator in the association with the Mediterranean diet. In other words, greater 
adherence to the Mediterranean diet may lead to reduced fat mass, which in turn 
contributes to lower AIP values. This hypothesis is supported by a previous study, in which 
higher adherence to the Mediterranean diet was associated with lower abdominal obesity, 
particularly with reduced visceral adipose tissue (VAT) [47].  
Strengths and limitations 
This study has several strengths. The large sample size increased statistical power and 
the precision of the estimates. In addition, adjustment was made for a wide range of 
sociodemographic and lifestyle factors that could confound the relationship between diet 
and lipid outcomes. A further strength of this study is the analysis of lipid parameters and 
atherogenic indices as continuous variables. This approach captures the full metabolic 
variability of the cohort and preserves statistical power by avoiding the limitations of 
categorical stratification. Furthermore, using continuous data ensures that the findings 
remain robust regardless of potential future updates to clinical cut-offs for dyslipidemia. 
Nevertheless, future research should explore whether specific dyslipidemic phenotypes 
exhibit differential responses to the Mediterranean pattern. Finally, a validated 
questionnaire to screen for adherence to the Mediterranean diet was used.

Nonetheless, this study has several limitations. First, its cross-sectional design prevents 
any inference of causality. Second, body composition was evaluated through skinfold 
measurements, which, while not considered the gold standard, offer a practical estimation 
of fat-free and fat mass. Although more accurate techniques exist, their application in 
routine clinical settings is limited by high costs, lengthy procedures, and the requirement 
for highly specialized personnel. In contrast, skinfold assessments are faster, easier to 
perform, and, in our center, are characterized by very low intra- and inter-operator 
variability [48]. Third, participants were recruited from a single center and were primarily 
individuals seeking dietary counseling for weight loss. While this may introduce selection 
bias and limit generalizability to the general population, the sample reflects a clinically 
relevant subgroup at increased cardiometabolic risk. Fourth, although the exclusion criteria 
were implemented to reduce confounding and strengthen internal validity, this approach 
may limit the generalizability of the findings to individuals with established chronic 
conditions such as cardiovascular disease or diabetes. Nevertheless, the study population, 
which includes more than 10,000 individuals actively seeking dietary counseling for weight 
loss, reflects a relevant and expanding segment of the population at increased 
cardiometabolic risk. Fifth, the sample was predominantly composed of women, reflecting 
the higher tendency of females to seek a dietary weight-loss programs [49, 50]. This sex 
imbalance may limit the generalizability of the findings to a more balanced population. 
Sixth, dietary adherence was assessed through a self-reported questionnaire, which may 
be subject to recall and social desirability bias. Moreover, the absence of detailed 
quantitative dietary data (e.g., total energy intake or macronutrient distribution) prevented 
exploration of potential confounding or mediation through specific dietary components. 
Seventh, no formal correction for multiple hypothesis testing was applied, given the 
exploratory nature of the study and the use of pre-specified regression models. Therefore,

the possibility of type I error cannot be completely excluded. Finally, as with any 
observational study, potential residual confounding could not be ruled out. 
Conclusion 
In conclusion, adherence to the Mediterranean diet is associated with more favorable 
atherogenic and lipid profiles in individuals seeking weight‑loss treatment; however, these 
benefits are significantly modulated by body fat content. Excess adiposity may attenuate or 
even neutralize the positive effects of the Mediterranean diet on lipid parameters. 
Importantly, these findings highlight the need to implement Mediterranean diet–based 
dietary interventions within integrated clinical strategies that also target body fat reduction. 
Such an approach may optimize cardiovascular risk management and improve patient 
care by jointly addressing dietary quality and body composition in individuals at increased 
cardiometabolic risk.
