Abstract
Importance: Accurate risk prediction is essential for targeted prevention 
of type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). However, the global applicability and 
methodological rigor of existing prediction models remain uncertain.
Objective: To systematically review and meta-analyze the geographic 
distribution, methodological quality, and predictive performance of all 
published T2DM risk prediction models.
Data Sources: PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, CNKI, 
WanFang, and VIP were searched from inception to December 2025 
(eAppendix S1 in the Supplement).
Study Selection: Studies that developed or validated a multivariable 
prediction model for incident T2DM in general adult populations and 
reported at least one performance measure.
Data Extraction and Synthesis: Two reviewers independently extracted 
data and assessed risk of bias using the PROBAST tool. A random-effects 
meta-analysis was used to pool C-statistics. Heterogeneity was explored

via subgroup analyses and meta-regression. The study followed TRIPOD-
SRMA and PRISMA reporting guidelines.
Main Outcomes and Measures: The primary outcome was the geographic 
origin of models. Secondary outcomes included pooled measures of 
discrimination (C-statistic/AUC) stratified by region and an overall 
assessment of methodological quality (PROBAST).
Results: A total of 65 studies comprising 97 distinct prediction models 
were included (eTable 1). Geographic distribution was highly skewed, with 
70.1% of models developed in Asian populations (China: 47.4%; Japan: 
13.4%; South Korea: 9.3%), while only 7.2% originated from the US and 
4.1% from Europe. Logistic regression was used in 97.9% of models. 
External validation was performed for only 21 models (21.6%). According 
to PROBAST, 91.8% of models were at high risk of bias (eTable 2), 
primarily due to inadequate handling of missing data, lack of external 
validation, and poor calibration reporting. Meta-analysis revealed wide 
variation in discrimination by geographic region (eTable 7). US-based 
models achieved the highest pooled AUC (0.97; 95% CI, 0.94–0.99), but 
this finding is likely influenced by overfitting, small sample bias, and 
publication bias (see Discussion). European models showed a pooled AUC 
of 0.84 (0.81–0.87), while Chinese models showed the lowest performance 
(AUC, 0.79; 0.76–0.82). Due to very high heterogeneity (I² > 80% in most 
regions), these pooled estimates should be interpreted as descriptive 
summaries rather than precise estimates of true regional performance. 
Performance was lowest in prediabetic cohorts (AUC, 0.72; 0.68–0.76); 
however, this finding is preliminary due to the limited number of models 
and high heterogeneity. Funnel plot asymmetry suggested potential 
publication bias (Egger's test p=0.03); The most frequently included 
predictors were age (69.1%), body mass index (64.9%), family history of 
diabetes (44.3%), and waist circumference (39.2%) (eFigure 4 and eTable 
3).
Conclusions and Relevance: T2DM prediction models exhibit striking 
geographic inequity and poor methodological quality, with over 90% at 
high risk of bias. The substantial variation in performance by region and 
the lack of external validation critically limit their global clinical utility. 
These findings underscore an urgent need for rigorous external validation 
in diverse populations and de novo model development in under-
represented regions, guided by PROBAST and TRIPOD standards.

1. Introduction
Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) affects an estimated 537 million adults 
worldwide, with projections exceeding 783 million by 2045 [1]. Early 
identification of at-risk individuals is essential for targeted prevention, yet 
universal biochemical screening remains impractical in most settings [2]. 
Over the past two decades, numerous multivariable prediction models 
have been developed to identify high-risk individuals using readily 
available clinical and demographic variables [3,4].
A 2012 systematic review by Abbasi et al. identified 25 prediction models 
and found that fewer than one-quarter had undergone external validation 
[5]. More recently, our updated review encompassing 65 studies and 97 
models reveals that while methodological approaches have evolved, 
critical gaps persist [6]. However, a comprehensive analysis of geographic 
disparities in model development and performance has been lacking. 
Understanding whether prediction models are transportable across 
populations is crucial for global health equity; if models developed in one 
region perform poorly in another, their clinical utility is severely limited.
This systematic review and meta-analysis aimed to: (1) characterize the 
geographic distribution of all published T2DM prediction models; (2) 
evaluate their methodological quality using the PROBAST tool; (3) meta-
analyze model discrimination by geographic origin and population type; 
and (4) identify the most frequently included predictors to inform future 
research and practice.
2. Methods
2.1 Study Design and Registration
This systematic review and meta-analysis was conducted following the 
TRIPOD-SRMA [7] and PRISMA 2020 [8] statements. The protocol was 
prospectively registered on PROSPERO (CRD420261322116).
2.2 Search Strategy
We systematically searched PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane 
Library, CNKI, WanFang, and VIP from database inception through 
December 2025. The search strategy combined terms for diabetes,

prediction models, and risk scores, adapted from prior reviews (eAppendix 
S1).
2.3 Eligibility Criteria
We included studies that developed, validated, or updated a multivariable 
prediction model for incident T2DM using cohort, nested case-control, or 
case-cohort designs, and reported at least one performance measure. We 
excluded cross-sectional studies, studies restricted to gestational or type 
1 diabetes, models using only genetic or specialized biomarkers, 
conference abstracts, editorials, and narrative reviews.
2.4 Study Selection and Data Extraction
Two reviewers independently screened titles, abstracts, and full texts. 
Data extraction, following the CHARMS-PF checklist [9], included: first 
author, year, country, study design, sample size, number of events, model 
type, predictors, and performance measures (C-statistic/AUC, calibration) 
(eTable 1).
2.5 Risk of Bias Assessment
Methodological quality was assessed independently by two reviewers 
using the PROBAST tool [10,11], which evaluates risk of bias and 
applicability across four domains: participants, predictors, outcome, and 
analysis. Each domain and the overall model were rated as low, high, or 
unclear risk of bias (eTable 2).
2.6 Statistical Analysis
For the primary meta-analysis, to avoid within-study dependence, we 
selected one primary model per study (the basic model with the best-
reported discrimination, or the most complete model if no basic model was 
available), resulting in 65 independent models. Sensitivity analyses using 
all 97 models (with cluster-robust variance estimation) yielded consistent 
results (eMethods). We performed a random‑effects meta‑analysis using 
the inverse‑variance method to pool C‑statistics (AUCs). AUCs were 
transformed using the logit function [log(AUC/(1‑AUC))] to stabilize 
variance. Standard errors were derived from reported 95% confidence 
intervals; when confidence intervals were not reported, we imputed the 
mean standard error from studies that did report them. Sensitivity

analyses excluding imputed values did not materially change the results 
(eMethods) [12].
Heterogeneity was quantified using the I² statistic. Subgroup analyses 
were conducted by geographic origin (eTable 7), population type, and 
validation status (eTable 4). Meta‑regression was also used to explore the 
impact of continuous covariates (year, sample size, events per variable). 
Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots (Figure 4), Egger’s 
regression test. All analyses were performed using R version 4.1.0 (meta 
and metafor packages). Detailed statistical methods are provided in 
eMethods.

3.3 Model Performance by Geographic Region
Meta-analysis revealed substantial variation in discrimination by 
geographic origin (eTable 7). US-based models achieved the highest 
pooled AUC (0.97; 95% CI, 0.94–0.99), followed by European models (0.84; 
0.81–0.87). Chinese models showed significantly lower performance (AUC, 
0.79; 0.76–0.82; meta‑regression p<0.001 for comparison with US). 
Models from Japan and other Asian countries showed intermediate 
performance (AUC, 0.82–0.85) (Figure 5). Due to very high heterogeneity 
(I² > 80% in most regions), these pooled estimates should be interpreted 
as descriptive summaries rather than precise estimates of true regional 
performance. Performance was lowest in prediabetic cohorts (pooled AUC, 
0.72; 95% CI, 0.68–0.76) compared to general population cohorts (AUC, 
0.80; 0.78–0.82) — but this finding is based on only 19 models with 
substantial heterogeneity, so definitive conclusions await further external

3.4 Comparison of Modeling Approaches
Only one machine learning model and one Cox regression model were 
identified among the 97 models, precluding a robust comparison. The 
single machine learning model reported an AUC of 0.86 (95% CI, 0.82–
0.90), which was within the range of logistic regression models from the 
same region. Given the extreme scarcity of non‑logistic models, no 
meaningful statistical comparison could be performed.

Univariable meta‑regression (eTable 6) showed that year of publication 
(β=0.002, p=0.68), log‑sample size (β=0.01, p=0.54), and events per 
variable (β=0.003, p=0.82) were not significantly associated with model 
discrimination. Inclusion of biomarkers was significantly associated with

3.6 Publication Bias
Funnel plot asymmetry (Figure 4) suggested potential publication bias 
favoring studies reporting higher AUCs (Egger's test p=0.03), indicating 
that models with poor discrimination may be systematically under-
reported.

3.7 Most Frequent Predictors
Across all models, the most frequently included predictors were age 
(69.1%), body mass index (BMI, 64.9%), family history of diabetes (44.3%),

4.1 Principal Findings
This systematic review and meta-analysis of 97 T2DM prediction models 
yields three principal findings. First, there is a striking geographic inequity 
in model development. Over 70% of all published models were developed 
in Asian populations, with minimal representation from the US, Europe, 
and virtually none from Latin America, Africa, or the Middle East. This 
distribution does not reflect the global burden of diabetes, which is 
increasingly concentrated in low- and middle-income countries [1], and 
raises serious concerns about the global applicability of existing models.
Second, methodological quality is poor. Consistent with previous reviews 
[5,6], we found that 91.8% of models are at high risk of bias according to 
PROBAST criteria (eTable 2), and only 21.6% have undergone any form of 
external validation. The predominant issues — inadequate handling of 
missing data, lack of calibration reporting, and failure to validate 
externally — are preventable and should be addressed by strict adherence 
to established guidelines [10,11].
Third, substantial geographic variation in model performance exists, but 
its interpretation is complex (eTable 7). The significantly higher pooled 
AUCs for US-based models (0.97) compared to those from China (0.79) or 
Europe (0.84) must be interpreted with great caution. Such extremely high 
AUC values are unusual in clinical prediction models and likely reflect 
overfitting, small sample bias (many US models were developed in small 
cohorts), and/or selective reporting of positive results rather than true 
superior predictive performance. Furthermore, the high heterogeneity (I² 
> 80%) across regions indicates that the pooled AUCs may not be directly 
comparable as measures of true regional performance. The consistently 
low performance in prediabetic cohorts (AUC, 0.72) provides preliminary 
evidence that existing models have limited utility for identifying 
progression from prediabetes to diabetes, but this finding is based on only 
19 models with substantial heterogeneity, so definitive conclusions await 
further external validation studies.
4.2 Comparison with Previous Reviews
Our findings extend those of Abbasi et al. [5] and Li et al. [6] by providing 
the first comprehensive analysis of geographic disparities in T2DM 
prediction modeling. While Li et al. reported that 97.9% of models used 
logistic regression and that external validation was limited, they did not 
systematically analyze performance by geographic origin or conduct 
PROBAST assessments stratified by region. Our analysis reveals that the

problem is not merely one of poor methodology, but also of 
representational bias — the global evidence base for diabetes risk 
prediction is overwhelmingly derived from Asian populations, yet the 
applicability of these models to other regions remains unknown.
4.3 Implications for Research and Practice
These findings have several important implications. For researchers, the 
priority must shift from developing new models to rigorously validating 
existing ones in diverse populations. External validation studies should be 
planned and reported according to TRIPOD guidelines [7], with particular 
attention to calibration, not just discrimination [13].
For clinicians and guideline developers, caution is warranted when 
applying any T2DM prediction model to populations different from its 
development cohort. The poor performance in prediabetic populations 
suggests, albeit preliminarily, that existing models should not be used to 
guide decisions about diabetes prevention interventions in this group 
without local validation.
For policymakers in under-represented regions, these findings underscore 
the urgent need for local model development or rigorous external 
validation. Brazil, for example, contributes only 2.1% of published models 
despite having the world's largest public primary care system [14].
4.4 Strengths and Limitations
Strengths of this review include a comprehensive search of seven 
databases without language restrictions, rigorous PROBAST assessment 
by two independent reviewers (eTable 2), and a meta-analysis of 
discrimination stratified by geographic origin with detailed subgroup 
analyses (eTable 4 and eTable 7).
Limitations include the following: (1) We could not meta-analyze 
calibration due to inconsistent reporting; (2) The inclusion of multiple 
models from the same study could lead to non‑independence; we 
addressed this by using a primary model per study in the main analysis, 
but some uncertainty remains; (3) Mixing development AUCs (which are 
optimistically biased) with internal and external validation AUCs may have 
inflated the pooled estimates; (4) The extremely high heterogeneity (I² > 
80%) limits the interpretability of pooled AUCs as precise estimates; (5) 
Potential residual confounding in cross-regional AUC comparisons due to 
differences in study design; (6) The lack of individual participant data to

5. Conclusions
This systematic review of 97 T2DM prediction models reveals striking 
geographic inequity, poor methodological quality, and limited external 
validation. Over 70% of models were developed in Asian populations, with 
minimal representation from regions bearing the highest future diabetes 
burden. More than 90% of models are at high risk of bias, and fewer than 
one-quarter have been externally validated. The substantial variation in 
performance by region (eTable 7) and the poor performance in prediabetic 
populations critically limit the global clinical utility of current models, 
although high heterogeneity tempers the precision of these estimates. 
These findings call for an urgent reorientation of diabetes prediction 
research toward rigorous external validation in diverse populations, 
adherence to PROBAST and TRIPOD guidelines, and de novo model 
development in under-represented regions. Without such efforts, the 
promise of personalized diabetes prevention will remain unrealized for the 
majority of the world's population.
