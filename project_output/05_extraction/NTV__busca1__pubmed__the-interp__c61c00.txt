Abstract

Background: Multiple studies have consistently reported elevated 
oxidative stress and an impaired niacin skin flush response in 
patients with schizophrenia. This study aimed to compare 
differences in oxidative stress levels and the niacin skin flush 
response between patients stratified by the intensity of their 
response and healthy controls, and to analyze the correlations 
among oxidative stress markers, the niacin response, and clinical 
symptoms within the different response subgroups.
Methods: A cross-sectional case-control design was employed. 
Fifty patients with chronic schizophrenia and 50 demographically 
matched healthy controls were enrolled. Plasma levels of 
malondialdehyde (MDA), total nitric oxide synthase (TNOS), and 
inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) were measured in all 
participants, and the niacin skin flush response was assessed. 
Symptom severity in patients was rated using the Positive and 
Negative Syndrome Scale (PANSS). Based on the intensity of the 
niacin response, patients were further divided into high-response 
and low-response subgroups. Regression analysis was used to 
explore the associations among oxidative stress indicators, the 
niacin response, and clinical symptoms.

Results: MDA levels were significantly higher in the patient group 
than in the control group, whereas both TNOS and iNOS levels 
were significantly lower. The pattern of associations among 
oxidative stress markers, the niacin skin flush response, and 
clinical symptoms differed across response subgroups. These 
relationships may be influenced by factors such as sex, body mass 
index (BMI), age, and the dosage of antipsychotic medication.
Conclusion: Patients with chronic schizophrenia exhibit 
abnormalities in oxidative stress and show individual variability in 
the niacin skin flush response. The intensity of the niacin response 
may be associated with factors such as sex, BMI, age, and 
medication, leading to significant differences in the relationships 
among variables across subgroups in regression analysis. The 
results suggest that oxidative stress may be involved in the 
pathophysiological processes of schizophrenia, and that niacin 
sensitivity may reflect the level of oxidative stress in the body and 
correlate with different clinical symptomatic profiles.

Introduction
Schizophrenia is one of the most common psychiatric disorders, 
with a global prevalence of approximately 1%. It typically manifests 
in late adolescence or early adulthood, imposing substantial 
economic and psychological burdens on patients' families and 
society. The disorder predominantly follows a chronic course with a 
high relapse rate.
The diagnosis of schizophrenia is currently based on the ICD-10 or 
DSM-5 criteria, with core symptoms encompassing positive 
symptoms (e.g., hallucinations, delusions), negative symptoms (e.g., 
blunted affect, avolition), and cognitive impairment. Its exact 
etiology remains incompletely understood, and it is widely 
regarded as a neurodevelopmental disorder associated with genetic, 
psychological, and environmental factors. The prevailing 
hypothesis involves dysfunction in neurotransmitter systems such 
as dopamine and serotonin, upon which current pharmacological 
treatments are primarily developed. However, a proportion of 
patients show poor or even no response to antipsychotic medication, 
indicating treatment resistance. It is estimated that only about 30% 
of patients achieve complete symptomatic remission after 
treatment, primarily in positive symptoms, while negative

symptoms and cognitive impairment are considered the most 
significant predictors of psychosocial functioning [1, 2] .
Recent research suggests that patients with schizophrenia exhibit 
an overactive oxidative stress response, indicating its potential 
involvement in the pathogenesis of the disorder [3] . Imre and 
Kirkas [4] noted in their study that previous evidence has already 
indicated the presence of immunonutritional impairment in bipolar 
disorder. Combined with their own findings in schizophrenia, 
inflammation and oxidative stress may play shared 
pathophysiological roles across psychiatric disorders. Therefore, 
investigating such markers not only contributes to understanding 
the pathological mechanisms of schizophrenia but also offers new 
perspectives on the common mechanisms underlying multiple 
mental disorders.  Although no validated biomarkers are currently 
available for clinical stratification or treatment outcome prediction, 
related investigations are actively ongoing.
Measuring the plasma levels of malondialdehyde (MDA), total nitric 
oxide synthase (TNOS), and inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) 
in patients aids in assessing the oxidant-antioxidant imbalance and 
inflammatory status. As a primary end product of lipid peroxidation, 
the concentration of MDA directly reflects the level of oxidative 
stress. Studies have confirmed that MDA levels are significantly

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
abnormal in patients with schizophrenia across different illness 
stages and subtypes [5-8]. Nitric oxide synthase (NOS) catalyzes 
the production of nitric oxide (NO). Among its isoforms, the 
inducible form (iNOS) is upregulated under pathological conditions 
such as acute stress or chronic inflammation, serving as a 
biomarker for inflammation and tissue damage. Research indicates 
that NOS levels are abnormal in both the peripheral blood and 
brain tissues of patients with schizophrenia [9-13].
The niacin skin flush response is triggered by the activation of the 
cutaneous GPR-109A receptor by niacin, leading to the production 
of prostaglandin E₂ (PGE 2) via the phospholipase A₂￿PLA 2 ￿–
arachidonic acid￿AA￿–cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) pathway, which 
induces vasodilation [14]. An attenuated response has been 
repeatedly demonstrated in patients with schizophrenia [15-18], 
possibly indicating abnormalities in the signaling pathway, 
functional deficits of key enzymes, or reduced local vascular 
reactivity [19-21]. Methods for assessing this response include the 
oral niacin challenge [22] , visual rating scales [23], and laser 
Doppler flowmetry [24]. The present study employed a computer-
based quantitative scoring method to minimize subjective bias. 
Reduced niacin sensitivity is associated with greater functional 
impairment, abnormal protein expression, and inflammatory

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
dysregulation in patients [25, 26]. Furthermore, arachidonic acid 
levels in erythrocyte membranes are generally lower in patients 
than in healthy controls [27, 28]. Consequently, an attenuated 
niacin response is considered a potential endophenotype that may 
aid in differential diagnosis [24, 29].
Based on this background, the present study employed a case-
control design. It aimed to compare the differences in oxidative 
stress markers and the niacin skin flush response between chronic 
schizophrenia patients with distinct niacin response profiles and 
healthy controls, and to further explore the correlations among 
MDA, TNOS, iNOS, the niacin response, and clinical symptoms. 
This work was conducted to provide preliminary evidence for 
elucidating their underlying relationships.
Subjects and methods
Study Participants
The inclusion criteria for the schizophrenia group (SZ group) were 
as follows: (a) meeting the diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia 
according to the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental 
Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5); (b) age between 18 and 65 years;

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
and (c) hospitalization for ≥1 year, a total illness duration of ≥2 
years, and currently being in a clinically stable phase.
The healthy control group (HC group) comprised 50 individuals 
recruited via community advertisements in Suzhou, who were 
matched to the patient group in terms of age, sex, and body mass 
index (BMI). Their inclusion criteria were: (a) no personal history of 
psychiatric disorders; and (b) age between 18 and 65 years.
Exclusion criteria for both the schizophrenia group and the healthy 
control group included: (a) comorbid neurological disorders (e.g., 
epilepsy, brain tumors, or a history of severe brain injury); (b) use 
of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs or corticosteroids within 
the 14 days prior to the study; (c) pregnancy or lactation; and (d) a 
history of alcohol or substance abuse. All participants provided 
written informed consent after receiving a comprehensive 
explanation of the study. The study protocol was approved by the 
Ethics Committee of Suzhou Guangji Hospital (Approval No.: 2019-
051).
General information from patients, including sex, age, body mass 
index (BMI), and medication details, was collected using 
standardized questionnaires. Antipsychotic dosages were uniformly 
converted to chlorpromazine equivalent doses for assessment. The

severity of clinical symptoms was evaluated by two trained 
psychiatrists using the Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale 
(PANSS). The inter-rater reliability for PANSS scores, expressed as 
the correlation coefficient (r), exceeded 0.8.
Blood Sample Collection and Biochemical Assays
Blood samples were collected from the antecubital vein from all 
participants after an overnight fast (10–12 hours). Following 
anticoagulation treatment, the samples were centrifuged at 3000 
r/min for 15 minutes to separate the plasma. The supernatant 
plasma was aspirated using a pipette, aliquoted into 500 μL 
microcentrifuge tubes, and stored at –80 °C until batch analysis.
Plasma levels of MDA (catalog no. A003-1), TNOS and iNOS 
(catalog no. A014-2) were measured with the assistance of the 
Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute. MDA was quantified 
using the thiobarbituric acid (TBA) method, 
while TNOS and iNOS were determined by colorimetric assays. 
Results are expressed as nmol/L for MDA and U/mL 
for TNOS and iNOS.
Measurement of TNOS and iNOS activities
Total nitric oxide synthase (TNOS) and inducible NOS (iNOS) 
activities in plasma were determined using a commercial

Niacin Skin Flush Test
In the present study, the instrument used to evaluate the cutaneous 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
flushing response to niacin was provided by Shanghai Tianyin 
Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (model number: TY-Lab-01). This device 
consists of three main components: a six-well standard patch, an 
automated imaging system, and intelligent image recognition 
software (patent number: ZL 20211070775.0). Six different 
concentrations of nicotinic acid methyl ester solution were 
prepared as threefold serial dilutions, starting from 60 mmol/L. 
Specifically, the first concentration was 60 mmol/L, followed by 20 
mmol/L, 6.67 mmol/L, 2.22 mmol/L, 0.74 mmol/L, and finally 0.25 
mmol/L. Equal volumes of each concentration were applied to the 
corresponding wells of the patch. The application sites on the 
subject's right forearm were arranged linearly: the most distal site 
was designated as the first point, with the second, third, fourth, 
fifth, and sixth points placed progressively more proximal. The 
patch was placed in direct contact with the subject's right forearm 
for one minute and then removed. Immediately after removal, the 
forearm was positioned and secured within the testing apparatus. 
An automated imaging system captured photographs of the right 
forearm at ten‑second intervals over a ten‑minute period, yielding a 
total of 60 images per subject. The intelligent image recognition 
software was then used to identify the skin areas that had been in 
contact with the nicotinic acid methyl ester solution in each

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
photograph. The image taken at the 10‑second time point was 
algorithmically defined as the baseline reference representing the 
skin's initial state; consequently, the remaining 59 images were 
included in the final analysis. For each subject, this process 
generated 354 erythema area values (59 time points × 6 
concentrations). The software automatically identified and 
calculated the erythema area at each captured time point and 
subsequently summed the total area across different time points 
[30].
For some participants, the flushing response at concentration 
levels 4, 5, and 6 was so weak that the computer software could not 
reliably delineate the erythema area at these sites. Consequently, 
these measurements were recorded as missing values. To preserve 
the authenticity of the data and the accuracy of the study findings, 
the total area values corresponding to these three concentration 
points were omitted from the quantitative analysis, as presented in

Statistical Analysis
Data Description and Normality Testing
Continuous variables were first assessed for normality using the 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Shapiro–Wilk test. Data conforming to a normal distribution are 
presented as mean ± standard deviation (mean ± SD), while non-
normally distributed variables are reported as median 
(interquartile range) [M (IQR)]. Categorical variables are 
summarized as frequency (percentage).
Methods for Between-Group Comparisons
Based on the distribution characteristics of the variables and the 
number of groups, a tiered testing strategy was adopted:
￿Two-group comparisons: Normally distributed variables 
were analyzed using the independent samples  t-test; non-
normally distributed variables were compared using the 
Mann–Whitney U test.
￿Overall comparisons across three or more groups: 
Normally distributed variables were examined using one-way 
analysis of variance (one-way ANOVA); non-normally 
distributed variables were analyzed with the  Kruskal–
Wallis H test.
￿Post hoc pairwise comparisons: If the overall ANOVA 
result was significant ( P < 0.05), Tukey's HSD  test was 
applied for post hoc analysis. If the overall Kruskal–Wallis 
test was significant, the Dunn test was used for pairwise

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
comparisons, with P-values adjusted using the Bonferroni 
correction to control for multiple comparisons.
￿Categorical variable comparisons: The χ²  test was used.
Correlation Analysis and Regression Modeling
Based on the results of normality testing, Pearson correlation 
analysis was used for normally distributed variables, and 
Spearman's rank correlation analysis was used for non-normally 
distributed variables to preliminarily examine bivariate 
associations.
To further quantify the relationships between variables while 
controlling for potential confounding factors, regression models 
were subsequently constructed. When building the regression 
models, sex, BMI, age, and antipsychotic dosage (converted to 
chlorpromazine equivalents) were included as 
covariates regardless of the significance of the bivariate 
correlations, in order to account for their potential influence. The 
linearity assumption of the relationships was first assessed. If the 
relationship was approximately linear and the residuals satisfied 
the model assumptions, least-squares linear regression was applied.
Effect Sizes and Significance Level
For results reaching statistical significance, corresponding effect 
sizes are reported. All tests were two-tailed, with the significance

Comparison of Demographic and General Clinical 
Characteristics
Based on the median total niacin flush area at the first 
concentration point at 10 minutes (denoted as 10min-1, and so 
forth for other time points) in the patient group, patients were 
divided into a low-response group and a high-response group (each 
N = 25) for subsequent analysis.
As shown in Table 1, this study compared differences across

Between the two patient subgroups, no significant differences were 
observed in the severity of clinical symptoms (scores on various 
scales) or in antipsychotic dosage. Among the high‑response group, 
8 patients received atypical antipsychotic monotherapy, and 17 
patients received combination therapy with different antipsychotics. 
In the low‑response group, 9 patients received atypical 
antipsychotic monotherapy, and 16 patients received combination 
therapy with different antipsychotics. In each group, only one

Regression Analysis
Building on the aforementioned correlation analyses, we performed 
multiple linear regression to construct the core analytical models. 
Although sex, age, BMI, and chlorpromazine-equivalent 
antipsychotic dosage showed no significant differences between

Discussion
This study demonstrates that patients with chronic schizophrenia 
exhibit two distinct pathological manifestations: elevated levels of 
the oxidative stress product MDA, and decreased levels of TNOS 
and iNOS. Furthermore, significant differences were observed in 
the area of the niacin skin flush response among different patient 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
subgroups. These findings show some inconsistencies with previous 
studies concerning oxidative stress and the niacin skin response 
[33, 35, 36].
The core finding of this study is that, among chronic schizophrenia 
patients with different types of niacin response, there exist 
correlations of varying strength and direction between nitric oxide 
synthase (TNOS/iNOS), the area of the niacin skin flush response, 
and the severity of clinical symptoms. Additionally, this study did 
not find a direct association between the level of the oxidative 
stress marker MDA and either clinical symptoms or the niacin skin 
response. This differs from some prior research, suggesting that in 
this chronic patient population, MDA levels may represent a 
relatively independent pathological dimension.
The present study found that peripheral plasma MDA levels were 
significantly higher in patients with chronic schizophrenia than in 
healthy controls. This result is consistent with the conclusions of 
the majority of previous studies, which have reported elevated 
MDA in first-episode, drug-naïve patients [5], chronically medicated 
patients [33, 37], and those with treatment resistance [6]. However, 
some studies focusing on first-episode, drug-naïve and chronic 
patients have reported contradictory findings [38, 39]. Research by 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Yang et al. [40] suggests that MDA levels in patients may decrease 
following antipsychotic treatment.
The changes in TNOS and iNOS levels observed in this study are 
consistent with some existing reports, such as those by Malik and 
Yang et al. [13, 41], but inconsistencies also exist. For example, 
Taneli et al. [42] found that serum NO levels in schizophrenia 
patients remained higher than those in healthy controls and 
showed no significant change even after 6 weeks of antipsychotic 
treatment. Furthermore, studies on postmortem brain tissue from 
patients indicated significantly higher neuronal NOS expression in 
the frontal cortex compared to controls [43]. Research suggests 
that NO at low to moderate concentrations may exert 
neuroprotective effects, whereas excessively high concentrations 
can be neurotoxic [10]. Excessive NO can induce oxidative stress, 
impair neuronal function, and lead to cell death, indicating that 
overproduction of NO may be related to the pathological 
mechanisms of schizophrenia. Animal model studies provide further 
support for this view: nNOS knockout mice exhibit upregulated 
dopamine D1 receptor signaling, accompanied by abnormal social 
behavior, hyperactivity, and impaired spatial memory [44]; 
concurrently, NOS inhibitors can block the behavioral and 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
biochemical alterations induced by PCP in rats [45].
The inconsistencies among the aforementioned study results may 
be related to differences in patients' medication status, illness 
duration, and other factors. Previous research indicates that 
antipsychotic medications themselves can influence inflammatory 
and oxidative stress systems. Different types and doses of drugs 
may have bidirectional regulatory effects on iNOS expression, with 
some potentially inducing inflammation in the short term and 
others suppressing it. For example, Nonaka-Hashida et al. [46] 
found that antipsychotics could reduce elevated methylated 
arginine levels and increase L-arginine in patients with 
schizophrenia, thereby improving NO bioavailability. Animal 
experiments also suggest that cholinergic receptor antibodies 
derived from patients can interact with brain M1 receptors, 
triggering the production of NO, PGE2, and MMP-3, and inducing 
the expression of COX-1 and iNOS mRNA in the prefrontal cortex—
effects that can be inhibited by haloperidol [47]. Furthermore, 
olanzapine has been shown to attenuate astrocyte proliferation in 
the rat prefrontal cortex and downregulate the expression of 
related pro-inflammatory, oxidative stress, and apoptotic molecules 
[48]; risperidone can reduce iNOS expression, suggesting it 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
possesses certain anti-inflammatory potential [49] .
In summary, the inconsistencies in MDA and NOS findings may 
reflect dynamic changes in oxidative stress and inflammation levels 
in patients with schizophrenia, such as elevation during acute 
phases and a gradual decrease in chronic stages. In this study of 
chronic schizophrenia patients, correlation analyses revealed no 
significant associations between MDA levels and clinical symptoms 
or the niacin skin flush area. The lack of significant findings may be 
related to the relatively small sample size, although this possibility 
cannot be ruled out. Considering the high degree of heterogeneity 
inherent to schizophrenia itself, the results of this study also 
indirectly highlight the complexity of the disorder and the potential 
methodological differences across studies. Chronic patients 
predominantly present with negative symptoms, and their 
neuroimmunopathological mechanisms may differ from those in the 
acute phase or in patients with shorter illness durations. This 
aspect warrants further in-depth investigation in the future.
The present study indicates significant associations between TNOS 
and iNOS levels and different dimensions of clinical symptoms. 
Yang et al. [13] reported a positive correlation between plasma NO 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
levels and PANSS positive symptom scores; McNeill [50] proposed 
that elevated peripheral NO metabolite concentrations may be 
associated with enhanced inflammatory activity in psychopathology. 
Animal experiments further suggest a link between dopamine 
dysfunction-related cognitive deficits and NO [51], and that NOS 
inhibitors can attenuate schizophrenia-like effects induced by 
NMDA receptor hypofunction. When NOS enzyme activity becomes 
excessively elevated, oxidative stress processes intensify, 
potentially leading to aggravated cellular damage; conversely, 
effective management of psychiatric symptoms may help mitigate 
such stress responses. We hypothesize that the observed decrease 
in NOS levels in patients with chronic schizophrenia may reflect a 
degree of regulation in their neuroinflammatory state. Imre et al. 
[52] reported elevated inflammatory markers in schizophrenia but 
found no association with cognition. In contrast, our MDA and NOS 
indices reflected oxidative stress and abnormal inflammation–
endothelium interactions. Therefore, a multidimensional biomarker 
approach integrating inflammation, oxidative damage, and vascular 
function offers greater value for elucidating pathophysiological 
mechanisms and for risk stratification.
Although the different niacin skin flush response groups showed no 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
significant differences across clinical symptom dimensions, this 
study still identified correlations between response intensity and 
clinical symptoms. Notably, after controlling for covariates, 
regression analysis revealed a differential pattern. In the low-
response group, most bivariate correlations became non-significant 
after multivariate adjustment. In the high-response group, however, 
some initially uncorrelated variables emerged as significant 
predictors following adjustment. This important finding suggests 
that the relationships among variables in different response groups 
may be mediated or confounded by the control variables in the 
model (sex, age, BMI, and chlorpromazine-equivalent antipsychotic 
dosage). It also indicates that differences in niacin sensitivity may 
partially reflect variations in the severity of clinical symptoms and 
the level of systemic inflammation.
Metabolomic and proteomic studies suggest that patients with 
schizophrenia exhibit impairments in energy metabolism and lipid 
biosynthesis [53]. Abnormalities in phospholipid metabolism, in 
particular, can be manifested and detected through an attenuated 
niacin skin flush response. As summarized by Messamore, the 
abnormal niacin response in schizophrenia is independent of 
psychotropic medication use and may indicate a deficiency in 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
essential fatty acids [54]. Research by Chen et al. further notes that 
the attenuation of the niacin skin flush response is not associated 
with illness severity, violent behavior, or antipsychotic dosage, 
suggesting its potential as a trait marker for schizophrenia and 
supporting its utility as an objective diagnostic biomarker for 
distinguishing patients from healthy controls [55]. A study by 
Tavares et al. demonstrated elevated phospholipase A2 (PLA2) 
activity in schizophrenia patients, with higher activity observed in 
niacin non-responders than in responders; after 8 weeks of 
antipsychotic treatment, this activity decreased [31]. This may help 
explain why a subset of patients exhibits a niacin response similar 
to that of healthy controls.
Correlational studies have also shown that in individuals at ultra-
high risk and in first-episode psychosis patients, niacin sensitivity is 
inversely correlated with positive symptom scores [56]. Zhang et al. 
reported that an attenuated niacin response in first-episode 
psychosis patients is significantly associated with negative 
symptoms, indicating that the absence of a niacin response could 
serve as a potential biomarker for a subtype characterized 
predominantly by negative symptoms and poor treatment response 
[57]. Notably, it has recently been reported that several 
cardiovascular risk markers exhibit significant differences among 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
different clinical subgroups of patients with schizophrenia, 
suggesting that biomarker-based stratification strategies are 
emerging as a new direction in psychiatric research [58]. In 
conjunction with our results, niacin response—an endophenotypic 
indicator reflecting oxidative stress–inflammation pathways—may 
in the future be combined with other biomarkers (e.g., metabolic 
and inflammatory markers) to facilitate patient risk stratification or 
the selection of individualized treatment regimens.
Currently, the variety of methods used to assess the niacin skin 
flush response hampers direct comparability across studies, and 
differences in outcome criteria may lead to variations in reported 
significance. There is a need to establish more standardized 
measurement protocols in the future to overcome the current 
limitations in comparability. The findings of this study reinforce the 
view that multiple variables often interact with sex, age, BMI, and 
antipsychotic medication. This highlights the importance of 
comprehensively considering these factors in future research and 
offers insights for developing more targeted intervention strategies.
Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, as a cross-sectional study, 
its design cannot capture the dynamic evolution of the relevant 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
indicators, and the conclusions reflect characteristics only at a 
specific time point. Future research should include patients at 
different illness stages and with varied clinical profiles to conduct 
longitudinal follow-up for more in-depth exploration. Second, this 
study did not perform subgroup analyses or statistical control 
based on the specific types of antipsychotic medications. Given that 
different medications may exert differential effects on the NOS 
system, oxidative stress, and the niacin skin flush response, future 
studies should refine their design to control for additional potential 
confounding factors. These refinements may include, but are not 
limited to, stratifying by the type of antipsychotic used, 
incorporating a longitudinal time axis, improving within-subject 
comparisons, and dynamically tracking and exploring the 
correlations among the various indicators. Third, the sample sizes 
of some subgroups in this study were relatively small (e.g., n=25), 
which may lead to insufficient power in the regression analyses, 
causing some true associations to fail to reach statistical 
significance, and may also render the observed significant 
associations unstable. Therefore, these regression results should be 
regarded as exploratory and preliminary, and need to be validated 
in larger samples in future research. Fourth, this study does not 
offer a novel pathophysiological or therapeutic insight into 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
oxidative stress or the niacin flush response in schizophrenia. 
Limitations include the lack of large‑scale Chinese data, the 
inclusion of only stable‑phase patients, and unstandardized 
assessment methods. Despite computer‑based quantification to 
enhance objectivity, this study remains a confirmatory, rather than 
a conceptual, contribution to existing evidence in Chinese 
stable‑phase patients.
Conclusion
In summary, this preliminary study suggests that TNOS and iNOS 
levels in patients with schizophrenia may be potentially associated 
with the severity of their clinical symptoms and the sensitivity of 
the niacin skin response. These findings further support the notion 
that NOS may be involved in the abnormal process of the niacin 
skin flush response in schizophrenia, while also indicating that 
oxidative stress and inflammatory responses may be related to the 
pathophysiological mechanisms of the disorder. The sensitivity of 
the niacin response may reflect the body's oxidative stress status 
and show connections with different clinical symptomatic profiles. 
From a clinical translation perspective, the aforementioned 
biomarkers could be used for patient risk stratification (e.g., 
identifying subgroups with higher oxidative stress levels) and 
provide a basis for selecting targets for adjunctive therapies 
directed at oxidative stress, such as antioxidants and anti-
inflammatory agents. We hope this study can provide partial 
theoretical support for the development of novel adjuvant 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPTARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
therapeutic strategies targeting oxidative stress and inflammation, 
and offer insights for improving the clinical prognosis of 
schizophrenia.
