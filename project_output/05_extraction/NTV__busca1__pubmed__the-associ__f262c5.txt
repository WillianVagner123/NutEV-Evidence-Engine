ABSTRACT
Background 
Community Health Centres (CHCs) are community-governed organizations often 
located in communities facing significant challenges with social determinants of 
health. To date, no review has summarized the evidence on the effects of team-
based models of care on patient outcomes. The objective of this systematic 
review was to synthesize the evidence on the characteristics of interprofessional 
teams practicing in CHCs and their associations with patient outcomes.
Methods
A systematic review was conducted following the guidelines of the Cochrane 
Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions. In October 2024, an 
electronic search was performed in five databases and the grey literature using a 
combination of descriptors and keywords. No restrictions were applied regarding 
language or year of publication. Screening and data extraction were 
independently conducted by two reviewers. The included papers focused on 
interprofessional teams composed of at least three different types of providers 
(e.g., physician, nurse, and nutritionist) practicing in CHCs and addressing at 
least one patient outcome (e.g., satisfaction, diabetes management). The risk of 
bias was assessed by two independent reviewers using the Joanna Briggs 
Institute checklist.
Results 
From 6,309 identified papers, 36 papers were included. Most papers focused on 
team composition (n=18) and staffing patterns (n=8). Adjustments to staffing

patterns—such as increasing full-time equivalents, expanding team size, and 
diversifying skill mixes—were associated with improved patient satisfaction and 
increased number of visits. Adding a chiropractor reduced opioid prescriptions 
and patient pain, while including a pharmacist improved hepatitis management 
and patient satisfaction. Inconsistent findings were observed regarding the 
inclusion of nurse practitioners and physician assistants in interprofessional 
teams and their impact on the number of patients seen in CHCs. Specialized 
interprofessional teams addressing diabetes, pain management, and childhood 
obesity had better health outcomes and care management compared to standard 
care, highlighting the value of tailored interprofessional collaboration in achieving 
improved health outcomes for specific populations. 
Conclusions
Specific team compositions are associated with improved health outcomes for 
the populations served by CHCs. Future research is needed to deepen the 
understanding of the associations between team composition, the type of care 
provided, and patients' clinical and psychosocial needs.

BACKGROUND
Community Health Centres (CHCs) are community governed organizations 
typically located in areas where residents face significant challenges related to 
social determinants of health and limited access to healthcare. In 2021, Nagel et 
al. conducted a concept analysis and identified six key attributes that define 
CHCs: primary care, accessibility, preventive care, a defined population, health 
promotion, and comprehensive care (Nagel et al., 2021). Firstly, CHCs often 
serve as the primary point of entry for healthcare among vulnerable populations, 
including low-income communities, individuals with mental health or substance 
use issues, immigrants, refugees, and visible minorities. Secondly, the care 
philosophy of CHCs emphasizes culturally sensitive care, health promotion and 
prevention, adopting a holistic approach that takes into account both the clinical 
and psychosocial contexts of patients (Nagel et al., 2021). 
Internationally, CHCs are most commonly found in North America and 
China. In Canada and the U.S. (in the U.S., they are known as Federally 
Qualified Health Centers, or FQHCs), CHCs share similar governance, 
organizational structures, and models of care. They are typically governed by 
community boards with consumer representation and operate as non-profit 
organizations, funded primarily through government support (Alliance for 
Healthier Communities, 2020; British Columbia Association of Community Health 
Centres, 2023; Wright. 2017). CHCs provide a broad range of services, including 
medical, dental, psychosocial, and educational care, offering services either free 
of charge or at reduced cost for individuals without insurance or not registered

with a primary care physician. The defining features of CHCs, compared with 
other primary care settings, are their accessibility to uninsured or unregistered 
patients, community-based governance, and salary-based clinician 
compensation (Suleman et al., 2024).  In China, CHCs are the main providers of 
urban primary care, while township hospitals and village clinics serve rural areas. 
Chinese CHCs operate under three models, government-managed, hospital-
managed, or private, and patients are generally expected to pay for services 
regardless of the model (Li et al., 2015). 
To support these purposes, a team-based model of care (TBMC) has 
been introduced in CHCs since their conception. A TBMC is an evidence-based 
approach where at least three different types of healthcare professionals (HCPs) 
collaborate with patients and their families or caregivers to achieve shared 
treatment goals (Kiran et al., 2020; Kiran et al., 2022; Somé et al., 2020). In 
2017, Baik conducted a concept analysis to define and examine team-based 
care in clinical practice, identifying three core attributes: interprofessional 
collaboration, a patient-centered approach, and an integrated care process. 
Current literature agrees on the effectiveness of TBMC for patients, families, 
interprofessional teams, and healthcare organizations. Compared to traditional 
physician-focused care, TBMC has shown success in improving health 
outcomes, enhancing patient experience, reducing healthcare utilization, 
improving continuity of care, and lowering costs (Kiran et al., 2020; Kiran et al., 
2022; Somé et al., 2020).

Several systematic reviews have been published examining the 
effectiveness of TBMC in various primary healthcare settings (Doose et al., 2022; 
Hinneh et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023). For instance, a systematic review and meta-
analysis by Hinneh et al. (2024) found that TBMC for managing hypertension in 
Africa led to reductions in blood pressure. Furthermore, their study indicated that 
the inclusion of specific providers, such as community health workers and 
nurses, within interprofessional teams contributed to these improvements in 
blood pressure management (Hinneh et al., 2024). Other reviews have also 
explored various interprofessional team characteristics and their impact on 
patient, team, and organizational outcomes. For example, the umbrella review by 
Wei et al. (2021) suggested that team-level characteristics such as size and 
adequate staffing can enhance team collaboration, improve care quality, and 
positively affect patient outcomes (Wei et al., 2022). Most of these reviews 
encompass a broad range of primary care settings, such as clinics, family health 
teams, and home-visit programs, yet it remains unclear whether CHCs were 
included. Considering that CHCs have a distinct governance structure and serve 
a higher proportion of patients affected by social determinants of health (e.g., 
low-income individuals, newcomers) compared to other primary care settings 
(Lewis et al., 2016), this unique context warrants dedicated attention.
Although several literature and systematic reviews have been conducted 
on primary healthcare, less attention has been paid to synthesizing the literature 
specifically on TBMC in CHCs, particularly regarding how different team 
characteristics influence care quality and patient outcomes. Therefore, we argue

that a new systematic review is needed and this is supported by two key points. 
First, existing reviews, including for example, the systematic reviews published 
by Marten et al. (2021), Rodis et al. (2022) and Wang et al. (2015) conducted in 
CHC settings focus on a specific provider or type of care, such as pharmacist-led 
interventions or telemedicine (Martens et al., 2021; Rodis et al., 2022; Wang et 
al., 2015). However, prior studies have highlighted the value of interprofessional 
collaboration across a broader range of providers to improve the accessibility and 
quality of care for patients and families. Second, while some reviews (Choi et al., 
2020; Nakamura et al., 2019) have examined CHC performance and productivity, 
such as cost and visit volume, less attention has been given to patient- and 
family-centered outcomes, including patient-reported outcomes, clinical 
outcomes, and care experiences. To the best of our knowledge, there is no 
systematic review that assesses the direct benefits of TBMC for patients and 
families in CHCs.
To address these gaps, we conducted a systematic review aimed at 
building upon previous work and examining the effects of different characteristics 
of TBMC in CHCs for communities, patients, and families. Our review specifically 
focused on TBMC involving at least three different types of providers, consistent 
with the conceptualization of team-based care proposed by Baik (2017), captures 
a broad range of TBMC models and characteristics (e.g., team size, composition, 
dynamics, utilization of e-health, etc.), and assesses patient outcomes. The 
objectives of this systematic review were to: 1) examine the association between 
TBMC characteristics and models of care and patient outcomes in CHCs, 2)

discuss future research directions in this area.
METHODS
Design
A systematic review of the literature was conducted in accordance with the 
Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions guidelines (Higgins 
et al., 2019). The review protocol was registered in PROSPERO (Audet et al., 
2024). 
Search Strategy
In October 2024, a search was performed across five databases: 1) MEDLINE, 
2) CINHAL, 3) Embase, 4) Cochrane Library, and 5) PsycINFO, to identify papers 
examining TBMC characteristics and patient outcomes in CHCs. Multiple queries 
were executed in each database by combining two independent sets of keywords 
and descriptors: 1) CHCs and 2) TBMC (see Table 1). Additionally, a search of 
the grey literature was conducted in November 2024, following the Cochrane 
recommendations (Higgins et al., 2019), to retrieve pre-publication works, 
reports, and conference proceedings. The grey literature search included: 1) 
Opengrey, 2) ClinicalTrials.gov, 3) Google Scholar, 4) National Library of 
Medicine, and 5) the Virginia Henderson Database. No restrictions on language 
or publication date were applied. For each included paper, reference lists 
(backward search) and publications citing the included papers (forward search) 
were reviewed for potential additional relevant papers. An academic librarian was

consulted to help develop and execute the search strategy. The full search 
strategies are outlined in Appendix A.
Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
All published and unpublished quantitative papers addressing TBMC in CHCs 
were eligible for inclusion. Mixed-method papers were included as well, provided 
that the quantitative component was presented separately and could be 
independently extracted from the qualitative portion. Based on Baik’s (2017) 
concept analysis of team-based care, papers were included if they described 
teams involving interprofessional collaboration among three or more types of 
healthcare providers and provided direct patient care under a collaborative and 
integrated approach. All aspects of team characteristics and models of care, 
such as size, credentials and accreditations, provider types, team dynamics (e.g., 
relationships, workload), staffing patterns, integration of e-health (e.g., 
telemedicine, electronic health records), and skill mix, were considered (Baik, 
2017). Similarly, based on Liu et al. (2014) concept analysis on patient 
outcomes, all types of patient outcomes, including clinical and psychosocial 
outcomes, patient-reported outcome and experience measures (PREMS and 
PROMs), and healthcare resource utilization, were included (Liu et al., 2014). To 
be eligible, papers had to focus on one or more team characteristics and their 
association with one or more patient outcomes. CHCs were defined broadly to 
include any health center meeting the description provided by Nagel et al. (2021) 
and were required to meet the following three criteria: 1) be labeled as a CHC, 
community health clinic, federally qualified health center (FQHC), Centre local de

services communautaires (CLSC), or other related terms; 2) be based in primary 
healthcare settings; and 3) be publicly funded (Nagel et al., 2021). All types of 
patient populations were included, and there were no restrictions on geographical 
location, language, or publication date. Research protocols were included if they 
were linked to a subsequent result publication.
Exclusion criteria included qualitative papers, discussion papers, letters to 
the editor, and all types of reviews. Review articles were excluded to ensure 
inclusion of primary quantitative studies only and to avoid overlap with primary 
studies identified through the literature search. Conference proceedings that did 
not report study findings and research protocols without a corresponding results 
publication were excluded, as were studies conducted in primary care settings 
other than CHCs. Papers focusing on the practice of a single provider (e.g., 
physician-led care) or a dyadic team (e.g., physician-nurse) were excluded, as 
well as papers examining specific interventions or programs (e.g., addition of 
weekly meetings for staff or smoking cessation programs for patients) within 
CHCs. Papers involving the pilot testing or implementation of specific new 
interventions that were not related to teams’ day-to-day and routine 
characteristics, structures, models of care, or practices were excluded. Papers 
that measured organizational outcomes (e.g., cost) or interprofessional team 
outcomes (e.g., clinician communication) without assessing patient-level 
outcomes were also excluded.

Data Extraction
A standardized extraction form was created and pilot-tested by the research 
team before use. The following data were extracted from each included paper: 1) 
title, 2) first author, 3) year of publication, 4) location, 5) study design, 6) research 
objective/question, 7) CHC description, 8) population/sample size, 9) team 
characteristics, 10) team composition, 11) comparison, 12) data sources, 13) 
tools used, 14) data collection timeline, 15) outcome measures, 16) participant-
level covariates, 17) team-level covariates, 18) organizational-level covariates, 
19) additional qualitative data collection, and 20) key findings. Data from 
secondary analyses and papers related to the same research project were 
extracted alongside the primary study and color coded in the extraction chart. 
Extraction was performed by one reviewer (LAA) and reviewed for accuracy by a 
second reviewer (ICA).
Data Analysis
Following the Cochrane methodological guidance for descriptive analysis in 
systematic reviews, descriptive statistics were used to summarize the 
characteristics of the included papers (Davey et al., 2011). In line with the 
recommendations of Popay et al. (2006), a narrative synthesis of the available 
evidence was conducted, and a thematic analysis was used to group findings 
based on TBMC characteristics (e.g., provider types, staffing patterns, electronic 
health record utilization, etc.) (Popay et al., 2006). Themes were identified by the 
12
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
research team through an inductive process based on findings reported from the 
included papers. A p-value of 0.05 was set for identifying statistically significant 
associations (Davey et al., 2011). Meta-analysis was not attempted due to the 
methodological heterogeneity of the included papers (Higgins et al., 2003). 
Study Quality Appraisal
The Joanna Briggs Institute (JBI) Checklists were used to assess the 
methodological quality of the included papers (Barker et al., 2023; Munn et al., 
2023). These checklists were selected because they accommodate various 
quantitative study designs. The JBI checklists for cohort, cross-sectional, 
economic evaluation, quasi-experimental, and RCT studies were applied, with 
the appropriate checklist selected based on each study’s design. For mixed-
methods studies, the quantitative component was evaluated using the 
appropriate JBI checklist, as JBI does not provide a checklist specifically for 
mixed-methods designs. An overall score was assigned to each paper, 
calculated as the percentage of "yes" responses relative to the total possible 
points. The risk of bias and the assignment of each score were assessed by two 
independent reviewers (LAA, ICA). A calibration session was conducted with five 
full-text papers, yielding an inter-rater agreement of 92% (near-perfect 
agreement) (Belur et al., 2021; Park & Kim, 2015). Divergences were discussed 
between the reviewers to reach consensus.

Patient Outcomes

Summary of Findings
Through thematic analysis, five main groups of TBMC characteristics were 
identified: 1) type of providers, 2) staffing patterns and team dynamics, 3) 
specialized interprofessional teams, 4) accreditations, and 5) e-health. These 
categories are further elaborated in the following sections, with Table 3 
summarizing all categories and outcomes. Patient outcome themes are 
presented under each of those five categories. Findings with statistical 
significance are presented along with their corresponding p-values. Results that 
were not subject to statistical validation are presented as reported.
Type of providers
A total of 18 papers examined the addition of specific types of providers within 
interprofessional teams and their association with a wide range of patient 
outcomes. Seven papers examined the inclusion of a chiropractor in 
interprofessional teams. Five papers were generated from the same research 
study, resulting in chiropractors being studied in three distinct settings. Garner et 
al. (2007) and Prater et al. (2020) identified significant associations between the 
addition of a chiropractor and reductions in pain and disability (p>0.01), as well 
as improvements in participants' overall health (p<0.05). Emary et al. (2017,

Staffing patterns and team dynamics
A total of six papers examined staffing patterns. Staffing patterns were defined by 
the mix and/or number of full-time equivalent (FTE) employees across various 
categories of providers. The most frequently reported provider categories in the 
included papers were primary care providers, advanced practice nurses, allied 
health professionals, and behavioral and mental health providers. Most of the 
papers identified that an increase in FTE employees was associated with a 
higher number of visits (Han & Ku, 2019; Ku et al., 2015; Luo et al., 2021; 
Thorsen et al., 2020). Luo et al. (2021) and Ku et al. (2015) suggested that 
higher proportions of FTE physicians within interprofessional teams were 
associated with increased productivity and a higher adjusted volume of patients 
seen (p<0.001). Thorsen et al. (2020) and Han et al. (2019) found that higher 
proportions of FTE behavioral health staff within the entire healthcare team were 
linked to better management of patients with depression, anxiety, and diabetes. 
Chuang et al. (2019) found that lower provider-to-patient ratios were associated 
with higher cancer screening rates (p<0.05). Miharti et al. (2021) suggested that 
interprofessional teams with a lower staff mix (e.g., variety of different types of 
healthcare providers within a team) outperformed those with a higher staff mix, 
being associated with increased care delivery to mothers and infants (e.g., 
vaccination rates, contraception use) (p<0.05). One paper examined the 
relationship between staff workload, staff relationships, and the management of 
diabetes. Vargas Bustamante et al. (2017) found that a high workload alone was

Specialized interprofessional teams
A total of five papers explored specialized interprofessional teams (i.e., teams 
that are part of standard practice and fully integrated into daily care within the 
CHC) focused on social liaison service inclusion (Young, 1994), weight 
management for children with obesity (Anand et al., 2010), diabetes (Katz et al., 
1998), pain management (Prater et al., 2020), and hepatitis C (Sims et al., 2018). 
Young et al. (1994) found that participants followed by a specialized social liaison 
team reported feeling a greater sense of responsibility and engagement in their 
care (p<0.05). Anand et al. (2010) investigated a weight management clinic for 
children with obesity and found that, out of 402 participants, nearly 80% reported 
making changes to their lifestyle, physical activity, or diet, and about half showed 
a decrease in body mass index (BMI) after a 12-weeks follow-up. Katz et al. 
(1998) reported that, after intervention by a diabetes-specialized team, 66% of 
participants were managing their glucose at home (p<0.001). Additionally, most 
participants who had experienced hypoglycemic episodes at home saw 
resolution post-intervention. Among patients without hypoglycemia, mean HbA1C 
levels were significantly better controlled after the intervention compared to 
baseline measures (p=0.02). Prater et al. (2020) compared a specialized pain 
team to chiropractor care and found reductions in reported pain and disability in 
both groups. Participants receiving chiropractic care reported lower pain levels

Accreditations
Two papers in the US compared CHCs with and without the Patient-Centered 
Medical Home Model (PCMH) accreditation (Chuang et al., 2019; Park et al., 
2018) and one paper the National Health Service Corps (NHSC) (Han et al., 
2019) accreditation. The PCMH is an accreditation available for healthcare 
centers in the US that focuses on the delivery of team-based, patient-centered 
care, continuity of care, care collaboration and transitions, and quality 
improvement. To be certified, healthcare centers must meet all 40 core criteria 
(e.g., after-hours access to appointments, involving patients in treatment 
discussions) and at least 25 of the elective criteria. Park et al. (2018) found that 
in PCMH-CHCs, an increase of one FTE advanced practice staff (e.g., advanced 
practice nurse, NP, PA) was associated with an approximate incremental 
increase of 539 visits annually (p=0.03) compared to CHC without the PCMH 
accreditation. No other significant associations were identified for any other types 
of providers. Elsewhere, Chuang et al. (2019) did not find any significant 
association between PCMH accreditation and cancer screening rates. 
Han et al. (2019) compared CHC participating in the NHSC program with 
CHC not enrolled in program and examined whether NHSC accreditation was 
associated with higher number of visits. The NHSC serves as a hiring program

E-health
A total of six papers examined e-health including the utilization of EHR and 
telemedicine in CHCs. A total of four papers examined the practice of 
interprofessional teams via telemedicine. McCarthy et al. (2021) explored the 
implementation of a telepharmacy sub-team within an on-site interprofessional 
team. Shin et al. (2014), Pyne et al. (2015), and Fortney et al. (2013) examined 
the delivery of care by interprofessional teams via telemedicine, including both 
on-site and off-site providers. Compared to on-site care provided by 
interprofessional teams alone, all papers identified significant associations 
between the addition of telemedicine care and improvements in several 
outcomes: higher monthly mean number of patient seen (McCarthy et al., 2021), 
better control of HbA1C for patients with diabetes (Shin et al., 2014), a greater 
reduction in depression symptoms, an increase in depression-free days, and 
overall improved wellbeing for patients with depression (p<0.001; p<0.009) 
(Fortney et al., 2013; Pyne et al., 2015). 
Two papers examined the utilization of electronic health records (HER) by 
interprofessional teams. Frogner et al. (2017) categorized interprofessional

DISCUSSION
The aim of this systematic review was to examine the association between 
TBMC characteristics and patient outcomes in CHCs. A total of 36 papers were 
retrieved, focusing on five themes of interprofessional teams’ characteristics and 
six themes of patient outcomes. Most of the papers identified benefits for patients 
and families resulting from specific TBMC characteristics. 
The findings from this review emphasize the significance of provider types 
within interprofessional teams and their impact on care delivery and patient 
outcomes. Some papers indicate that physicians are the most productive 
providers, with the highest volume of visits per day and per year, underscoring 
their key role within interprofessional teams. In recent years, the literature has 
identified several factors that contribute to the high productivity and patient 
volume of primary care physicians, including their skills and experience, the 
types of patients they see, and the regulations in their workplace. For instance, 
appointment durations may vary depending on the patient's condition and needs. 
Physicians also often treat both recurring patients and individuals with low-
26
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
complexity needs, which leads to shorter appointments and higher visit volumes 
(Kajaria-Montag et al., 2024; Morgan et al., 2014). Additionally, factors such as 
time pressure (i.e., the need for physicians to see patients in less time) and 
compensation may further influence their high volume and productivity 
(Freedman et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021).
This review also highlights the value of a diverse range of providers and 
specialized interprofessional teams in delivering high-quality, targeted care to 
patients and families managing complex clinical conditions. Previous studies 
have emphasized the significance of aligning the scope of practice of specific 
providers with the unique needs of patients based on their conditions. Patient 
conditions often require specialized care, and each provider type brings specific 
skills, training, and expertise to address these needs (Khan et al., 2022). For 
instance, several studies have highlighted the benefits of chiropractic treatment 
for patients with pain, disability, and musculoskeletal disorders, which can be 
attributed to chiropractors' targeted approach to these issues (Trager et al., 
2024). Specialized interprofessional teams were associated with improved 
clinical indicators for patient with diabetes, hepatitis C and childhood obesity, as 
the approach, provided by a diverse set of providers, addresses the multifaceted 
needs of patients. These teams highlight the value of a variety of provider types 
having specialized services, each contributing complementary expertise to care 
for patients with complex conditions (Bouton et al., 2023).
Some of the retrieved papers focused on telemedicine and EHR utilization, 
highlighting the role of electronic platforms in improving care provided by 
27
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
interprofessional teams. The telehealth model of care expanded significantly 
during the COVID-19 pandemic and has remained widespread due to its 
convenience. Previous studies indicate that telehealth is particularly effective for 
appointments where in-person interactions are not necessary. It helps eliminate 
barriers such as travel and geographic distance, making healthcare 
appointments more accessible and convenient for patients. This increased 
accessibility allows for easier meetings between patients and clinicians, leading 
to stronger continuity of care, more follow-up appointments, and ultimately better 
management of patient conditions, with a reduction in negative outcomes and 
deterioration (Ma et al., 2022; Snoswell et al., 2022). EHRs have been in use by 
clinicians for decades, even before the pandemic. Research suggests that 
healthcare centers with EHR systems benefit from features like appointment 
reminders, follow-up coordination with specialists, improved communication 
between providers, and enhanced administrative support. These functions 
contribute to better clinician workflows, higher-quality care, more effective follow-
up, and improved continuity of care (Nguyen et al., 2022; Rotenstein et al., 
2023).
Finally, the association between accreditations and patient outcomes 
showed mixed results. Two out of three papers focusing on the PCMH and 
NHSC found no significant impact of these accreditations on patient outcomes. 
These findings emphasize the importance of bridging the gap between policies 
and actual practice in healthcare settings. Previous research has highlighted that 
for policies to drive meaningful change, they must have a direct impact on 
28

ARTICLE IN PRESS
bedside practice (Cantiello, 2022). To explain the lack of clear findings, other 
studies have suggested that certain policies and organizational structures, such 
as accreditation, are often reflected in other team characteristics like provider-to-
patient ratios and team size. As a result, the overarching accreditation itself may 
not be a strong enough variable to show a direct link to patient outcomes (Han et 
al., 2019; Park et al., 2018).
The findings presented here should be interpreted with caution for four 
important reasons. First, several papers presented descriptive data, comparative 
groups, or pre- and post- findings without testing whether these changes were 
statistically significant. The absence of statistical validation raises concerns about 
the robustness of the findings. Second, five of the retrieved papers were linked to 
the same overarching research project, which could also have led to an 
overestimation of the findings and conclusions drawn by the authors, as well as 
our summary of the results. Third, in teams where certain provider types (e.g., 
interpreter, community health worker) or categories (e.g., behavioral and mental 
health provider) might be represented by small FTE proportions, relatively small 
additions to FTE capacity may be enough to produce measurable effects on 
outcomes. Finally, limited information was reported in the included studies 
regarding the context of the CHCs, as well as the regulatory frameworks and 
scopes of practice of the healthcare providers within their teams. For instance, 
variations in provider roles and authority (e.g., NP, PAs) across urban versus 
rural CHCs and teaching versus non-teaching CHCs may influence clinical 
practice and, consequently, patient outcomes.

Future research should focus on four key directions. First, more empirical 
data is needed to examine the types of providers within interprofessional teams 
and their association with patient profiles, including clinical, mental health, 
socioeconomic, and equity-related factors. The findings from this review highlight 
the impact of provider types on patient outcomes, but most of the included 
papers have focused on the general population or patients with a narrow set of 
conditions (e.g., diabetes). Less attention has been given to subpopulations with 
shared characteristics, such as individuals with low income, multimorbidity, or 
psychiatric disorders. More research is needed to identify the most effective 
provider types for these subgroups, as well as how specific providers can 
address the unique needs of each subgroup. 
Second, while this review identified several papers that focused either on 
a specific type of provider or on a mix of providers, less attention has been paid 
to understanding the value of varied healthcare provider types, such as 
specialized professions, and which combinations of providers are most effective 
for managing patients based on their conditions. For instance, specialized 
interprofessional teams are designed to collaborate on treating patients with 
specific conditions, but models like TBMC involve a broader range of providers, 
and patients may see different types depending on their needs. It remains 
unclear which combinations of providers are most appropriate for patients with 
particular conditions.

Third, future research should examine outcomes related to healthcare 
resource utilization. Surprisingly, the papers included in this review did not 
examine outcomes like emergency room (ER) visits, hospital admissions, or 
primary care visits outside of CHCs. This may be due to the focus of the included 
studies on team characteristics at the individual level, assessing direct benefits 
for patients, clinicians, and CHCs. However, future research exploring healthcare 
resource utilization could provide deeper insights into how TBMC models affect 
access to care and whether the care provided within CHCs is sufficient. Previous 
research has suggested that metrics like ER visits and hospital admissions could 
serve as proxies for the quality and continuity of care delivered by 
interprofessional teams, if measured accurately (Audet et al., 2024; McDonald et 
al., 2024).
Finally, future research should explore a broader range of emotional and 
psychosocial indicators. While several studies have examined various clinical 
and health promotion outcomes, and some have assessed PROMs related to 
satisfaction and care experience, few have investigated the long-term emotional, 
mental, and psychosocial outcomes associated with TBMC in CHCs. For 
example, future studies should consider outcomes such as distress, perceived 
readiness and satisfaction in self-managing care, feelings of isolation and 
burden, and other psychosocial factors experienced by individuals living with 
chronic conditions or affected by social determinants of health and inequities. 
Investigating these outcomes would enhance our understanding of how TBMC 
influence the well-being of patients, caregivers, and families.

This review has several key strengths, as well as some limitations. First, to 
the best of our knowledge, this is the first systematic review to comprehensively 
examine a broad range of TBMC characteristics and patient outcomes 
specifically within CHC settings. In our work, we captured a variety of TBMC 
characteristics, including provider types, staffing, EHR and telemedicine usage, 
and health center accreditations. Additionally, we retrieved a wide array of 
outcomes, covering clinical indicators, lifestyle promotion, access to care, patient 
experience, and mental health indicators. Second, for this review, we conducted 
an extensive search across multiple sources (e.g., databases, grey literature, and 
secondary searches), with no restrictions on language, location, or publication 
year. Furthermore, we included a broad spectrum of quantitative designs and 
types of CHCs and similar organizations. This thorough search enabled us to 
gather studies from diverse geographical areas, spanning different time periods, 
and employing various research methodologies. However, some limitations 
should be acknowledged. Despite our extensive search strategy across several 
databases and grey literature, there is still a possibility that some relevant studies 
were missed. Additionally, the high heterogeneity of the results prevented us from 
conducting a formal meta-analysis. Finally, the aim of this review was to retrieve 
objective TMC characteristics; thus, we did not conduct an analysis related to 
team effectiveness or team functioning. 
CONCLUSION
CHCs cater to a wide range of populations with complex and multifaceted needs, 
underscoring the significance and relevance of TBMC in these settings. This

review emphasizes the importance of TBMC characteristics and their influence 
on patient experience and satisfaction, the management of both clinical and 
psychosocial conditions, and access to care. However, to accurately assess 
these impacts and strengthen the evidence base, studies must employ robust 
methodological designs and rigorous analyses.
