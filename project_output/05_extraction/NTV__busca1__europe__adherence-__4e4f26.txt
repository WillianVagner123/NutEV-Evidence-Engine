Introduction
Climate change and environmental pollution are com -
plex issues with many facets. Environmental education 
is the most effective approach to mitigating environ -
mental challenges, which can help raise environmen -
tally conscious individuals [ 1]. According to Roth (1992), 
an environmentally literate individual is someone who 
can understand the relationship between ecological and 
societal systems, believes in the integrity of human and 
nature, is conscious of the environmental implications 
of technological advancements and knows that he/she 
can learn about the environment throughout his/her life.

Abstract
Aim This study aimed to evaluate the relationship between environmental literacy, sustainable food literacy, and 
adherence to the Mediterranean diet (MD) in adults.
Method Data were collected via a web-based questionnaire created using Google Forms between April and August 
2024 in Erzurum, Turkey. A total of 394 adults participated through snowball sampling. The questionnaire included 
sections on demographic information, anthropometric measurements, the Environmental Literacy Scale for Adults 
(ELSA), the Sustainable Food Literacy Scale (SFLS), and the Mediterranean Diet Adherence Screener (MEDAS).
Results 38.6% of the participants were found to adhere to the Mediterranean Diet (MD). Married participants were 
more likely to have adhered to the MD than single ones (odds ratio [OR]:0.41, p = 0.006). Adherence to the MD was 
associated with higher education duration (OR = 1.05, p = 0.011), a higher SFLS total score (OR = 1.01, p = 0.025), and a 
higher ELSA score (OR = 1.04, p = 0.004).
Conclusion The MD is a sustainable dietary model that enhances health and is environmentally friendly. For this 
reason, it may be possible to direct society towards healthier and less environmentally damaging food choices 
through training to increase individuals’ sustainable literacy and environmental literacy levels.

Knowledge, attitudes, behaviours, and awareness are the 
four core components of environmental literacy [2].
Our planet is suffering from the rapid growth of the 
world’s population, increased food consumption, and 
changing dietary habits. This underscores the urgent 
need to promote food systems that are not only nutri -
tious but also sustainable. Accordingly, the world should 
adopt an adequate, balanced, nutritious, sustainable diet 
that enhances food security, promotes local or seasonal 
agricultural production, and exhibits a minimal environ -
mental footprint [ 3]. One of the most widely recognized 
dietary patterns that aligns with these principles is the 
Mediterranean dietary pattern, recognized for its numer -
ous health advantages and its low environmental impact 
[4]. The Mediterranean diet (MD) focuses on the intake 
of natural and domestic species, with seasonal, local 
goods and traditional cooking methods significantly con -
tributing to biodiversity preservation and environmental 
sustainability [5].
The Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO) stated 
that to achieve sustainable nutrition, consumer behaviour 
must change and strategies that positively impact human 
health and ecosystems should be promoted to facilitate 
the transition from existing food systems [ 6]. Similarly, 
the World Health Organization’s 2030 Sustainability 
goals include improving food security and enhancing 
nutrition, providing sustainable food production systems, 
protecting the environment, and taking measures against 
the climate crisis [7]. In this context, it is essential to cre -
ate food literacy initiatives that will provide people with 
the knowledge and skills they need to understand food 
systems, make informed dietary choices, and develop 
healthy eating behaviours. By equipping individuals with 
these competencies, food literacy can improve personal 
health and well-being and enhance the sustainability of 
agricultural frameworks and the safeguarding of ecologi -
cal systems [8].
Prospective and large sample studies have demon -
strated that sustainable eating practices are linked to 
less negative environmental effects [ 9– 11]. However, 
studies exploring the relationship between sustainable 
eating practices and food literacy remain limited. Cur -
rent data indicates that food and nutrition literacy can 
provide the knowledge necessary to understand the 
factors dietary habits and their environmental conse -
quences [ 12, 13]. Nevertheless, the literature review 
revealed that no research has examined the potential 
relationship between the degree of adherence to the 
MD and the level of environmental and food literacy. It 
is essential to understand this relationship since it may 
influence both public policy and academic studies. By 
investigating the connection between environmental 
literacy, sustainable food literacy, and adherence to the 
MD, this study aims to provide insights that can guide 
future educational programs, policies, and research 
initiatives to promote environmental sustainability 
and healthy eating. Thus, the purpose of this study is 
to assess the relationship between adult adherence to 
the MD, environmental literacy, and sustainable food 
literacy.
Materials and methods
Study design and participants
This descriptive and cross-sectional study recruited 
394 adults who consented to participate in Erzurum, 
Turkey. The data for this study were collected through 
a web-based questionnaire created using Google 
Forms. The online questionnaire was disseminated 
via various social media channels such as Twitter, 
Facebook, WhatsApp, and Instagram. Data collec -
tion was carried out between April and August 2024. 
A snowball sampling method was employed to recruit 
participants. Individuals who voluntarily agreed to 
participate by selecting the “I consent to participate 
in this study voluntarily” option at the beginning of 
the form and who completed the questionnaire in full 
were included in the final sample. Inclusion criteria 
for the study were: being between 18 and 65 years of 
age, not having any diagnosed chronic illnesses, psy -
chological or eating disorders, not following a special 
diet, and providing informed consent to participate. 
Participants were excluded if they were pregnant or 
breastfeeding, did not meet the age criteria, or were 
adhering to a special dietary pattern. A total of 457 
individuals initially accessed the online survey. How -
ever, based on the predefined exclusion criteria, 63 
participants were excluded from the study: 30 due to 
incomplete responses, 15 due to chronic illnesses, 8 
due to a history of psychological disorders, and 10 due 
to following a special diet. As a result, the final analy -
sis was conducted with a sample of 394 participants.
The research was conducted with “Ethics Committee 
Approval” from the Erzurum Technical University Eth -
ics Committee (Meeting Number: 03, Decision Number: 
17, 2024). The study was carried out following the princi-
ples outlined in the Helsinki Declaration. Informed con -
sent was obtained electronically at the beginning of the 
questionnaire.
Data collection
 Several measures were used to evaluate the relation -
ship between environmental literacy, sustainable food 
literacy, Mediterranean diet adherence, and variables 
such as age, gender, education, marital status, and BMI. 
The questionnaire form comprised a general informa -
tion form, anthropometric measurements, dietary habits, 
the Environmental Literacy Scale for Adults (ELSA), the

Sustainable Food Literacy Scale (SFLS), and the Mediter -

Socio-demographic characteristics and anthropometric 
measurements
The socio-demographic characteristics, including age 
(in years), gender (male/female), years of education, and 
marital status (single or married) were questioned.
The participants self-reported their height (cm) and 
body weight (kg). The body mass index (BMI) was calcu -
lated by dividing body weight by the square of height and 
assessed according to the World Health Organization’s 
BMI categorisation [14].
Environmental literacy scale for adults
The Environmental Literacy Scale for Adults (ELSA) was 
created by Atabek-Yiğit et al. (2014) to assess the environ-
mental literacy levels of adults [1]. There are 20 items and 
three subscales on the scale: Environmental conscious -
ness level (3, 10, 11, 12, 13, 15, 16, 17, 19), Environmental 
anxiety level (1, 2, 4, 5, 7, 8), and Environmental aware -
ness level (6, 9, 14, 18, 20). Responses to the questions are 
provided on a Likert scale, with options ranging from “1” 
Strongly Agree, “2” Agree, “3” Undecided, “4” Disagree, 
to “5” Strongly Disagree. Items 4 and 16 are scored in the 
opposite direction.
All items are scored between 1 and 5, and the scale 
has a lowest possible score of 20 and a maximum score 
of 100. High scores on the test indicated high environ -
mental literacy levels, whereas low scores indicated low 
environmental literacy levels in individuals. The scale’s 
Cronbach’s alpha was 0.881. The Cronbach’s alpha val -
ues for the Environmental Consciousness, Anxiety, and 
Awareness subscales were 0.807, 0.765, and 0.715 [1].
Sustainable food literacy scale
The Sustainable Food Literacy Scale was developed by 
Teng and Chih (2022) with an emphasis on environmen -
tal sustainability and is intended to be used to assess 
people’s tendencies to adopt sustainable diets [15]. Kubi-
lay and Yüksel (2023) researched the validity, reliability, 
and adaptation of the scale in Turkish [ 16]. The scale has 
26 items and a five-factor (Sustainable Food Knowledge 
1, Sustainable Food Knowledge 2, Cooking and Kitchen 
Skills, Attitudes and Intentions for Action, and Action 
Strategies). Response options for the questions are mea -
sured on a Likert scale, ranging from (“1” strongly dis -
agree, “2” disagree, “3” slightly disagree, “4” undecided, 
“5” slightly agree, “6” agree, and “7” strongly agree). There 
is no reverse item on the scale. The scores for each item 
range from 1 to 7. As the score increases, the level of sus -
tainable food literacy increases. Cronbach’s alpha value of 
the scale was determined as 0.941 [16].
Mediterranean diet adherence screener
Schröder et al. (2011) developed the Mediterranean 
Diet Adherence Scale (MEDAS) to measure adherence 
to the MD [ 17]. Pehlivanoglu et al. (2020) conducted 
the scale’s validity, reliability, and Turkish adapta -
tion [ 18]. There are 14 items on the scale. The scale 
is used to evaluate the type of oil used in meals, the 
daily intake of olive oil, portion sizes of fruits and veg -
etables, and the consumption of margarine, butter, and 
red meat. It also examines the weekly intake of wine, 
legumes, fish and seafood, nuts, cakes, and tomato 
sauce with olive oil, in addition to whether there is 
a preference for white meat over red meat. The final 
score is determined by taking 1 or 0 points for each 
question based on the amount of consumption. The 
range of MEDAS scores is 0 to 14, with 7 points indi -
cating adherence to the MD. The Cronbach’s alpha 
value for the scale was 0.829 [ 18].
Statistical analysis
SPSS version 23.0 software (IBM Corpn, Armonk, 
NY, USA) was used to analyze the data. Kolmogorov-
Smirnov tests, Skewness and Kurtosis, and visual anal -
ysis (histogram and probability graphs) were used to 
evaluate the data to see if they were normally distrib -
uted. While frequency (n) and percentage (%) were uti -
lized to represent categorical variables, the mean (X¯) 
and standard deviation (SD) were used for continuous 
variables. For comparisons between two independent 
groups, the Independent Samples t-test was used for 
normally distributed data, while the Mann–Whit -
ney U test was employed for non-normally distrib -
uted data. In cases involving more than two groups, 
the Kruskal–Wallis H test was used. Categorical vari -
ables were analyzed using the Chi-square test. Pear -
son correlation analysis was used for the relationship 
between variables. A binary logistic regression analy -
sis was conducted to evaluate the effect of factors on 
non-adherence vs. adherence to the MD and calcu -
late the odds ratio (OR) and 95% confidence interval 
(CI). Non-adherence (code-0) vs. adherence to the 
MD (code-1) was used as the dependent variable, age 
(years), education duration (years), SFLS total score, 
ELSA score, gender (men-reference group vs. women), 
marital status (Single vs. Married- reference group), 
and BMI classification (underweight-reference group, 
normal weight, overweight/obese) were used as inde -
pendent variables. The fit of the logistic regression 
model was evaluated with the Hosmer–Lemeshow 
test [ 19]. The threshold for statistical significance was 
determined at p < 0.05.
The G*Power (version 3.1.9.7, Universitat Düssel -
dorf, Düsseldorf, Germany) was utilized for post-hoc 
power analysis following the study, employing the point

Results
The general characteristics of the individuals are 
given in Table  1. Accordingly, 85.0% of the individu -
als were female, and 15.0% were male. The mean age 
was 23.4 ± 6.12 years, and the duration of education was 
14.4 ± 2.81 years, 67.3% were in the normal BMI range, 
and 22.8% were overweight/obese. The mean MEDAS 
score of the individuals was 6.1 ± 1.91, and 38.6% of them 
were found to adhere to the MD (≥ 7 scores). The Sus -
tainable Food Literacy Scale total score was 141.6 ± 19.62. 
The Environmental Literacy Scale score was 82.4 ± 8.35 
(Table 1).
The results showed that women had a higher ELSA 
score (82.9 ± 7.73) than men (79.5 ± 10.87) ( p = 0.025). 
The bachelor/master degree group had a higher MEDAS 
score ( p = 0.023) than the secondary/high school group. 
According to educational status and BMI classification, 
there was no significant difference in the ELSA scores 
(p > 0.05). However, a significant difference was observed 
in the MEDAS scores based on educational status: par -
ticipants with a Bachelor or Master degree had higher 
MEDAS scores compared to those with only secondary 
or high school education (p = 0.023). No significant differ-
ence in MEDAS scores was found across BMI classifica -
tions (p > 0.05) (Table 2).
Table 3 shows the distribution of the SFLS total score 
and its subcomponents by gender, education status, and 
BMI classification. The total SFLS score and subcom -
ponents were statistically higher in women than men 
(p < 0.05). However, no significant difference was found 
according to education status and BMI classification 
(p > 0.05).
The factors associated with the participant’s adher -
ence to the MD are presented in Table  4. Accordingly, 
the results indicated that the mean age ( p = 0.007), total 
score of SFLS ( p = 0.029), the SFLS’ subcomponent food 
skills (p = 0.001), and the ELSA score ( p = 0.005) were sig-
nificantly higher among participants adhering to the MD 
compared to those who were not.
The relationships among some variables, the SFLS, 
ELSA, and MEDAS, are shown in Table  5. SFLS total 
score was positively associated with all subcomponents 
of SFLS ( p < 0.001), ELSA (p < 0.001), and MEDAS score 
(p < 0.05). A positive relationship was also found between 
all subcomponents of SFLS and ELSA ( p < 0.001). 
MEDAS scores show a positive correlation with food 
skills ( p < 0.001) and action intent and action strategies 
(p < 0.05), which are subcomponents of SFLS. Also, a sig -
nificant positive relationship was observed between the 
ELSA score and MEDAS (p < 0.05).
A binary logistic regression analysis was conducted 
to identify factors associated with adherence to the 
MD (Table  6). Age was positively associated with MD 
(OR = 1.05, 95% CI: 1.01–1.09, p = 0.005). Similarly, edu-
cation duration was positively associated with adherence

Discussion
The results of the current study suggest that participants’ 
educational status is associated with adherence to the 
MD, and that Environmental Literacy and Sustainable 
Food Literacy scores are higher in women than in men. 
Additionally, differences in age, SFLS total score, and 
ELSA total score were observed between participants 
who adhered to the MD and those who did not. Upon 
further evaluation of factors influencing MD adherence, 
variables such as marital status, education duration, 
and ELSA and SFLS scores were identified significant 
variables.
In this study, women demonstrated significantly higher 
ELSA scores than men (Table  2). This finding is consis -
tent with the prior investigation by Akçay and Şengül, 
who reported that female students had better environ -
mental literacy levels than males [ 20]. Another study 
found no significant relationship between gender differ -
ences and ELSA total score. However, the score obtained 
from the environmental awareness subscale was found to 
be significantly higher in women [ 21]. These results sug-
gest a potential gender-based difference in environmental 
consciousness, which might influence broader sustain -
able practices.
No significant association was found between edu -
cational status and ELSA scores in this study (Table  2). 
Similarly, a study conducted among students reported 
no statistically significant difference in environmental 
literacy among grade levels [ 20]. In contrast, another 
study found that participants who were members of envi-
ronmental groups and had greater educational attain -
ment also had higher levels of environmental awareness 
[21]. This result is consistent with previous studies that 
suggested women might become more involved in

gender-based disparities in real dietary behaviour. Unex -
pectedly, there was no significant difference in SFLS 
scores by educational attainment. Given that the majority 
of participants were young and university-educated, this 
could be because the sample was homogeneous. In these 
groups, media exposure or personal interests may have 
a greater impact on food literacy than formal education. 
These findings suggest that sustainable nutrition may be 
affected by multidimensional factors (environmental and 
individual) in addition to educational status.

university and postgraduate qualifications [ 16]. However, 
another study showed that education level did not signifi-

possible explanation for the absence of a significant asso -
ciation in our study is the limited number of participants 
classified as obese, which could have reduced the statisti -
cal power to detect such a relationship (Table 3).
MD is widely recognized as a sustainable diet due to its 
environmental benefits [ 4, 30]. One of its key features is 
the reduction in meat consumption, which studies have 
shown to have a less negative impact on the environ -
ment [31]. For instance, a study with over 6000 partici -
pants showed that participants who adhered more to the 
MD resulted in lower dietary CO 2 emissions than those 
who did not [ 32]. In another study, greater adherence to 
the MD is correlated with a decreased ecological foot -
print [33]. In a study by Rosi et al., a low correlation was 
observed between the students’ compliance with the MD 
and their total carbon and ecological footprints [ 34]. In 
this study, consistent with the literature, it was found that 
environmental literacy and sustainable nutrition knowl -
edge and attitudes increased depending on compliance 
with the MD.
Education status also emerged as a significant factor 
in adherence to the MD in our study. Participants with 
higher education levels exhibited greater adherence, 
consistent with other studies that have identified a posi -
tive correlation between education duration and MD 
adherence [ 35, 36]. In this study, education duration 
was among the factors affecting adherence to the MD 
(Tables 5 and 6). In addition, a significant difference was 
found between the MEDAS score and education levels of 
the participants categorized as Secondary School/High 
School and Bachelor/Master degree (Table  2). Higher 
education may facilitate greater awareness and imple -
mentation of dietary practices that promote health and 
sustainability.
Studies have shown that compliance with the MD is 
affected by age, gender, and marital status factors [ 37–
41]. A research investigation involving university staff  
revealed a significant positive association between com -
pliance with the MD and age [38]. In another study, com-
pliance with the MD according to age was higher in the 
elderly than in the young. Researchers attribute this to 
the fact that older individulas do not have a job, which 
facilitates cooking and eating at home easier [39]. A study 
conducted in Ireland with individuals over 50 years found 
a positive relationship between increasing age and female 
gender and compliance with the MD [ 40]. Similarly, in 
a cohort study found that compliance with the MD was 
lower among individuals younger 49 years and higher 
in individuals over 62 years [ 41]. In this study, age was 
among the factors affecting adherence to the MD, and a 
positive relationship was found between age and adher -
ence to the MD (Tables 4, 5 and 6).
Numerous studies have demonstrated that adher -
ence to MD is higher in women than men [ 36, 42, 43]. 
For instance, in a study of 1,333 adults, women had sig -
nificantly higher MEDAS scores than men [ 42]. Simi -
larly, research by Mieziene et al. in Lithuania found that 
women scored higher on MEDAS than men [ 36], and a 
study examining MD compliance during the COVID-19 
pandemic also observed the same pattern [ 43]. However, 
a study conducted among university students found no 
significant gender difference in MEDAS scores [ 44]. In 
contrast to these studies, the present study revealed no 
statistically significant difference between males and 
females regarding MEDAS scores (Tables  4 and 6). The 
divergence may be attributed to the specific characteris -
tics of our study population and the tools used to mea -
sure adherence, which may yield different outcomes 
depending on their sensitivity to gender differences.
Lastly, marital status was found to influence MD adher-
ence in our study, with participants who lived alone 
exhibiting greater adherence than those in relationships. 
This result is consistent with research by Apostolaki et al., 
which also found a link between marital status and MD 
adherence [37]. However, another study did not show any 
relationship between age, gender, marital status and, MD 
adherence [45]. In a study, MD adherence was assessed 
using different tools in the same population. The influ -
ence of gender and age variables on adherence to the MD 
exhibited considerable variability. Therefore, the effect of 
the measurement tools used to determine the effect of 
factors such as gender, age, and marital status should be 
considered [46]. In the present study demonstrated that 
marital status was among the factors affecting compli -
ance with the MD (Table 6).
Strengths and limitations
The research possesses several notable strengths. It rep -
resents one of the pioneering investigations evaluating 
the constructs of environmental literacy, sustainable food 
literacy, and adherence to the Mediterranean Diet (MD) 
among adults. Furthermore, the study identified sig -
nificant associations between adherence to the MD and 
being married, having higher sustainable food literacy, 
Page 8 of 9
Özata-Uyar et al. BMC Public Health         (2025) 25:4171 
and higher environmental literacy. These results promote 
our knowledge of the potential relationships between 
sociocultural and demographic characteristics and sus -
tainable and healthful eating habits. The research has 
several limitations: it employed a cross-sectional design, 
which implies that although it was unable to establish a 
causal relationship, it was nonetheless employed to eval -
uate the interrelations among the measured variables. 
Secondly, the self-reporting via online snowball sampling 
methodology was selected for data collection, which 
introduces the possibility of self-report biases and inac -
curacies in reporting [ 47]. Thirdly, although we included 
male and female adults between 19 and 64 in the study, 
the sample may not be representative because it mainly 
consisted of younger adults and women. Finally, the indi -
viduals’ self-reported data included measurements of the 
participants’ height and body weight, which may have 
affected the accuracy of BMI.
Conclusion
In conclusion, this study identified significant associa -
tions between adherence to the Mediterranean Diet and 
factors such as longer education duration, higher sus -
tainable food literacy, higher environmental literacy, 
and marital status. These findings suggest that certain 
sociodemographic and literacy-related factors may influ -
ence MD adherence in young adults. Nonetheless, the 
results highlight the potential value of promoting sus -
tainable and environmental literacy in supporting health-
ier dietary patterns. Future studies with more diverse and 
representative samples are recommended to validate and 
extend these findings.
