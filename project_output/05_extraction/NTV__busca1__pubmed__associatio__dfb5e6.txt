Abstract
Background: Osteoarthritis (OA) is increasingly recognized as a 
metabolically influenced inflammatory disease, particularly in the context of 
obesity. Wnt1-inducible signaling pathway protein 1 (WISP1), a downstream 
mediator of Wnt/β-catenin signaling, has been implicated in adipose tissue 
inflammation and cartilage remodeling; however, clinical data regarding 
circulating serum WISP1 levels in obesity-associated OA remain limited.

This study aimed to investigate the relationship between serum WISP1 
concentrations and OA status in obese adults. Methods: This cross-
sectional study included 180 participants classified into three groups: 
healthy non-obese controls (n = 60), obese individuals without OA (n = 60), 
and obese individuals with OA (n = 60). Clinical, anthropometric, metabolic, 
and inflammatory parameters were recorded. Serum WISP1 concentrations 
were measured using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Group

operating characteristic (ROC) analyses were performed. Results: Serum 
WISP1 concentrations differed significantly among groups, showing a 
stepwise increase from healthy controls to obese OA (–) and obese OA (+) 
participants (p < 0.001). WISP1 remained independently associated with

Introduction
Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most prevalent degenerative joint disorder 
worldwide and represents a leading cause of chronic pain, disability, and 
reduced quality of life among aging populations (1). Pathologically, OA is 
characterized by progressive articular cartilage loss, subchondral bone 
remodeling, osteophyte formation, and varying degrees of synovial 
inflammation, ultimately resulting in impaired joint function (2). In parallel 
with global demographic aging and the rising prevalence of obesity, the 
overall burden of OA is projected to increase substantially in the coming 
decades (3). Although historically regarded as a consequence of mechanical 
“wear and tear,” OA is now widely recognized as a complex, multifactorial 
disease in which biomechanical, metabolic, and inflammatory factors interact 
to drive structural joint deterioration (4).

Obesity is among the most influential modifiable risk factors for OA. 
Beyond increasing mechanical loading on weight-bearing joints, adipose 
tissue functions as a metabolically active endocrine organ that secretes 
adipokines and pro-inflammatory mediators capable of influencing cartilage 
and synovial biology (5). Elevated circulating levels of cytokines such as 
interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) have been 
associated with synovial inflammation and cartilage degradation in OA (6). 
Moreover, increased expression of leptin and its functional receptor (Ob-Rb) 
has been demonstrated in osteoarthritic cartilage, suggesting a role for 
adipokine-mediated modulation of matrix turnover (7). These observations 
have contributed to the concept of “metabolic OA,” wherein obesity-related 
low-grade systemic inflammation synergizes with mechanical stress to 
exacerbate structural joint damage (8).
Recent investigations have increasingly emphasized the inflammatory 
component of OA, suggesting that its role extends beyond a secondary 
response to structural damage. Synovial tissue frequently exhibits 
macrophage and T-lymphocyte activation, and synovitis has been associated 
with structural progression and pain severity (9). Pro-inflammatory cytokines 
and chemokines stimulate the production of catabolic enzymes, particularly 
matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and aggrecanases (notably ADAMTS-4 
and ADAMTS-5), thereby accelerating degradation of type II collagen and 
aggrecan (10). These processes contribute to a self-perpetuating cycle of 
matrix breakdown, cellular stress, and impaired repair capacity (11).

Within this inflammatory-metabolic framework, the Wnt/β-catenin 
signaling pathway has emerged as a key regulator of cartilage homeostasis. 
Physiological Wnt activity is essential for cartilage development; however, 
dysregulated activation promotes chondrocyte hypertrophy, catabolic 
enzyme expression and osteophyte formation in OA (12). Experimental 
studies demonstrate that excessive Wnt signaling accelerates cartilage 
degeneration, whereas its inhibition mitigates structural damage, 
underscoring its relevance in OA progression (13).
Wnt1-inducible signaling pathway protein 1 (WISP1) is a matricellular 
protein and downstream effector of Wnt/β-catenin signaling. As CCN4, a 
member of the CCN family of matricellular proteins, WISP1 interacts with 
extracellular matrix components and cell-surface receptors to regulate 
cellular proliferation, fibrosis, and tissue remodeling (14). In human 
osteoarthritic cartilage, WISP1 expression is increased in lesioned regions 
compared with preserved areas and is associated with disease-related 
alterations in DNA methylation. In vitro studies indicate that WISP1 exposure 
in chondrocytes reduces extracellular matrix synthesis while enhancing 
expression of matrix-degrading enzymes such as MMPs and ADAMTS (15). 
Furthermore, Wisp1-deficient mice develop attenuated cartilage 
degeneration following experimental OA induction, supporting its 
involvement in structural joint pathology (16).
Beyond its role in joint tissues, WISP1 has also been characterized as 
an adipokine linked to obesity and metabolic dysfunction. Increased WISP1

expression in adipose tissue of obese individuals correlates with markers of 
inflammation and insulin resistance (17). Serum WISP1 concentrations have 
been associated with body mass index (BMI) and metabolic parameters in 
human cohorts (17-20), and experimental studies demonstrate that WISP1 
can interfere with insulin signaling pathways and modulate inflammatory 
cascades (18). These findings position WISP1 at the intersection of adiposity, 
insulin resistance, and low-grade systemic inflammation.
Despite accumulating experimental and tissue-level evidence 
implicating WISP1 in cartilage remodeling and metabolic inflammation, 
clinical data regarding serum WISP1 concentrations in OA remain scarce. 
Most available studies have focused on intra-articular expression or 
experimental models, whereas the relationship between systemic WISP1 
levels and OA status in well-characterized obese populations has not been 
adequately examined. It therefore remains unclear whether elevated serum 
WISP1 reflects adiposity-related metabolic activation alone or is also 
associated with OA at the systemic level.
Therefore, the present study aimed to evaluate serum WISP1 
concentrations in obese individuals with and without OA and to explore their 
association with clinical and inflammatory parameters. By addressing the gap 
between experimental findings and serum biomarker data, this study seeks 
to clarify the potential relevance of WISP1 within the metabolic–inflammatory 
framework of obesity-associated osteoarthritis.

Study Design
This single-center, cross-sectional observational study was conducted 
at the Department of Internal Medicine, Kanuni Sultan Suleyman Training 
and Research Hospital. The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the 
Institutional Clinical Research Ethics Committee (approval date: 20 February 
2025; decision no: E-80929729-000-269138296) and was performed in 
accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. Written informed consent was 
obtained from all participants prior to enrollment. An a priori power analysis 
was conducted using G*Power software (version 3.1.9.2; Heinrich Heine 
University, Düsseldorf, Germany). Based on variance estimates derived from 
a pilot subset and assuming a one-way ANOVA (fixed effects, omnibus), the 
effect size was calculated as Cohen’s f = 0.31. With a significance level (α) of 
0.05, statistical power (1−β) set at 0.95, and three study groups, the minimum 
required total sample size was 164 participants. A total of 180 individuals 
were ultimately included, exceeding the calculated requirement.
Study Population
A total of 180 participants aged 18–80 years were consecutively 
recruited during the study period. Participants were categorized into three 
predefined groups (n = 60 per group) according to BMI and OA status: Group 
1, healthy non-obese controls (HC); Group 2, obese individuals without OA 
[obese OA (-)]; and Group 3, obese individuals with OA [obese OA (+)]. Groups 
were frequency-balanced with respect to age and sex distribution. Obesity 
was defined according to World Health Organization criteria as BMI ≥30

kg/m² (21). Anthropometric measurements were obtained under 
standardized conditions using calibrated equipment. The diagnosis of OAwas 
established based on compatible clinical symptoms and radiographic 
findings, including joint space narrowing, osteophyte formation, and 
subchondral sclerosis, in accordance with contemporary American College of 
Rheumatology clinical classification frameworks for symptomatic OA (22). 
Radiographic severity was graded using the Kellgren–Lawrence

experienced orthopedic specialist. Radiographs were assessed by the same 
orthopedist, and in cases of diagnostic uncertainty, images were 
independently reviewed by a second orthopedist to reach consensus. 
Laboratory personnel were blinded to clinical group allocation.

Exclusion criteria included secondary arthritis (rheumatoid arthritis, 
gout, psoriatic arthritis, post-traumatic arthritis), prior major joint surgery, 
congenital musculoskeletal abnormalities, severe systemic disease (advanced 
hepatic, renal, or cardiac failure), active malignancy, diagnosed diabetes 
mellitus, chronic inflammatory or autoimmune disorders, long-term systemic

Data Collection
Demographic data (age, sex, family history), comorbidities, and 
medication use were recorded from electronic medical records. Body weight 
and height were measured using standardized calibrated devices, and BMI 
was calculated as weight (kg) divided by height squared (m²). Body 
composition analysis was performed using a multi-frequency bioelectrical 
impedance analyzer (Tanita MC-780MA, Tokyo, Japan), providing 
measurements of total body fat percentage, visceral fat index, fat-free mass, 
and skeletal muscle mass. All measurements were conducted in the morning 
following an overnight fast of at least 8 hours. Participants were instructed 
to avoid caffeine and strenuous physical activity for 24 hours prior to 
assessment. Measurements were performed by the same trained operator 
according to manufacturer guidelines. Blood pressure was measured in the 
seated position after a minimum of 10 minutes of rest. Two readings were 
obtained and averaged for analysis.
Sample Collection
Fasting venous blood samples were collected between 08:00 and 09:00 
a.m. after an overnight fast of 8–10 hours. Samples were drawn into plain 
tubes, centrifuged at 4°C (relative centrifugal force equivalent to 
approximately 3000 × g) for 10 minutes, and serum aliquots were stored at

Laboratory staff were blinded to group allocation. Routine biochemical

Measurement of Serum WISP1 Levels
Serum WISP1 concentrations were measured as the primary biomarker using 
a double-antibody sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kit 
(Human WISP1 ELISA Kit, Sunred Biological Technology Co., Shanghai, 
China; Catalog No. 201-12-2360) according to the manufacturer’s 
instructions. Samples and standards were analyzed in duplicate. Optical 
density was measured at 450 nm using a calibrated microplate reader 
(BioTek ELx808, Winooski, VT, USA), and mean values were used for 
statistical analysis. The assay sensitivity was 20.2 pg/mL, with a measurable 
range of 22–6000 pg/mL. Serum WISP1 concentrations were expressed in 
pg/mL. The intra-assay and inter-assay coefficients of variation were <10% 
and <12%, respectively. Repeated freeze–thaw cycles were avoided to 
preserve analyte stability. All samples were analyzed in duplicate within a

Statistical Analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics version 
26.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). Normality of continuous variables was 
assessed using the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test. Normally distributed variables 
were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD), and non-normally

variables were reported as frequencies and percentages. Group comparisons 
were performed using one-way ANOVA for normally distributed variables and 
the Kruskal–Wallis test for non-normally distributed variables. Post hoc 
comparisons were conducted using Bonferroni correction (ANOVA) or Mann–
Whitney U test (Kruskal–Wallis). Categorical variables were analyzed using 
Pearson’s chi-square test. The distribution of serum WISP1 levels across

healthy non-obese controls, 60 obese individuals without OA, and 60 obese 
individuals with OA. The median age of the study population was 54 years. Of 
the participants, 152 (84.4%) were female and 28 (15.6%) were male. Within

Significant differences were observed in anthropometric and body 
composition parameters. BMI, waist circumference, waist-to-hip ratio, 
muscle mass, visceral fat level, and fat-free mass index were significantly 
higher in both obese OA (–) and obese OA (+) groups compared with HC (all

fat percentage was significantly highest in the obese OA (+) group, lower in 
the obese OA (–) group, and lowest in HC (p < 0.001).

were higher in both obese groups than in healthy controls (all p < 0.001),

obese OA (+). HDLc levels were lower in the obese OA (–) group than in 
controls (p = 0.007), with no significant difference between the two obese 
groups (Table 1).

Serum WISP1 concentrations differed significantly across groups, being 
lowest in healthy controls, intermediate in obese individuals without OA, and 
highest in obese individuals with OA. This stepwise pattern is biologically 
plausible and consistent with previous literature. Murahovschi et al. (17) 
demonstrated increased WISP1 expression in adipose tissue in obesity, 
linking it to inflammatory activation and insulin resistance–related metabolic 
processes, while Mirr et al. (24) reported elevated circulating WISP1 levels 
associated with metabolic dysfunction. In the context of OA, Blom et al. (25) 
showed that Wnt pathway activation is associated with increased WISP1 
expression and cartilage degeneration, and van den Bosch et al. (15) 
documented WISP1 overexpression in damaged osteoarthritic cartilage, 
including epigenetic regulation. Together, these findings provide a coherent 
framework in which the higher serum WISP1 levels observed in obese 
individuals with OA may be associated with reflect the coexistence 
convergence of metabolic activation and structural joint remodeling. Most 
prior evidence is derived from tissue-based or experimental studies, whereas 
the present analysis evaluates systemic serum concentrations. Therefore, 
serum WISP1 should not be interpreted as a direct surrogate of intra-
articular expression but rather as a marker potentially associated with 
reflecting integrated metabolic and joint-related processes.
Subgroup analysis showed no significant difference in serum WISP1 between 
knee and non-knee OA, suggesting that the association is not confined to 
weight-bearing joints. Griffin et al. (26) emphasized that obesity-related OA

involves metabolic mechanisms beyond mechanical load, while Zhuo et al. 
(27) and Courties et al. (28) highlighted interactions between metabolic 
disturbances and joint tissues across multiple compartments. Similarly, 
Conde et al. (29) and Pottie et al. (30) demonstrated systemic effects of 
adipose-derived factors and complex metabolic–inflammatory pathways in 
OA. Within this context, the lack of localization-specific variation in WISP1 
levels supports a systemic metabolic–inflammatory contribution across 
multiple joint compartments, although subgroup size and phenotypic 
heterogeneity warrant cautious interpretation.
Anthropometric findings support the interpretation of serum WISP1 levels. 
As expected, obese participants exhibited higher BMI and central adiposity 
compared with controls, consistent with established links between excess 
adiposity and OA risk (26–28). However, these measures did not clearly 
differentiate obese OA (+) from OA (–) individuals, suggesting that body mass 
alone may not determine OA status within obesity. This dissociation is 
consistent with prior work emphasizing metabolic and inflammatory 
alterations beyond mechanical load (29,30).

lower HDLc. However, lipid parameters did not significantly differ between

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The cross-sectional design 
of the present study does not allow causal inference and limits precludes 
temporal inference interpretation; therefore, it cannot be determined 
whether elevated serum WISP1 levels precede OA development or reflect 
established structural and metabolic alterations. Serum WISP1 has not been 
extensively characterized in obese OA phenotypes, and analyses were 
conducted in a single cohort without external validation. OA classification 
was based on clinical and radiographic criteria without advanced 
inflammatory phenotyping, limiting characterization of disease 
heterogeneity. In addition, patient reported outcome measures, such as pain 
severity and functional status, were not assessed, which limits the ability to 
directly relate serum WISP1 concentrations to clinical manifestations of OA. 
Although multivariable models adjusted for major confounders, residual 
confounding from visceral adiposity, medication use, physical activity, and 
other metabolic factors cannot be excluded. In particular, the use of 
osteoarthritis-related medications, including non-steroidal anti-inflammatory

systemic inflammatory markers and circulating WISP1 levels cannot be 
excluded. In addition, intra-assay coefficient of variation values and extended 
analytical validation parameters were not assessed, which may represent a 
limitation.
Serum measurements do not identify the primary tissue source of 
WISP1; circulating levels may originate from multiple tissues, including 
adipose tissue, synovium, and articular cartilage, all of which have been 
implicated in OA and metabolic inflammation. Therefore, serum WISP1 
should be interpreted as reflecting integrated systemic activity rather than 
tissue-specific expression. Future studies incorporating synovial fluid 
analysis, tissue-level expression profiling, and paired systemic local 
measurements are warranted to clarify the relative contribution of these 
sources and the absence of synovial fluid assessment limits interpretation of 
systemic versus intra-articular contributions. In addition, the single-center 
design and subgroup sample sizes may restrict generalizability. The study 
population was predominantly female; therefore, the findings should be 
interpreted in the context of a female-predominant cohort. Finally, ROC 
findings reflect internal discrimination and require independent validation 
before clinical applicability can be considered.
