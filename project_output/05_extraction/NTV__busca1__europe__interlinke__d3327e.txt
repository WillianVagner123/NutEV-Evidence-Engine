Abstract
Background Food insecurity and limited food literacy are important public health concerns associated with poorer 
psychological well-being and lower quality of life. This study aimed to examine the relationships between food 
insecurity, food literacy, quality of life, and mental well-being among adults in Türkiye.
Methods A cross-sectional online survey was conducted among adults aged 18–64 years in Türkiye (n = 1,295). Food 
insecurity was assessed using the USDA Food Security Survey Module (FSSM), food literacy was measured with the 
Short Food Literacy Questionnaire (SFLQ), quality of life was evaluated using the EUROHIS Quality of Life 8-Item Index 
(EUROHIS-QOL-8), and mental well-being was assessed with the Short Warwick–Edinburgh Mental Well-being Scale 
(SWEMWBS).
Results Food insecurity was prevalent in 44.1% of participants. Logistic regression analysis indicated that lower 
income (p < 0.001), lower education (p < 0.001), female gender (p = 0.003) and having more children (p = 0.018) 
were significantly associated with higher odds of food insecurity. The food-insecure group had lower food literacy 
(p < 0.001), quality of life (p < 0.001), mental well-being (p < 0.001), and number of employed individuals (p < 0.01). 
FSSM scores were negatively correlated with SFLQ (p < 0.01), EUROHIS-QOL-8 (p < 0.01), and SWEMWBS (p < 0.01). In 
linear regression analysis (R² = 0.248, p < 0.001), a one-year increase in age was associated with a 0.038-point decrease 
in FSSM total score. One-point increases in EUROHIS-QOL-8, SWEMWBS, and SFLQ scores were associated with 
decreases of 0.173, 0.042, and 0.064 points in FSSM total score, respectively. A one-unit increase in the number of 
employed individuals was associated with a 0.478-point decrease in FSSM total score.
Conclusions Food insecurity is associated with socioeconomic challenges, inadequate food literacy, lower quality 
of life, and reduced mental well-being. Policies integrating economic support with nutrition education, food literacy 
promotion, and mental health services may help address food insecurity and improve population well-being.

Background
Food security exists when all people, at all times, have 
physical, social, and economic access to sufficient, safe, 
and nutritious food that meets their dietary needs and 
food preferences for an active and healthy life. It is struc -
tured around four key dimensions: availability, access, 
utilization, and stability over time. If any of these dimen -
sions is compromised, food insecurity may result [ 1]. 
Food insecurity can take many forms. Individuals may 
worry about running out of food before they can afford 
to buy more. They may also be unable to maintain a bal -
anced diet, skip meals, or, in extreme cases, go without 
food for several days [ 2]. Food insecurity is also a major 
social determinant of health [3]. Individuals experiencing 
food insecurity are more vulnerable to chronic physical 
and mental health problems, as well as communicable 
and non-communicable diseases, compared with those 
who are food-secure [4].
Food literacy has received increasing attention as a fac -
tor associated with food insecurity [ 5]. It is defined as “a 
collection of interrelated knowledge, skills, and behav -
iors required to plan, manage, select, prepare, and eat 
food to meet needs and determine intake” [ 6]. Beyond 
knowledge and skills, food literacy enables individuals, 
households, and communities to maintain diet quality 
and make balanced food choices over time. Enhancing 
food literacy through education, skill-building, and sup -
port in navigating food-related policies and programs has 
been proposed as a strategy to promote healthy dietary 
practices and mitigate food insecurity [ 7]. Additionally, 
higher levels of food literacy have been linked to lower 
levels of food insecurity [8].
Food insecurity has been associated with quality of 
life and mental well-being outcomes [ 9]. Adults experi -
encing food insecurity report poorer life satisfaction, 
increased stress, and reduced psychological resilience 
[9, 10]. Although food insecurity and food literacy have 
been investigated in various populations [ 5, 11– 14], 
there is still a lack of comprehensive research linking 
these factors to quality of life and mental well-being. 
Understanding these interrelationships is essential for 
developing effective strategies and public health policies 
that promote healthier nutritional behaviors and psy -
chological resilience. Therefore, the present study aimed 
to determine the prevalence of food insecurity among 
adults in Türkiye and to examine its associations with 
food literacy, quality of life, and mental well-being.
Methods
Study design and participants
This descriptive cross-sectional study was conducted 
among adults aged 18–64 years. A total of 1295 indi -
viduals (531 men and 764 women) participated. Sample 
size was determined using an a priori power analysis 
conducted with G*Power version 3.1.9.7 [ 15]. An inde -
pendent samples t-test (two-tailed) was used as the basis 
for the calculation. Assuming a medium effect size (d = 
0.40), an alpha level of 0.05, and a statistical power of 0.90 
(1– β), the minimum required sample size was calculated 
as 216 participants.
Participation was voluntary, and informed consent was 
obtained from all participants. Anonymity and confiden -
tiality were ensured throughout the process.
Data collection
Data were collected online between February and April 
2024 using a structured questionnaire administered via 
Google Forms. The survey link was disseminated via 
social media platforms such as WhatsApp, Instagram, 
and X. A snowball sampling approach was employed, 
where initial respondents were invited to share the sur -
vey with others in their networks. The questionnaire 
comprised six sections, including questions on sociode -
mographic characteristics, anthropometric measure -
ments, food insecurity, food literacy, quality of life, and 
mental well-being.
Sociodemographic characteristics
The sociodemographic variables assessed included age, 
gender, marital status, educational level, number of 
children, number of household members, income level, 
employment status, and primary occupation. Additional 
items evaluated financial status (family income rela -
tive to the minimum wage; balance between income and 
expenses).
Anthropometric measurements
Self-reported weight and height were used to calculate 
Body Mass Index (BMI), defined as weight (kg) divided 
by height squared (m²). BMI categories followed the 
World Health Organization (WHO) classification: under-
weight (< 18.50 kg/m²), normal weight (18.50–24.99 kg/
m²), overweight (25.00–29.99 kg/m²), and obese (≥ 30.00 
kg/m²) [16].

Food insecurity
Food insecurity status during the past 12 months was 
measured using the 10-item validated U.S. Department 
of Agriculture (USDA) Adult Food Security Survey Mod -
ule (FSSM) [ 17]. Affirmative responses were summed 
to yield a total score between 0 and 10. Based on USDA 
guidelines, participants were categorized as high food 
security (score = 0), marginal food security (score = 1–2), 
low food security (score = 3–5), or very low food security 
(score = 6–10). As FSSM scores increase, levels of food 
security decrease, indicating higher levels of food inse -
curity. For statistical analyses, high and marginal food 
security were combined and defined as “food secure, ” 
while low and very low food security were combined and 
defined as “food insecure [ 18]. In present study, Cron -
bach’s α coefficient was 0.880.
Food literacy
Food literacy was assessed using the Short Food Literacy 
Questionnaire (SFLQ) developed by Krause et al. [ 19]. 
The SFLQ consists of 12 items rated on four- or five-
point Likert scales and evaluates functional, interactive, 
and critical food literacy within a unidimensional struc -
ture. Higher total scores indicate greater food literacy. 
In the original study, Cronbach’s α was 0.82. The Turk -
ish adaptation and validation were conducted by Durmuş 
et al. [ 20], who reported a Cronbach’s α of 0.803. Par -
ticipants scoring ≥ 31 points were categorized as having 
adequate to excellent food literacy, while those scoring < 
31 points were classified as having inadequate to limited 
food literacy [ 21]. In the present study, Cronbach’s α was 
found to be 0.90.
Quality of life
Quality of life was evaluated using the World Health 
Organization Quality of Life Scale, Short Form (EURO -
HIS-QOL-8) [ 22]. This eight-item unidimensional mea -
sure assesses overall quality of life, general health, energy, 
daily living activities, self-esteem, social relationships, 
finances, and home environment. Each item is rated on a 
five-point Likert scale, and higher scores represent better 
quality of life. The Turkish validation was conducted by 
Eser et al. [ 23], who reported a Cronbach’s α of 0.85. In 
the present study, Cronbach’s α was found to be 0.90.
Mental wellbeing
Mental well-being was measured using the Short War -
wick-Edinburgh Mental Wellbeing Scale (SWEMWBS), a 
seven-item unidimensional short form of the WEMWBS 
developed by Stewart-Brown et al. [ 24]. Items are rated 
on a five-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree to 5 = 
strongly agree), yielding total scores ranging from 7 to 35, 
with higher scores indicating better mental well-being. 
The Turkish adaptation and validation were conducted 
by Demirtaş and Baytemir [25], who reported Cronbach’s 
α values ranging from 0.84 to 0.86. In the present study, 
Cronbach’s α was found to be 0.92.
Statistical analysis
Data analyses were performed using the Statistical Pack -
age for the Social Sciences (SPSS), version 28.0. The 
normality of continuous variables was assessed using 
the Kolmogorov–Smirnov and Shapiro–Wilk tests. Cat -
egorical variables were summarized as frequencies and 
percentages, and group differences were examined using 
the chi-square test. Descriptive statistics for continuous 
variables were presented as number (n), mean (X ̄), and 
standard deviation (SD). Differences between two inde -
pendent groups were evaluated using the independent 
samples t-test. Pearson’s correlation coefficients were 
calculated to assess the relationships among continuous 
variables. To identify psychosocial predictors of food 
insecurity, multivariable linear regression analysis was 
performed with FSSM total score as the dependent vari -
able. Standardized beta coefficients ( β) were calculated 
to compare the relative contribution of each predictor. 
The absolute values of the standardized β coefficients 
were plotted in a radar chart to visually display the rela -
tive strength of each predictor in the regression model. 
Additionally, binary logistic regression analysis was con -
ducted to determine demographic factors associated with 
food insecurity, and results were reported as odds ratios 
(ORs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Statistical sig -
nificance was set at p < 0.05 for all analyses.
Results
Table  1 summarizes the descriptive characteristics of 
participants (n = 1,295). The mean age of individuals was 
31.2 ± 11.60 years, ranging from 18 to 64 years. Among 
the participants, 59.0% were female and 41.0% were male. 
More than half of the respondents were single (53.6%), 
and most had a university degree or higher (66.9%). The 
mean household size was 5.0 ± 2.3 persons, with an aver -
age of 2.4 ± 1.28 children per household. The mean num -
ber of employed individuals per household was 1.4 ± 1.02.
Regarding economic status, 11.2% of households 
reported an income below the minimum wage, 45.1% 
reported income at the level of the minimum wage, and 
only 43.7% earned above the minimum wage. Nearly 
half of the participants (44.3%) stated that their income 
was lower than their expenses. The unemployment rate 
among the participants was 56.4%, and the most com -
mon occupations were students (34.1%), civil servants 
(16.6%), and housewives (16.1%).
The mean FSSM score was 2.8 ± 3.20, with 55.9% of 
individuals categorized as food-secure (FS) and 44.1% 
as food-insecure (FI). The mean SFLQ total score was 
28.1 ± 9.15, indicating that 60.0% of participants had

Discussion
This study aimed to assess the relationships between food 
insecurity, food literacy, quality of life, and mental well-
being among adults in Türkiye. The results showed that 
nearly half of the participants (44.1%) experienced food 
insecurity. Food insecurity was more prevalent among 
females, unmarried individuals, those with lower edu -
cation and income levels, and households with a higher 
number of children. Moreover, inadequate food literacy, 
lower quality of life, and poorer mental well-being were 
significantly associated with food insecurity.
Food insecurity remains a critical public health nutri -
tion concern in both developed and developing coun -
tries. According to the 2024 State of Food Security and 
Nutrition in the World  report, an estimated 2.3 billion 
people worldwide experienced moderate or severe food 
insecurity in 2024, corresponding to 28.0% of the global 
population [ 26]. Although there is no nationally repre -
sentative estimate of food insecurity in Türkiye, studies 
conducted in different population groups report sub -
stantial prevalence rates, including 42.7% among adults 
[27], 21.6% among women of reproductive age [ 28], 68% 
among university students [ 29], and 30% among older 
adults [ 30]. In the present study, the prevalence of food 
insecurity was 44.1%. Variations across studies may be 
attributed to differences in sociodemographic character -
istics of the study samples, as well as differences in mea -
surement tools and classification criteria used to assess 
food insecurity. The prevalence observed in the present 
study may partly reflect Türkiye’s prolonged period of 
high inflation, declining purchasing power, and increas -
ing living costs [31].
Food insecurity is influenced by several sociodemo -
graphic factors, including gender, marital status, number 
of children, education level, and income [ 32, 33]. In this 
study, lower household income and a lower number of 
employed household members were associated with food 
insecurity, which is consistent with evidence showing

men, and gender-based inequalities in access to educa -
tion, employment, and productive resources contribute 
to increasing the risk of poverty and inadequate food 
access among women and girls [ 35– 37]. Marital status 
was another important factor, as unmarried individuals 
showed higher food insecurity, likely due to fewer oppor -
tunities for income sharing and resource pooling [ 4]. The 
number of children in the household also increased risk, 
since more dependents create greater financial pressure 
and caregiving responsibilities [ 38, 39]. Lower education 
level was also associated with higher vulnerability, con -
sistent with evidence showing that individuals with less 
education and fewer food-related skills face greater chal -
lenges in securing and managing food resources [40, 41].
Food insecurity not only reflects economic hardship 
but also poses serious health risks. Studies have linked it 
to lower nutrient intake, poor sleep quality, higher rates 
of depression, and a greater prevalence of metabolic dis -
orders such as type 2 diabetes and hypertension [ 2, 39]. 
Paradoxically, food insecurity has also been associated 
with overweight and obesity, particularly among women, 
due to reliance on inexpensive, calorie-dense, and nutri -
ent-poor foods [ 42, 43]. However, in the present study, 
no significant differences in BMI were observed between 
food-secure and food-insecure participants. Differences 
in food insecurity measurement and classification, as well 
as energy-related factors such as physical activity, eating 
behaviors, gender, and age in the study population, may 
explain the absence of an association [44].
Food insecurity and food literacy showed a signifi -
cant negative association in the present study. This find -
ing is consistent with previous research. Begley et al. [ 5] 
reported that lower engagement in food literacy–related 
behaviors, such as planning, management, shopping, 
preparation, and cooking, was significantly associated 
with food insecurity. Similarly, Jomaa et al. [ 14] reported 
that food insecurity was associated with lower par -
ticipation in food literacy practices, such as selecting, 
purchasing, and preparing healthy and nutritionally bal -
anced foods. Limited food literacy may limit individu -
als’ ability to plan, choose, and prepare affordable meals, 
thereby increasing their vulnerability to food insecurity, 
particularly under economic constraints [ 5, 45]. Addi -
tionally, in this study, inadequate food literacy was iden -
tified in 60.0% of participants, a proportion higher than 
those reported in earlier research, with Morgan et al. 
[46] observing inadequate food literacy in about 38–40% 
of university students and Hanbazaza and Alaklabi [ 13] 
reporting rates of 30–35% among adults. This difference 
may be related to the sociodemographic characteristics 
of the study population and to variations in how food lit -
eracy is assessed and classified across studies.
Consistent with previous research [ 47– 49], higher 
levels of food insecurity in the present study were sig -
nificantly associated with lower quality of life scores. 
Possible pathways that may help explain the association 
between food insecurity and lower quality of life include 
poor diet quality, limited access to healthy foods, finan -
cial difficulties, and increased stress. Together, these 
factors may contribute to reduced social participation, 
increased daily strain and feelings of insecurity, and lower 
perceived quality of life [ 50– 52]. These findings highlight 
that food insecurity extends beyond material deprivation 
and is closely related to perceived quality of life through 
nutritional and social mechanisms.
Food insecurity is related to poorer mental health and 
well-being outcomes [ 53]. In this study, food insecurity 
showed a negative correlation with mental well-being, 
and a 1-unit increase in SWEMWBS was associated 
with a 0.042-point decrease in the FSSM total score. Evi -
dence from analyses across 160 countries similarly dem -
onstrates that food insecurity is strongly associated with 
reduced well-being and increased mental health symp -
toms, independent of income and other structural fac -
tors [ 54]. A recent meta-analysis further indicated that 
food insecurity significantly increases the risk of stress 
and depression [ 55], supporting our finding that indi -
viduals experiencing food insecurity had lower mental 
well-being. A recent study conducted in Türkiye reported 
that individuals experiencing food insecurity had sig -
nificantly lower Flourish Index scores, indicating poorer 
overall well-being. The same study further highlighted 
that the adverse effects of food inflation were particularly 
pronounced among food-insecure groups, emphasiz -
ing the role of the current economic context in shaping 
well-being outcomes [ 56]. Several mechanisms may help 
explain these associations. Chronic uncertainty associ -
ated with food insecurity may trigger physiological stress 
responses, including activation of the hypothalamic–
pituitary–adrenal axis, which can contribute to elevated 
cortisol levels and depressive symptoms [ 10]. Food inse -
curity also carries emotional and social consequences—
such as shame, guilt, and social isolation—that further 
intensify psychological distress and undermine individu -
als’ coping abilities [55, 57]. Together, these mechanisms 
illustrate how food insecurity adversely affects mental 
health and well-being and highlight the need for inte -
grated interventions that strengthen psychological resil -
ience alongside efforts to improve food security.
Limitations and strengths
There are several limitations to this study. First, the 
cross-sectional design allows for identifying associations 
between food insecurity, food literacy, quality of life, and 
mental well-being; however, it does not permit causal 
inference. Second, because data were collected online 
Page 8 of 9
Esin et al. Archives of Public Health            (2026) 84:56 
through voluntary participation, the sample may not be 
representative of the broader adult population and may 
be biased toward individuals with higher education levels 
or more reliable internet access. Third, body weight and 
height were self-reported rather than objectively mea -
sured, which may have introduced measurement error 
and affected the accuracy of BMI classification. Despite 
these limitations, this study’s strengths include the 
assessment of multiple related domains—food insecu -
rity, food literacy, quality of life, and mental well-being—
which allows for a more comprehensive understanding 
of their interactions in adults, while the large sample 
size strengthens the validity of the analyses and the use 
of validated instruments enhances the reliability of the 
findings.
Conclusions
This study showed that food insecurity was prevalent 
among adults and was significantly associated with lower 
food literacy, poorer quality of life, and reduced mental 
well-being. Regression analyses indicated that gender, 
education level, income status, and number of children 
were significantly related to food insecurity. Together, 
these findings highlight the multidimensional nature of 
food insecurity and underscore the importance of inte -
grated public health strategies that combine nutrition 
education, social and economic support, and mental 
health promotion to improve overall well-being and resil-
ience in the context of food insecurity.
