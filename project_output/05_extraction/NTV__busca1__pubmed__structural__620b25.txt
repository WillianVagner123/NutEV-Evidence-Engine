Abstract
Background Consistent overconsumption of high-sugar food and beverages (HSFB)–defined as consuming more than 25 g 
of added sugars per day [ 1]–is a health risk factor for Black young adult women in the United States. A diet high in added 
sugar can increase women’s likelihood of developing chronic conditions, like obesity, for which they are already at a high 
risk. Research often focuses on individual-level contributors to this eating behavior; however, studies infrequently consider 
how structural barriers may affect individuals’ eating habits.
Purpose The current study examines whether experiencing more structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food is 
associated with amount of consumption of HSFB and if this association is mediated by one individual-level factor - internal 
health locus of control.
Methods Data come from 504 Black young adult women (Mage= 24.7, 98.4% cisgender) who completed questionnaires via 
an online survey.
Results Experiencing more structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food was associated with more consumption 
of HSFBs (β = 0.05, p = .013, 95% CI = 0.01, 0.09). Specifically, the availability of fast food near where women lived, dif -
ficulties accessing transportation to purchase healthy foods, and the cost of food or grocery delivery are associated with more 
HSFB consumption. Internal health locus of control did not mediate the association between barriers and consumption (95% 
CI = -0.01, 0.02).
Conclusions More research is needed to assess the mechanisms linking structural barriers and HSFB consumption. Address-
ing contextual factors, like structural barriers, may be a critical step to equitably improve eating behaviors for Black young 
adult women.

United States have overweight (classified as a body mass 
index of 25.0–30.0) or obesity (body mass index of 30.0 
or greater), which can increase their risk for chronic condi -

of overweight and obesity compared to Black young adult

For Black young adult women, the consumption of high-
sugar foods and beverages can increase their risk of devel -
oping or worsening conditions like obesity, heart disease, 
diabetes, and chronic kidney disease, all of which dispro -
portionately affect this group [ 7–9]. This vulnerability is 
especially salient during young adulthood, a highly transi -
tional period of life encompassing milestones like attending 
college, entering the workforce, establishing close relation-
ships, and starting a family [ 10]. For Black young adults, 
this developmental stage can lead to inconsistent access to 
high-fiber foods, including fruits, vegetables, and whole 
grains, and greater consumption of prepackaged and pro -
cessed foods that contain added sugars [ 11, 12]. Research 
has shown that while sugar intake has generally declined 
since the 2000s [ 13, 14], it remains elevated among Black 
women and young adults [15, 16]. This suggests that Black 
young adult women may have a greater likelihood of con -
suming high-sugar food and beverages. Given that young 
adulthood is a critical developmental period for establishing 
lifelong health behaviors [17, 18], it is necessary to identify 
factors driving high-sugar food and beverage consumption 
for Black young adult women to clairify areas to intervene 
early and prevent the early onset of chronic diseases.
Structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food 
may affect Black young adult women’s ability to engage in 
healthy eating. We define structural barriers as rooted in dis-
proportionate inequities Black people face due to societal 
marginalization [19]. Structural barriers pose impediments 
to access and consumption of safe and nutritious food, 
specifically perceived healthy food, meaning that what is 
considered “safe and nutritious food” may vary by person. 
Structural barriers can include limited or uncertain access to 
adequate food (food insecurity) [ 20] or disproportionately 
fewer grocery stores in one’s neighborhood but an over -
saturation of fast-food restaurants and convenience stores 
[21]. Additionally, high costs associated with purchasing 
healthier food [22] or having fewer fresh food options avail-
able at the nearest grocery stores, supermarkets, or conve -
nience stores [ 23] can be barriers. Lack of availability of 
healthy food, consumption of readily accessible nutrient-
poor energy-dense foods [24], and limited knowledge, time, 
and financial resources are also common [ 25]. These barri-
ers can all lead to unhealthy eating, which can encompass 
eating behaviors like high consumption of sugar-sweetened 
beverages and foods high in added sugar [26–30].
Black young adult women may be disproportionately 
affected by structural barriers to accessing perceived 
healthy food, which may lead them to consume high-sugar 
foods and beverages. However, existing literature on struc -
tural barriers and consumption of high-sugar foods and 
beverages is limited in two notable ways. First, many exist-
ing studies examine only one or two structural barriers to 
accessing healthy food, with a preponderance of research on 
food insecurity [20, 21, 28, 29, 31–34]. Second, associations 
between structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy 
food and their consequences largely have been examined 
for Black adults writ large or older Black women, and 
there is a paucity of investigations specific to Black young 
adult women (i.e., at the intersection of race, gender, and 
age). Black people experience greater structural barriers to 
accessing healthy food compared to other racial and ethnic 
groups [20, 32], and research with older Black adult women 
suggests that they are particularly at risk for food insecurity 
[35, 36]. Although not specific to Black women, research 
with Black young adults indicates that structural barriers 
to accessing healthy food is a prominent issue for this age 
group [28, 31, 37, 38]. In one of the few studies with Black 
young adult women, 22% of the sample reported being food 
insecure [34]. In the current study, we fill gaps in the litera-
ture by examining the associations between a wider range of 
structural barriers and consumption of high-sugar food and 
beverages. We examine these associations specifically for 
Black young adult women, who may be at disproportionate 
risk of consumption of high-sugar food and beverages [5].
The food-insecurity obesity paradox states that people 
experiencing food insecurity, particularly women, face an 
elevated risk of having overweight or obesity [34, 35]. This 
is because individuals experiencing food insecurity may 
alternate between periods of hunger and consumption of 
nutrient-poor energy-dense foods (e.g., high-fat, high-sugar, 
high-calorie foods), which typically cost less than healthier 
options like lean meats, vegetables, and fruits [24]. Accord-
ing to this theory, increased energy intake may be an adap -
tive response to threats to food security [ 25]. When Black 
young adult women experience barriers to accessing food, 
specifically healthy food, this may increase their consump -
tion of less nutritious food options. Therefore, in accordance 
with the food-insecurity obesity paradox, we predict that 
more structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food 
will be associated with more consumption of high-sugar 
foods and beverages for Black young adult women [25, 35].
One individual-level factor that may explain the asso -
ciation between structural barriers to accessing perceived 
healthy food and consumption of high-sugar foods and bev-
erages is internal health locus of control, which is defined 
as an individual’s belief that they can control their health 
and that their health is contingent on their own actions and 
behaviors [39–41]. Black young adult women who experi -
ence structural barriers may especially feel that they won’t 
succeed no matter what effort they put in to make healthy 
choices because of these barriers beyond their control. 
Although research on internal health locus of control has 
largely failed to consider the role of structural barriers, some 
research does support the hypothesis that experiencing more

barriers may reduce internal health locus of control. For 
instance, food insecurity is associated with less self-efficacy 
for healthy eating [ 42–44]. In turn, individuals with low 
internal health locus of control and the related construct of 
self-efficacy are less likely to engage in unhealthy behaviors 
[45, 46]. Lower internal health locus of control or self-effi -
cacy has been associated with consuming a diet low in fruit 
and vegetables [ 46] and high in fat and excess sugars [ 45, 
47], including more consumption of sugar-sweetened bever-
ages and sugary snacks in samples of adolescents and young 
adults [48, 49]. However, the majority of this research is not 
specific to Black young adult women and largely focuses on 
the singular structural barrier of food insecurity. Therefore, 
we aim to examine whether Black young adult women who 
face more structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy 
food subsequently experience lower internal health locus 
of control and in turn, consume more high-sugar foods and 
beverages.
In the current study, we aim to examine if internal health 
locus of control explains the association between structural 
barriers to accessing perceived healthy food and consump -
tion of high-sugar food and beverages. We hypothesized that 
experiencing more structural barriers to accessing perceived 
healthy food would be associated with more consumption of 
high-sugar food and beverages (i.e., total effect; Hypothesis 1).
We propose a significant indirect effect of internal health 
locus of control on high-sweet foods and beverages, such 
that the association between structural barriers to accessing 
perceived health food and consumption of high-sugar foods 
and beverages will be partially mediated through internal 
health locus of control (i.e., indirect effect; Hypothesis 2). 
We predicted that experiencing more structural barriers to 
accessing perceived healthy food would be associated with 
lower internal health locus of control (i.e., direct effect; 
Hypothesis 3) and that lower health locus of control would 
be associated with more consumption of high-sugar food 
and beverages (i.e., direct effect; Hypothesis 4).
Methods
Participants
Data come from a larger study of the health of 504 Black 
young adult women from the United States ( Mage = 24.70, 
SDage = 4.90). Only Black women (with ethnic identi -
ties across the African diaspora; see Table 1) between the 
ages of 18 and 35 who resided in the United States were 
eligible to participate. The majority of participants identi -
fied their Black ethnic background as African American 
(74.40%) and almost all participants identified as cisgender 
women (98.40%). More than half of the participants were 
not currently students (54.20%) and the sample’s average 
household income was between $45,000 and $54,999. Less 
than 1% (0.60%, n = 3) of participants reported being diag -
nosed with diabetes.
Procedure
The larger study (conducted from September to November 
2021) was approved by Institutional Review Board at North 
Carolina State University. Participants were recruited from 
across the United States using the online research crowd -
sourcing platform Prolific, which has been cited as the only 
online behavioral research platform that provides quality 
participant responses high in attention, comprehension, and 
honesty [50]. Potential participants who meet eligibility cri-
teria and are enrolled in Prolific’s participant panel received 
information about the study. They then accessed the link to 
an online Qualtrics survey via the Prolific platform. After 
completing electronic informed consent via this survey, par-
ticipants re-answered eligibility questions. If participants 
were eligible, they completed a series of questionnaires that 
were presented in a randomized order. In accordance with 
best practices [51], the survey included a mix of types of bot

Measures
Structural Barriers to Accessing Perceived Healthy Eating
Self-reported access to perceived healthy eating was mea -
sured using nine items developed for the current study. 
Although there are questionnaires that examine access to 
healthy food, they are conducted predominantly with older 
adult populations. Therefore, we used the existing body 
of literature on structural barriers to accessing perceived 
healthy eating [ 23, 52] to develop our own measure that 
included items about access, especially relevant to young 
adults during the study collection period (e.g., use of tech -
nology for food delivery, COVID-19 precautions). Partici -
pants were asked to report the extent to which structural 
factors impacted their ability to eat healthy foods in the past 
month on a seven-point Likert-type scale from 0 ( not at all 
impactful) to 6 (extremely impactful). It is important to note 
that this measure assesses participants’ reports of barriers 
to accessing perceived healthy eating, meaning it does not 
assess objective healthy eating.
Before proceeding with use, we wanted to assess whether 
the items followed a unidemensional factor structure, since 
previous literature (e.g., Caspi et al., [53]) suggests that there 
are five dimensions of food access. Initial items included 
eight pre-formulated structural factors and one open-ended 
item that was recoded. These items included: (1) the cost 
of healthy foods, (2) the availability of healthy food near 
where I live, (3) the quality (e.g., freshness, variety, season-
ality) of healthy foods near where I live, (4) the availability 
of fast food near where I live, (5) the number of options 
available for places to purchase healthy foods near where I 
live, (6) difficulties accessing transportation to get to places 
to purchase healthy foods, (7) concerns about the safety of 
going out to purchase healthy foods during COVID-19,1 (8) 
the cost of food delivery or grocery delivery, and (9) some-
thing else we haven’t considered. Open-ended responses to 
item nine were examined and central themes were derived 
to either create a new item or re-code responses into a preex-
isting item. This generated one new item, which was called 
“lack of material resources” and included structural barriers 
like “storage for healthy foods” and “access to a kitchen.” 
Since it was not feasible to collect a new sample to do an 
exploratory factor analysis (EFA) and confirmatory fac -
tor analysis (CFA), we used the split sample strategy (see 
Cudeck & Browne [54]), to conduct the EFA with a random 
sample of ~ 50% of the cases (n = 248). Then, we conducted 
the CFA with the full sample. After conducting the EFA and 
CFA, two items were removed (see Supplemental Appen -
dix). A total mean score was calculated from the remaining 
eight items such that a higher score indicates having more 
structural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food. The 
internal consistency of the measure in the current sample 
was acceptable (Cronbach’s α = 0.84).
Internal Health Locus of Control
The internal health locus of control subscale of the Multidi-
mensional Health Locus of Control scale was used to assess 
participants’ feelings of control over their health [ 41]. Par-
ticipants rated their agreement with six items on a six-point 
Likert-type scale from 1 ( strongly disagree) to 6 ( strongly 
agree). An example item is “If I get sick, it is my own 
behavior which determines how soon I get well again.” The 
total subscale score was created by averaging items, such 
that higher scores indicate a greater internal health locus of 
control. The internal consistency of the measure in the cur -
rent sample was acceptable (Cronbach’s α = 0.72).
Consumption of High-Sugar Foods and Beverages
Participant’s average consumption of high-sugar food and 
beverages was measured using one item from the diet sub -
scale of the Diabetes Self-Care Activities measure, which 
was modified for non-diabetic populations [ 55]. While this

Covariates
Sociodemographic factors can impact Black young adult 
women’s access to healthy food and subsequent consump -
tion of high-sugar food and beverages [ 61]. Therefore, we 
measured seven potential covariates. We considered age 
and student status because in young adults who attend uni -
versity, consumption of high-fat and high-sugar foods are 
greater in younger young adults who attend university [62]. 
Participants entered a number for their age and indicated if 
they were currently a student using the following options: 
No (coded as 0) and Yes (undergraduate or graduate student; 
coded as 1). We also measured household income and paren-
tal education as proxies of socioeconomic status. As over half 
of our sample was under 24 years old and this age group is 
still not fully independent, often relying on their parents for 
financial support [63], we used a parental index of education 
as an additional proxy for socioeconomic status. As house -
hold income can impact diet choices [29], participants indi-
cated their total annual household income from a range of 
presented amounts in $10,000 increments from 1 ($25,000) 
to 10 (Over $100,000). We also measured parental educa -
tion (i.e., highest level of education completed by parents 
or guardians) because it is a predictor of nutrition-related 
health risk in Black young adult women [ 64]. Participants 
selected their parental education from eight categories, with 
options ranging from “Less than high school” to “Doctoral 
or professional degree.” The experience of living through 
the COVID-19 pandemic disproportionately impacted 
Black communities. Specifically, the pandemic affected 
food access and affordability in Black communities, increas-
ing the likelihood of high-sugar consumption [ 33]. There-
fore, participants indicated the degree to which COVID-19 
impacted their health negatively in the past month on a six-
point Likert-type scale from 1 (Not at all) to 6 (Extremely).
Data Analytic Plan
A power analysis using Monte Carlo simulation for a mediation 
model [65] using a confidence level of 95%, 10,000 replications, 
and moderate correlations between variables (r = .20) indicated 
that 96% power to detect an effect for the most complex model 
parameter (i.e., the indirect effect) was achieved with a sample 
size of 504. There were no missing data.
We first ran descriptive statistics to describe sample 
characteristics and used preliminary analyses to determine 
which of our variables needed to be included as sociode -
mographic covariates in the serial mediation. We examined 
seven potential sociodemographic covariates in our analy -
sis (age, student status, household income, parent educa -
tion, COVID-19 impact). Bivariate correlations, one-way 
ANOV As, and chi-squares were run to assess which should 
be included in our final models. To be included as a covari-
ate, the variable had to be significantly associated (defined 
as p<.05) with both a predictor and an outcome variable, 
to appropriately reduce confounding of primary statisti -
cal associations of interest [ 66]. The final covariates that 
met this criterion and were therefore included in our model 
were age and household income.
To answer our substantive research questions, we con -
ducted a Poisson regression mediation model with maxi -
mum likelihood estimation using Mplus version 8.3 [ 67]. 
In these analyses, the structural barriers to accessing per -
ceived healthy food (predictor) and internal health locus of 
control (mediator) were both modeled as continuous, and 
consumption of high-sugar foods and beverages (outcome) 
was modeled as a count variable. A Poisson regression was 
the most appropriate test considering our outcome variable 
type. In line with previous research, we tested for overdis -
persion to ensure that using a standard Poisson regression 
model was most appropriate versus an overdispersed Pois -
son regression model or negative binomial regression [ 68]. 
We first graphed the outcome variable, which appeared to be 
fairly normally distributed. Then, we conducted the model 
as a Poisson regression and as a negative binomial regres -
sion and compared the AIC values [69]. The Poisson regres-
sion model had the lowest AIC values and was therefore 
retained. Additionally, in the negative binomial regression 
model, the dispersion parameter had a coefficient of zero 
which indicates that the model is better estimated using 
Poisson regression. To assess whether each hypothesis was 
supported or not supported we examined p-values to see if 
they were below our predetermined significance value of 
0.05, which would indicate that the hypothesis was sup -
ported. We also examined 95% confidence intervals to see 
if they did not contain zero, which would also indicate that 
the hypothesis was supported. To assess mediation, we used 
the Monte Carlo method with 20,000 repetitions. From this 
simulation [70], we estimated a 95% confidence interval for 
the indirect effect (i.e., the product of the a and b paths). The 
indirect effect was deemed significant if it did not include 0.

Results
Descriptive Statistics and Preliminary Analyses
Table 2 presents descriptive statistics and correlations 
among the study variables. On average, participants reported 
that between 25% and 50% of their meals contained high-
sugar food and beverages within the past week. Women in 
our sample were moderately impacted by structural barri -
ers to perceived healthy eating ( M = 3.33, SD = 1.64) and 
reported fairly high levels of internal health locus of control 
(M = 4.37, SD = 0.80).
Mediation
Table 3 reports the direct, indirect, and total effects of the 
mediation paths in the model. Approximately 2% of the 
variance in consumption of high-sugar food and beverages 
was explained by structural barriers to perceived healthy eat-
ing, internal health locus of control, and covariates (Pseudo 
R2 = 0.02). The confidence interval for the model indirect 
effect included zero (95% CI = −0.01, 0.002), indicating that 
mediation was not supported.
Hypothesis 1
In support of hypothesis 1, the association between struc -
tural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food on con -
sumption of high-sugar food and beverages was significant. 
For every 1-unit increase in structural barriers to perceived 
healthy eating, the log of the expected count of high-sugar 
foods and beverage consumption increased by 0.05 units 
(β = 0.05, p =.013, 95% CI = 0.01, 0.09). In other words, 
experiencing more structural barriers to perceived healthy 
food is associated with a significantly greater proportion of 
consumption of high-sugar foods and beverages.
Hypothesis 2
Contrary to hypothesis 2, the associations between structural 
barriers to accessing perceived healthy food and internal 
health locus of control (β = 0.04, p =.070, 95% CI = −0.003, 
0.08) and between internal health locus of control and con -
sumption of high-sugar food and beverages ( β = −0.02, p 
=.742, 95% CI = −0.10, 0.07) were each nonsignificant.
Sensitivity analysis
To get more detailed insight into the specific structural bar-
riers that led to increased high-sugar food and beverage con-
sumption, a Spearman’s correlation was conducted between 
each of the structural barrier items and the high-sugar food 
and beverage outcome. Results revealed a positive weak cor-
relation between high-sugar food and beverage consump -
tion and the availability of fast food near where participants 
lived (rs=0.19, p<.001), difficulties accessing transportation 
to purchase healthy foods (rs=0.15, p=.001), and the cost of 
food delivery or grocery delivery (rs=0.13, p=.003).

examination of the association between structural bar -
riers to accessing perceived healthy foods in consump -

tural disadvantage–such as living in a food swamp or food 
apartheid [ 35], experiencing difficulty affording food [ 23, 
26, 71], and finding transportation to purchase healthy food 
[30, 72]–is associated with limited access to healthier food 
options. Having to navigate such barriers may lead Black 
young adult women to select convenient, time-saving, and 
cost-effective foods [ 26], many of which are more pro -
cessed and higher in added sugar [ 73]. When considering 
the life demands during young adulthood (e.g., school, 
work, caretaking, etc.), selecting the more convenient and 
cost-effective option is less stressful than trying to purchase 
healthier options that may exceed what is feasible finan -
cially and timewise [ 26]. Another reason that experiencing 
greater structural barriers may be associated with greater 
high-sugar food and beverage consumption is that Black 
young adult women may use high-sugar foods to cope with 
the psychosocial stress of these barriers. Stress eating is 
the consumption of energy-rich foods high in sugar and 
fat in response to negative stimuli or affect [ 74], and is a 
common coping mechanism in which Black young adult 
women engage when experiencing stress [ 75–77]. Taken 
together, our findings indicate that structural barriers to 
access to perceived healthy food may be associated with 
the nutritional choices of Black young adult women, even 
among those who only faced a relatively moderate number 
of barriers. Our results suggest that these barriers may be 
associated with less healthy eating even among those who 
do not experience significant barriers like food insecurity.
Contrary to our second hypothesis, internal health locus 
of control did not explain the association between struc -
tural barriers to accessing perceived healthy food and 
high-sugar food and beverage consumption. Health locus 
of control theory would suggest that external factors like 
structural barriers would impact one’s internal sense of 
control [78], however, a paucity of empirical studies have 
examined this proposition, and even fewer have consid -
ered multiple structural barriers for Black young adult 
women. It may be that Black women use adaptive and pro -
active coping strategies to combat any reduction in sense 
of control in the face of structural disadvantages. Adaptive 
coping strategies are generally action-oriented and include 
thoughts and behaviors that aim to resolve stressors [ 79], 
and proactive coping strategies are those that are leveraged 
in anticipation of future stressors [ 80]. For Black young 
adult women, factors such as the superwoman schema, 
awareness of inequity in society, and leveraging social 
support may be ways they proactively cope with barri -
ers and do so in an adaptive way for them. Superwoman 
schema includes a perceived obligation to project strength 
and succeed despite limited resources [ 81]. Some Black 
young adult women may recognize structural barriers they 
face but may take on a superwoman role, reframing these 
barriers to emphasize their own strength and focus on cre -
ating their own success to cope with the circumstances 
and confront life adversity [ 82]. When Black young adult 
women expect that they will experience greater structural 
barriers rooted in inequities in society, perhaps the mere 
presence of these barriers in their life may not limit their 
own sense of control because they do not internalize these 
as individual shortcomings but instead recognize them as 
societal flaws.
Another possible explanation is that Black young adult 
women facing barriers to healthy food access may also 
receive some degree of instrumental social support from 
family, friends, and community members to overcome 
these barriers. If they can successfully navigate any indi -
vidual barriers they face, their own sense of internal health 
locus of control may not be affected. An important consid -
eration is that women reported fairly low levels of impact 
from COVID-19, which was assessed as a covariate in this 
investigation. Because some of the structural barriers were 
tailored to COVID-19, this may indicate that they did not 
feel like COVID-19 imposed additional barriers. More -
over, our sample reported low levels of percieved structural 
barriers altogether, which suggests that they may not have 
perceived salient structural barriers to accessing healthy 
food, in general. Therefore, internal health locus of control 
may not be a reason why barriers increase high-sugar food 
and beverage consumption. It may be that if Black young 
adult women do not perceive these barriers as particularly 
salient, internal health locus of control naturally plays less 
of a role in guiding their consumption of high-sugar foods 
and beverages. When barriers are experienced (e.g., the 
cost of purchasing healthy food or food delivery) women 
may feel that these external constraints inevitably dictate 
what they can access and consume, thereby mitigating the 
influence of thier own internal sense of control. It should 
be noted that the predictors in the model explained a small 
amount of variance in the outcome, suggesting that future 
research should continue assessing various structural barri -
ers to accessing healthy food, specifically relevant to Black 
young adult women, that may affect consumption of high-
sugar foods and beverages.

Limitations and Future Directions
Several limitations and constraints on the generalizability 
of the current study should be noted. As data are from a 
cross-sectional correlational study, causal claims cannot 
be made. The use of mediation with cross-sectional data is 
not ideal. However, scholars suggest that the use of media -
tion is appropriate for theory-driven foundational work 
for which associations are not currently known and more 
information is needed before moving to a more costly and 
time-consuming experimental or longitudinal design [ 83]. 
The current investigation is an example of this foundational 
work, which is necessary basic research for early-phase 
intervention development and testing. Nearly all Black 
young adult women in our study were cisgender and Black 
women with other marginalized gender identities (e.g., non-
binary, transgender) may face additional structural barriers 
to healthy eating, so these results are not expected to rep -
licate beyond cisgender Black women. We utilized a non-
probability national sample and participants were recruited 
online. Although the majority of young adults are online, the 
results may not generalize to all Black young adults across 
the United States, and some who may face greater barri -
ers to access and health may not be fully represented in our 
sample. The majority of participants lived in the South, but 
we do not have data on the degree of urbanicity of the places 
where they resided. Urbanicity may impact structural bar -
riers to healthy eating, for instance, the degree of access to 
certain grocery stores or the time it takes to secure healthy 
food. Therefore, we cannot guarantee that the results of this 
study will replicate across different regions or would not 
further differ by urbanicity.
Some limitations arise from the measurement of struc -
tural barriers and high-sugar foods and beverages. A novel 
measure of structural barriers was used because of a lack 
of preexisting measures that were both sufficiently short 
and applicable to assess this construct in our target popu -
lation. However, this measure didn’t capture the length of 
time participants had experienced each structural barrier 
or the stressfulness of each barrier. Therefore, future work 
should consider testing the moderating role of barrier dura -
tion and stress and how this may illuminate further nuance 
in the role of internal health locus of control. Next, the mea-
sure of high-sugar food and beverage consumption was a 
single-item indicator of the construct and only asked about 
consumption during meals, rather than between meals (e.g., 
as snacks). It may be that we are missing a primary eat -
ing context in which consumption of high-sugar food and 
beverages occurs. Data from NHANES 2015–2018 reveals 
that among US adults ( ≥ 20 years) mean calorie intake was 
highest during snacking (e.g., desserts, sweets, salty snacks, 
sugar-sweetened beverages) among high added sugar 
consumers, which corresponded to 43% of calories from 
added sugars [4]. This is followed by dinner (23.5%), lunch 
(18.7%), and then breakfast (15%). Since our item focuses 
on consumption across meals, our estimate of high-sugar 
food and beverage consumption may be more conservative 
as it is not diasaggragated by meals and does not explicity 
focus on snacking where the bulk of consumption is. Addi -
tionally, our measure relies on participant’s interpretation of 
what constitutes high-sugar (e.g., just beverages or added 
sugars during cooking) and meals (e.g., all three meals 
or just one meal). This may mean that participants likely 
reported based on the most salient sources or occasions of 
their high-sugar food and beverage consumption.
However, by using a measure that captures the quantity 
of consumption, which is expressed in terms of a percent -
age of meals rather than in grams or other nutritional intake 
values, this allows participants to more easily recall their 
approximate intake. Additionally, as this item assesses con-
sumption over seven days, it gives a more general sense 
of high-sugar food and beverage consumption rather than 
over just 24-hours which could be unrepresentative of par -
ticipant’s typical consumption patterns. This measure was 
the only one available in the larger study and was selected 
because it offered a time-efficient way to get a general 
sense of nutrition. Given that we examined our research 
questions among a non-clinical sample, this measure pro -
vides a useful baseline of meal-time consumption and can 
be used as a starting point for further nutrition research. 
Future investigations should prioritize measures that rely 
less on long-term recall and are less prone to potential bias 
such as daily diary methods or leveraging the use of bio -
markers like urinary sucrose [ 84] and isotope ratios [ 85]. 
Studies should also focus on providing a more granular 
understanding of added sugar consumption separately for 
foods, beverages, meals, and snacks. This will be critical 
to accurately assess the added sugar consumption in Black 
young adult women.
The current investigation did not measure food insecurity 
in the current study and therefore was not able to account for 
this as a covariate. Experiencing food insecurity may have 
also been associated with women’s consumption of high-
sugar foods and beverages and could have magnified their 
expercience with the structural barriers. Therefore, future 
research should include food insecurity to assess how many 
women were food insecure and control for this when assess-
ing the extent to which structural barriers affect consump -
tion of high-sugar food and beverage consumption.
Implications
Black women are disproportionately at risk for negative 
health consequences associated with unhealthy eating [ 2]

Journal of Racial and Ethnic Health Disparities
and young adults consume high amounts of high-sugar 
food and beverages [ 5]. Yet there is a paucity of research 
on structural barriers, internal health locus of control, and 
high-sugar foods and beverages at the intersection of race, 
gender, and age, despite the necessity of this specificity for 
tailored intervention efforts. This study adds to the body 
of literature examining how structural factors may contrib -
ute to health behaviors for Black young adult women. Our 
results suggest that structural-level factors are associated 
with Black women’s consumption behaviors even relatively 
early in the life-course during young adulthood. Research -
ers can use this information to examine the environmental, 
psychological, and biological contexts in which structural 
barriers during young adulthood do and do not translate 
into health risk for Black women later in life. Focusing 
solely on individual-level attitudes necessary for behavior 
change may not go far enough to engender health equity for 
this population. As a first step, behavioral medicine prac -
titioners could employ screenings for structural barriers 
to healthy food access alongside nutrition assessments for 
Black young adult women. New behavioral medicine inter-
ventions can be designed or existing interventions modi -
fied to account for or address structural barriers, which may 
require interdisciplinary collaboration (e.g., implementa -
tion science). Policy at local and national levels should 
be enacted to focus on mitigating these structural barriers, 
and ensure that they are being ameliorated equitably for 
disproportionately impacted populations. Taken together, 
identifying structural barriers to health behaviors for mar -
ginalized racial, ethnic, and gender populations remains an 
important priority for a behavioral medicine grounded in 
health equity.
