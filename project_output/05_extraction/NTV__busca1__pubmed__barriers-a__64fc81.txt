Barriers and facilitators for implementation 
of lifestyle related blended care interventions 
for overweight, obesity and type 2 diabetes 
mellitus: a scoping review
Chiara Lansink1,2*  , Anouk Middelweerd1, Gozewijn Dirk Laverman1,2 and Nienke Beerlage-de Jong3 
Abstract 
Background Blended care, referring to the combination of digital health interventions (DHI) with in-person care, 
has proven cost-effective in promoting healthy behaviours, especially for lifestyle related conditions like type 2 diabe-
tes mellitus. However, despite positive results, many interventions fail during the clinical implementation. A successful 
implementation of blended care interventions requires a systematic approach including appropriate implementation 
strategies that address relevant barriers and facilitators. Therefore, this study explores barriers and facilitators for imple-
mentation of lifestyle related blended care interventions for weight management and type 2 diabetes mellitus.
Methods Following PRISMA-ScR guidelines, a scoping review was conducted using PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Sci-
ence, covering studies from January 1, 2000, to February 4, 2025. The search included terms related to ‘overweight,’ 
‘obesity,’ ‘diabetes mellitus type 2’ , ‘eHealth’ , ‘blended care’ , ‘setting’ and ‘implementation’ . Studies were included if they 
focused on lifestyle changes (e.g. weight loss, physical activity, or self-management) and reported barriers or facilita-
tors for implementing blended care, defined in this review as the integration of DHI with at least one in-person care-
focused interaction between a health care provider and an adult patient. No risk of bias assessment was performed. 
Data were extracted on study characteristics, participants demographics, DHI delivery methods, and implementation 
barriers and facilitators. Data was analysed using a narrative synthesis structured by the Consolidated Framework 
for Implementation Research (CFIR).
Results From 1329 screened studies, nine met the inclusion criteria. The findings indicate that implementation chal-
lenges cluster around financial constraints, increased workload for healthcare providers, and disparities in digital skills, 
which collectively limit the feasibility and sustained use of blended DHIs. The other way around, successful implemen-
tation is supported by adequate training, user-centred design, and strong alignment with existing clinical workflows. 
Tailoring interventions to organisational context and ensuring regulatory compliance further strengthen adoption.
Conclusions Blended DHIs offer opportunities to enhance personalization, patient involvement, and continu-
ity of care. However, their sustainable implementation requires addressing these overarching barrier and facilitator 
domains rather than isolated factors: Healthcare organisations can act to reduce organisational burden by providing 
sufficient resources, policymakers can support regulatory alignment, developers can prioritize user-centred design, 
and healthcare professionals can integrate blended care intro routine practice and support patients in its use.

Introduction
Diabetes Mellitus type 2 (T2DM) is one of the four 
major non-communicable diseases [1 ]. In 2021, the 
global prevalence of diabetes among individuals 
aged 20 to 79 reached 536.6 million and is expected 
to increase to 783.2 million by 2045 [2 , 3]. Individu -
als with overweight and obesity are at a higher risk of 
developing type 2 diabetes [4 ]. As the number of people 
living with overweight and obesity continues to grow, 
so too are associated healthcare costs [5 ].
Lifestyle factors, like diet and physical activity, are key 
in managing and even reversing T2DM [6 , 7]. However, 
adopting and maintaining a healthy lifestyle is chal -
lenging for many individuals, partly because of differ -
ent levels of motivation and self-management skills [8 , 
9]. For people with T2DM, these challenges are further 
compounded by the broader demands of self-manage -
ment, which includes not only making lifestyle changes 
but also adhering to medication and monitoring blood 
glucose levels [10– 12]. Effective self-management helps 
individuals take control of their health, manage symp -
toms, and improve their quality of life [13– 15]. Yet, 
despite clinical guidelines emphasising lifestyle change 
as the first step in treatment, existing programs, such 
as combined lifestyle interventions, often lack sufficient 
support to facilitate sustainable lifestyle changes [16].
The rapid growth of interactive digital health inter -
ventions (DHI), including smartphone apps for track -
ing diet and physical activity, has proven effective in

Although these reviews provide valuable insights into the 
broader implementation of digital health, they do not spe-

This review aims to bridge this gap by identifying key

T2DM. By synthesizing the results, the aim is to optimize 
healthcare delivery, enhance patient outcomes and sup -
port sustainable lifestyle changes.
Methods
Protocol and registration
This review has been conducted in accordance with the 
Preferred Reporting for Systematic Reviews and Meta-
Analysis Extension for Scoping Reviews guidelines, using 
the PRISMA-ScR checklist [43]. No formal protocol was 
registered for this review.
Eligibility criteria
The inclusion criteria for articles were as follows: (1) the 
publication must be in English or Dutch; (2) participants 
must be healthcare professionals directly involved in the 
care for patients with overweight, obesity and/or T2DM; 
and/or (3) participants must be adults (> 18  years) with 
T2DM and/or obesity/overweight. Participants may pre -
sent with a combination of metabolic syndrome criteria 
or cardiovascular risk factors. Studies were eligible if they 
included healthcare professionals, adult patients or both; 
(4) the publication must be a peer-reviewed scientific 
journal article or conference paper; (5) the intervention 
must be aimed at lifestyle changes such as weight loss or 
increasing physical activity or improving self-manage -
ment; (6) the study must focus on barriers or facilitators 
for implementing blended care, defined in this review as 
the integration of a DHI with at least one instance of in-
person care between health care provider and patient. The 
exclusion criteria included the following: (1) participants 
with co-morbidities that are not related to metabolic syn-
drome or cardiovascular risk factors; (2) participants with 
serious mental illness; (3) participants who are pregnant; 
and (4) participants with substance addiction.
Information sources and search strategy
A search was performed for articles between 1 st of Janu-
ary 2000 and the date of search (4th February 2025), on 
PubMed, Scopus, and Web of Science. These databases 
were selected because together they cover the fields most 
relevant to this review, including public health, techno -
logical, and implementation research related to blended 
care. The key words that were used for the search were 
‘overweight, ’ ‘obesity, ’ diabetes mellitus type 2’ , eHealth’ , 
‘blended care’ , ‘setting’ and ‘implementation’ . The com-
plete electronic search strategy for the PubMed database 
is detailed in Additional file  1. To ensure consistency 
among all databases, the same search strategy was 
applied for the three databases, PubMed, Scopus, and 
Web of Science. All searches were restricted to articles 
published in English or Dutch. Only journal articles, con-
ference papers, and meta-analyses were included, with all 
other publication types excluded.
Selection of studies
Duplicates were removed through Covidence [44], 
and the first author manually removed the remaining 
duplicates. The first author (CL) screened the titles and 
abstracts of all publications, while a second author (NB) 
independently screened 20% of these titles and abstracts. 
Initial agreement during title and abstract screening was 
limited (Cohen’s kappa, κ = 0.29), reflecting variability in 
the interpretation of the term ‘blended care. ’ Discrepan-
cies were discussed to clarify the inclusion criteria and 
improve consistency. A similar procedure was applied 
to full-text screening, with initial agreement also limited 
(Cohen’s kappa, κ = 0.26) but improved through discus -
sion and refinement of the criteria, ensuring reliability in 
the final selection of studies. A revised inter-rater agree -
ment was not calculated, as its purpose in this context 
was to identify discrepancies rather than to quantify final 
agreement. For this study we defined blended care as a 
digital health intervention combined with at least one 
in-person instance between health care provider and 
patient.
Data charting process
The standard Covidence data extraction template was 
adapted for the purpose of this scoping review. The 
adapted extraction form was pilot tested with three 
studies, based on which the form was modified to its 
definitive version. The extracted data covered: (1) Study 
characteristics (e.g. publication type); (2) general study 
and participants characteristics (e.g. study design, coun -
try); (3) mode of intervention delivery intervention if 
applicable (e.g. delivery platform); (4) qualitative data 
methods (e.g. interview type); (5) evaluation results (e.g. 
barriers and facilitators; and (6) other information (e.g. 
key conclusion of study authors). The data extraction 
was conducted by CL and then checked for accuracy and 
completeness by NB. Any discrepancies were resolved 
through consultation and consensus.

Synthesis of results
In line with the exploratory nature of this review, no for -
mal risk of bias assessment was performed. Given the 
predominantly qualitative nature of the included studies, 
findings were synthesized using a narrative approach. For 
consistency in reporting, the term digital health interven-
tion (DHI) was used throughout this scoping review to 
specifically refer to the digital component of the blended-
care interventions included. To identify the implementa -
tion determinants in a structured and systematic way, the 
Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research 
(CFIR) was used. Given the complexity of implementing 
digital health interventions, a framework that systemati -
cally addresses contextual, organisational, and individual 
influences was considered most appropriate. CFIR con -
sisted of five domains that can influence implementation. 
These domains include innovation (e.g. innovation type 
and adaptable components), outer setting (e.g. external 
influences), inner setting (e.g. influences within the set -
ting), individuals (e.g. characteristics and roles of the 
individuals involved) and implementation (e.g. activi -
ties and strategies used to implement the innovation) 
[39]. This framework provides a systematic method for 
understanding how different components interact and 
influence the overall success of DHI implementations. 
Relevant sentences addressing barriers and facilitators in 
the included studies were coded deductively according to 
the five CFIR domains and their constructs. Coding was 
conducted by CL and discussed with a second reviewer 
(NB), with any discrepancies or uncertainties resolved 
through discussion and consensus. No inductive cod -
ing was performed, as all relevant factors were captured 
within the existing CFIR framework.
Results
Search results
The search terms resulted in 2637 articles from three 
databases. Of these, 1105 (41.9%) were duplicates and 
were removed. There was initially a relatively low consen-
sus for the first 10% of the data. However, after discussing 
the discrepancies, particularly those arising from varying 
interpretations of terms, agreement improved consid -
erably. After screening titles and abstracts, 179 articles 
were selected for full-text reviews. Of these, 170 articles 
were excluded, predominantly because they did not have 
an in-person component. Eventually, nine studies were 
included in this scoping review (Fig. 1).
Study characteristics
The included studies were conducted across six differ -
ent countries; Sweden (n = 2) [45, 46], Spain (n = 2) [47, 
48], United Kingdom (n = 2) [38, 49], United States (n = 1) 
[50], Australia (n = 1) [51], the Netherlands (n = 1) [52].
Most studies (n = 4) focused on both healthcare pro -
fessionals and patients [45, 48–50], while two focused 
solely on healthcare professionals [51, 52]. Two studies 
included a broad range of health professionals, including 
GPs, nurses, healthcare assistants, administrative staff, 
and regional healthcare directors [38, 45]. Three studies 
included individuals at high risk of developing T2DM, 
based on the Finnish Diabetes Risk Score (FINDRISC) 
[46–48]. Of these, one study included participants with 
T2DM, pre-diabetes, and high-risk individuals [46], 
another focused on primary care professionals and high-
risk patients [48], and the third included only high-risk 
participants [47].
The DHIs evaluated across these studies varied in 
focus and implementation. Two studies [47, 48] assessed 
the same DHI, the DE-PLAN program, implemented in 
different Spanish regions; this intervention centred on 
education and structured contact between healthcare 
professionals and patients. Another study [52] evaluated 
a DHI offering individualised coaching and remote moni-
toring through telephone consultations. Two studies [46, 
50] focused on communication-enhancing DHIs such as 
video consultations to facilitate patient-provider interac -
tion. One study [49] investigated a remote monitoring 
intervention tracking health indicators, and another [38] 
explored an education-focused DHI aimed at improv -
ing diabetes and lifestyle knowledge among patients. 
Finally, two studies out of nine in this review used Theo -
ries, Models or Frameworks (TMF) to guide their imple -
mentation processes; Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, 
Implementation, Maintenance (RE-AIM) [47] and Nor -
malization Process Theory (NPT) [38].

Outer setting domain
Although the outer setting was not frequently mentioned 
in the studies [51, 52], several themes suggest that exter -
nal factors influence implementation of DHIs. Several 
outer setting barriers emerged. Liability concerns and 
perceived obligations to respond promptly to patient data 
were mentioned [51], reflecting the moral and legal pres -
sures that influence clinical decision-making. Regulatory 
requirements, particularly regarding data protection, 
were also aligning with policies and laws highlighting the 
broader challenge of aligning digital interventions with 
legal regulations [52]. Financing related issues further 
complicated implementation. Uncertainty about reim -
bursement procedures and the risk of uncompensated 
work were reported [51]. In contrast, some external fac -
tors functioned as facilitators. Support from larger organ-
isations was seen as an important facilitator for adoption, 
reflecting the relevance of partnerships and connections. 
Additionally, lack of reimbursement was not perceived as 
a limiting factor by some professionals [52], suggesting 
that financial barriers may be context dependent rather 
than universally limiting.
Inner setting domain
Healthcare professionals encountered a variety of chal -
lenges from the inner setting, when using DHI for 
patients [38, 45, 48–52]. Several barriers were identi -
fied. A major concern was that a patient monitoring app 
might create a false sense of security, as patients might 
think their doctor is constantly checking their data [51]. 
Professionals perceived that DHIs could either chal -
lenge the doctor–patient relationship. Although some 
professionals indicated that DHI could improve com -
munication [52], the majority believed that face-to-face 
care remained essential for tailoring information to the 
patient and ensuring understanding [51, 52]. Workload 
was another important factor. Increased workload was 
described as a barrier to implementation [48, 49]. How-
ever, under certain conditions workload was perceived 
as acceptable, as others considered a temporary rise in 
workload acceptable during the learning phase [51]. This 
suggests that workload may be function as a facilitator 
when it supports adaptation and learning, but becomes 
a barrier when the increase is substantial or prolonged. 
To minimize this barrier and enhance compatibility, it is 
therefore important that DHI be integrated with existing 
workflows, systems, and processes [38, 51, 52].
In addition, available resources barriers were reported. 
Concerns about adoption costs [49, 52], were intertwined 
with practical issues such as limited staff, insufficient 
space and unstable internet connections [50]. Imple -
mentation was further complicated by role uncertainty, 
highlighting that clear definition of staff responsibilities 
is crucial for smooth adoption and to prevent procedural 
barriers [38], which align with structural characteristics. 
Several facilitators were also identified within the inner 
setting. Access to knowledge and information, particu -
larly though training of healthcare professionals was seen 
as essential [38] and described in several studies [46–48]. 
However, in practice, training needs to be relevant and 
of sufficient duration. Long online modules with limited 
follow-up acted as a barriers, as they hindered healthcare 
professionals from fully engaging with the technology 
[45, 46].
Individuals’ domain
The roles and characteristics of individuals can influence 
the implementation of digital health innovations [47–49, 
51, 52]. Adoption was hindered by digital divide related 
barriers. These occurred at both patient and system lev -
els, including limited digital skills and health literacy as 
well as technological accessibility, especially for elderly 
patients [51, 52], reflecting capability constraints. Profes -
sionals noted that DHI tended to motivate more already 
independent patients, while vulnerable patients required 
additional support [51], indicating that DHI may inad -
vertently increase inequalities if additional guidance is 
not provided Personalized care remained essential, as 
online options were often perceived as impersonal [52] 
and less effective in ensuring patient understanding 
[51]. Patient preferences also influenced engagement, 
for example, many participants favoured group-based 
interventions over individual or mixed formats [47] In 
contrast, several facilitators were also described. DHI 
were appreciated for enhancing patient autonomy and 
supporting remote care [52]. In addition, knowing 
that healthcare professionals could see their self-mon -
itoring data motivated behaviour change by patients 
through a sense of supportive oversight [49]. Successful

Implementation process domain
Several studies have reported barriers and facilitators of 
the implementation process [38, 47, 48, 52]. Two of the 
included studies applied implementation frameworks to 
guide the process, namely Reach, Effectiveness, Adop -
tion, Implementation, Maintenance (RE-AIM) [47] and 
Normalization Process Theory (NPT) [38], reflecting 
structured approaches to planning and assessing con -
text. Key challenges include discontinuity of initial efforts 
and limited sustained commitment from professionals, 
which can impede integration [48]. For example, in the 
HeLP-Diabetes intervention, professionals recognized 
the value of the program but struggled to allocate suffi -
cient time and effort for its effective implementation [38], 
indicating that perceived value alone was insufficient to 
ensure sustained engagement In contrast, multiple facili -
tators were described. Providing healthcare professionals 
with opportunities to reflect on the intervention’s value 
through patient feedback was perceived to potentially 
increase their engagement [38], corresponding to reflect -
ing and evaluating implementation Moreover, involv -
ing patients in decisions regarding the use of DHIs can 
enhance compatibility with user needs and preferences 
[52], thereby facilitating alignment with the needs of 
innovation recipients Finally, early and iterative imple -
mentation, preferably before developing the development 
phase, can promote adoption, allowing end users to be 
involved at an early stage [38], illustrating the impor -
tance of small implementation cycles (doing) and ongo -
ing adapting.
Discussion
This study aimed to provide a comprehensive overview 
of the barriers and facilitators involved in implementing 
blended care interventions for lifestyle management in 
adults with overweight, or T2DM.
It is notable that the term “blended care” was not 
always explicitly used in the included studies, but all 
included a combination of a form of DHI with at least one 
physical appointment between a healthcare professionals 
and an adult patient. This variability in terminology con -
tributed to the initially low inter-rater reliability during 
study selection, which was resolved through discussion 
and consensus. No clear differences were found between 
countries regarding the implementation of these DHIs. 
Importantly, the absence of a standardized definition of 
“blended care” may in itself constitute an implementa -
tion barrier. Rather than constituting a direct barrier in 
clinical practice, the lack of a standardized definition 
primarily complicates the accumulation and transfer 
of implementation knowledge. When terminology var -
ies, evidence becomes fragmented, making it more dif -
ficult to develop theoretically informed and empirically 
grounded implementation strategies. This highlights the 
importance of using consistent terminology and clear 
definitions when describing DHIs and blended care. Uni -
form language would improve comparability between 
studies, support knowledge transfer, and help address 
shared implementation challenges across different con -
texts. Moreover, it is notable that all of the included stud-
ies were conducted in primary care, and none focused on 
secondary care. This may reflect the current emphasis on 
prevention and early intervention for T2DM in primary 
care but also raises questions about the applicability and 
integration of DHIs in more specialized or hospital-based 
care settings. The DHIs examined serve different roles 
within blended-care settings, including education, coach-
ing, remote monitoring, and communication with health-
care professionals. These varying functions influence 
patient engagement and the interpretation of outcomes. 
For example, coaching and monitoring interventions 
often require active patient involvement and continuous 
provider support, whereas communication tools may pri-
marily facilitate access and interaction. Recognising these 
distinct functions is key to tailoring implementation 
strategies and ensuring effective integration of DHIs into 
existing care pathways.
Unlike recent reviews focusing more on the effec -
tiveness of DHI [18, 53–56], this study contributes by 
identifying specific barriers and facilitators for its imple -
mentation. However, interestingly, most of the included 
studies focused on the effectiveness and design features 
of the interventions, while implementation-related 
aspects were often not reported. This implies that imple -
mentation to date still does not receive the attention it 
deserves. By structuring findings using CFIR, this study 
provides a comprehensive perspective on the implemen -
tation of blended care interventions. The results were 
synthesized narratively, which provided a descriptive 
overview of facilitators and barriers but does not quan -
tify the prevalence of specific factors. After all, early 
consideration of implementation increases the chances 
of developing an intervention that can be more widely 
applied and sustained in practice [57]. In line with ear -
lier implications of the study by Lee [58] and Ahmed 
[40], results of the current review suggest that successful 
Page 10 of 14Lansink et al. Implementation Science Communications            (2026) 7:84 
adoption of DHI technologies requires active involve -
ment of both patients and healthcare professionals in the 
development and implementation stages [38]. Impor -
tantly, to strengthen the evidence base, future trials and 
evaluations should prospectively include implementa -
tion outcomes to reflect alignment of implementation 
strategies and adopt standardized reporting frameworks 
to allow cross-comparisons between studies. This will 
improve transparency, enable knowledge transfer, and 
provide actionable insights into the factors that influence 
implementation.
To support structured thinking on implementation, 
theories, models and frameworks (TMF) such as the 
CFIR [39] and Non-adoption, Abandonment, Scale-up, 
Spread and Sustainability (NASSS) [59], RE-AIM [60] 
or NPT [61] offer valuable guidance for the implemen -
tation process. They provide a structured approach to 
understanding the factors that influence implementation 
outcomes [62], including the involvement of end users. 
The use of CFIR allowed us to capture the complexity 
inherent to digital health implementation by systemati -
cally identifying determinants across contextual, organi -
sational, and individuals levels. However, the current 
scoping review found that only two studies reported on 
applying a TMF to guide their implementation. This is 
in line with a recent review [63] that found that only 23 
studies out of 252 studies reported using an implemen -
tation science TMF, which underscores the gap between 
theory and practice. Adopting an iterative approach, 
guided by frameworks, ensures the strategy is all-encom -
passing and can adapt to emerging needs and challenges, 
ensuring a more effective and responsive implementation 
process. For example, what is the most suitable strategy 
for implementation will depend on the specific needs 
of the target population and the specific context of the 
implementation setting [38, 64]. Using TMFs in this way 
can support the early identification of such needs and 
contextual preconditions [65]. For example, healthcare 
professionals working in teams may benefit more from 
strategies tailored to collaborative environments [66].
For successful implementation, it is important to 
address personal characteristics of the target group. For 
example, the GPs in this review assumed that older age 
is a barrier to using DHI. Interestingly, a study by Her -
manus [67] showed that older people have a higher 
behavioural intention to use DHI. Nevertheless, the 
authors of that study also noted that older people use 
DHI less than younger people, which could be explained 
by the lack of digital skills among older people. Similarly, 
the National Institute for Public Health and the Envi -
ronment (RIVM) confirmed that digital divide among 
Dutch elderly remains a concern [68]. These findings 
suggest that implementation strategies should focus not 
on excluding older adults, but on strengthening digital 
literacy and providing tailored support to facilitate DHI 
uptake. At the same time, healthcare professionals rec -
ognize the benefits of DHIs, especially in its potential 
to personalize patient care [45, 46, 52]. They emphasize 
that in-person care remains essential for maintaining the 
patient-doctor relationship [51, 52, 69], but suggest that 
DHI could actually strengthen this patient-doctor rela -
tion by creating a sense of accessibility [51]. This further 
supports the value of integrating DHI into traditional 
care. This seems to particularly hold true for individu -
als with lower digital literacy. A blended care approach 
ensures that individuals who struggle with digital tools 
still receive adequate support while benefiting from the 
advantages of DHI [70].
Next, organisational considerations must be taken into 
consideration for maintaining patient engagement in self-
monitoring DHIs, which remains a challenging part of 
implementation [71]. Patient experiences with DHI were 
mixed, as they reflected both potential benefits and chal -
lenges. While self-monitoring data can empower indi -
viduals, it can also be perceived as burdensome [72] and 
leading to negative emotional responses to glucose fluc -
tuations [73]. This raises broader concerns about the psy-
chological impact of continuous monitoring. For some, 
the belief that their healthcare professionals monitors 
their data serves as a motivating factor for adherence [41]. 
However, this reliance also raises ethical and legal ques -
tions regarding professional liability and the responsibil -
ity to intervene in cases of abnormal readings [51]. These 
complexities highlight the need for clear guidelines defin-
ing roles, responsibilities [41] and data security [40, 41].
In addition to that, from an organisational perspective, 
DHIs must integrate seamlessly with existing healthcare 
systems [51, 52] to enable sustainable implementation. 
This includes for example, embedding DHIs within elec -
tronic health records, aligning them with routine clinical 
workflows, and addressing reimbursement mechanisms. 
Successfully integrating blended care into routine prac -
tice can improve quality of care and contribute to a more 
personalized care system.
To overcome some of the identified implementation 
barriers and harness facilitators, it is highly advisable to 
invest in in-person training for healthcare professionals, 
ideally delivered before and during initial implementa -
tion with follow-up sessions as needed [38, 45, 64]. How-
ever, the effectiveness of training depends also on factors 
such as content, duration and facilitation of the training 
[74] and its attainability may be hindered by its associ -
ated costs [40]. A more structured approach to organ -
izing efficient training, tailored to the specific needs of 
both professionals and patients, can support and improve 
sustainability of a blended care process.

In summary, this scoping review provides guidance 
for structured thinking about and concrete steps to take 
to optimize implementation of lifestyle related blended 
care for patients with obesity, overweight and/or T2DM. 
In considering concrete steps to take, using TMFs can 
support researchers and developers in making sure they 
address all relevant topics for implementation and ena -
bles cross-comparisons between implementation factors 
and processes in different projects. Furthermore, reduc -
ing organisational burden, ensuring usability through 
user-centred design, and embedding blended care within 
routine workflows and regulatory structures, together 
can strengthen long-term adoption.
Limitations
As with any scientific study, this study faced some limita-
tions. The main one being the relatively low agreement 
between the first and second screeners for the initial 
10% of the data. This discrepancy was attributable to the 
initially unclear interpretation of blended care and was 
resolved after discussions that led to the extended defini -
tion of blended care as described in the methods section. 
In addition, given the explorative nature of this paper, 
the authors placed emphasis on scoping the breadth of 
this field of study, rather than evaluating the quality of 
its data. In line with that, another potential limitation of 
this scoping review is its reliance on a narrative summary, 
which inherently involves subjective interpretations. 
Efforts were made to reduce bias by using a structured 
extraction form and regularly discussing interpretations 
with the second reviewer. Finally, all included articles 
unintentionally focused exclusively on primary care set -
tings. This may limit the generalizability of the findings 
to other healthcare settings, such as secondary or tertiary 
care. Therefore, further research into other healthcare 
settings is needed.
Conclusion
Blended care and digital health interventions offer oppor-
tunities to enhance personalisation, patient involvement, 
and continuity of care. However, challenges such as high 
implementation costs, workflow integration issues, and 
the digital divide highlight that sustainable implementa -
tion requires addressing overarching domains rather than 
isolated factors.
At the same time, there is a notable gap in the litera -
ture on real world implementation, with most studies 
focusing on effectiveness. This underscores the impor -
tance of developing strategies that support adoption and 
sustainability, while ensuring uniform terminology and 
understanding of blended care and DHIs. Healthcare 
organisations can reduce organisational burden, policy -
makers can support regulatory alignment, developers can 
prioritize user-centred design, and healthcare profession-
als can integrate DHI into routine practice and support 
patients in its use. Ultimately, successful integration of 
DHIs depends on critical evaluation and, where neces -
sary, redesign of care workflows to ensure DHI are mean-
ingfully embedded in blended care.
