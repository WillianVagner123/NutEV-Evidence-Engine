Abstract
Background: 
Obesity has emerged as a global pandemic with profound 
implications for respiratory health. The complex interplay 
between excessive adiposity and pulmonary function encompasses 
mechanical, metabolic, and inflammatory pathways that 
significantly impact morbidity and mortality.
Objectives: 
This comprehensive review synthesizes current evidence on 
obesity-related respiratory disorders, examining 
pathophysiological mechanisms, clinical phenotypes, 
epidemiological trends, and therapeutic considerations across the 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
spectrum of respiratory diseases. This review provides a novel 
integrative framework unifying mechanical, inflammatory, and 
metabolic mechanisms across all major obesity-related respiratory 
conditions, with emphasis on clinically actionable phenotyping 
and emerging pharmacological therapies.
Methods: 
We conducted a narrative review of peer-reviewed literature 
(PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, and Cochrane Database, January 
2000 – December 2024, using pre-specified MeSH terms; study 
selection and quality assessment followed SANRA guidelines  
focusing on the relationship between obesity and respiratory 
disorders including asthma, obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), 
obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS), chronic obstructive 
pulmonary disease (COPD), respiratory infections, and lung 
cancer.
Results: 
Obesity profoundly affects respiratory mechanics through a right-
shift of the static volume-pressure relationship of the chest wall,

reducing FRC and ERV without a true restrictive pattern, altered 
respiratory muscle function, and systemic inflammation. Asthma 
prevalence increases with body mass index , demonstrating 
distinct phenotypes including atopic, insulin-resistant, 
dyslipidemic, and non-Th2 neutrophilic subtypes. OSA affects 22% 
of men and 17% of women, with obesity being the principal 
modifiable risk factor. OHS occurs in 8–20% of obese patients with 
sleep-disordered breathing, characterized by daytime 
hypercapnia (arising from impaired neuromuscular ventilatory 
drive and progressive nocturnal hypoventilation) and increased 
cardiovascular mortality. Paradoxically, obesity appears 
protective in advanced COPD by reducing FRC, thereby increasing 
inspiratory capacity and limiting dynamic pulmonary 
hyperinflation, and ARDS—the so-called obesity paradox—while 
conferring increased risk in COVID-19 pneumonitis. In lung 
cancer, obesity demonstrates complex relationships with risk and 
prognosis that vary by sex, smoking status, and disease stage.
Conclusions: 
Obesity-related respiratory disorders represent a multifaceted 
clinical challenge requiring integrated multidisciplinary

pathophysiological mechanisms is essential for personalized 
therapeutic strategies addressing both weight management and 
respiratory-specific interventions. Emerging pharmacological

airway inflammation.

1. Introduction
 
Obesity is a rapidly growing global health problem with significant 
consequences for respiratory health. In addition to its metabolic 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
and cardiovascular effects, excess adiposity alters pulmonary 
physiology through mechanical loading of the chest wall, reduced 
some lung volumes, impaired respiratory muscle function, and 
systemic inflammation. These changes contribute to increased 
respiratory symptoms, functional limitation, and adverse clinical 
outcomes across a broad range of pulmonary diseases.(1)
The spectrum of obesity-related respiratory pathology is broad. 
Obesity is not a direct cause of COPD or ARDS; however, it 
modifies their clinical course and through the obesity paradox may 
confer a survival advantage in severe disease. In contrast, obesity 
is the principal modifiable risk factor for OSA and OHS, is strongly 
associated with asthma — particularly non-atopic phenotypes — 
and worsens outcomes in respiratory infections including COVID-
19 pneumonitis.
What distinguishes this review from prior works is its integrative, 
cross-disease mechanistic framework. Whereas previous reviews 
have addressed individual obesity-related respiratory conditions 
in isolation, we unify mechanical, adipokine-mediated, and 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
metabolic pathways across asthma, OSA, OHS, COPD, ARDS, and 
lung cancer. We further introduce updated evidence on GLP-1 
receptor agonist therapies and their respiratory implications, and 
provide a phenotypic classification with direct clinical 
management guidance.
2- Methods
A systematic literature search was conducted in 
PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, and the Cochrane Database of 
Systematic Reviews from January 2000 to December 2024. Search 
terms included: 'obesity AND respiratory diseases,' 'obstructive 
sleep apnea AND obesity,' 'obesity hypoventilation syndrome,' 
'asthma AND obesity phenotype,' 'COPD AND obesity paradox,' 
'ARDS AND obesity,' 'lung cancer AND BMI,' 'GLP-1 agonist AND 
respiratory function,' and related MeSH terms. Inclusion criteria: 
English-language peer-reviewed original articles, systematic 
reviews, and meta-analyses in human adult populations. Exclusion 
criteria: case reports, non-English publications, studies 
exclusively in pediatric populations (unless specifically relevant to 
mechanism), and pre-2000 publications except for seminal

Obesity-related alterations in the respiratory system occur at the 
morphological level and are closely intertwined with physiological 
disturbances. Structural changes lead to abnormal respiratory 
physiology, while physiological derangements can, in turn, 
promote or exacerbate morphological abnormalities. At the 
macroscopic level, excess fat deposition around the thoracic wall 
and abdominal cavity is a prominent feature.
Dysanapsis is another frequent finding in the lungs of has been 
demonstrated in overweight and obese children (1). It refers to a 
disproportion between airway size and lung volume, whereby the 
airways are relatively small for the amount of lung parenchyma. 
This imbalance is typically associated with preserved forced 
expiratory volume in one second (FEV₁), increased forced vital 
capacity (FVC), and a reduced FEV₁/FVC ratio(2) . In addition, 
airway wall thickening due to increased adipose tissue has been 
described. Experimental studies in porcine lungs demonstrated 
that adipose tissue extends along the conducting airways from the 
trachea to the subsegmental bronchi(3). Consistently, Elliott and 
colleagues showed that the area of adipose tissue within the 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
conducting airways of human lungs correlates positively with body 
mass index(4).
The primary mechanical consequence of obesity on the respiratory 
system is a right-shift of the static volume-pressure relationship of 
the chest wall. The weight of adipose tissue accumulated around 
— not within — the thoracic wall reduces its outward elastic recoil 
at low lung volumes and increases its inward elastic recoil at high 
lung volumes. This shifts the relaxation volume of the respiratory 
system — the functional residual capacity (FRC) — toward a lower 
lung volume. Since residual volume (RV) does not change 
appreciably in obesity, expiratory reserve volume (ERV) is 
consequently reduced. This is not a static volume effect on the 
lung itself, but a consequence of the altered chest wall mechanics. 
The chest wall static compliance is roughly normal or only mildly 
reduced, but pleural pressure is less negative at every lung 
volume, increasing the load on the inspiratory muscles.(5,6). As 
the degree of obesity increases, ERV declines in an exponential 
manner and shows an inverse relationship with other static lung 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
volumes and capacities(5,7). A comparable pattern has also been 
demonstrated for end-expiratory lung volume.(8)
Total lung capacity (TLC) is generally maintained within normal 
limits; however, in individuals with morbid obesity, TLC may 
decrease by as much as 20%(5), producing a restrictive ventilatory 
pattern. Accordingly, when a reduced TLC is observed in patients 
with a body mass index up to 40 kg/m², clinicians should consider 
the presence of an alternative or coexisting respiratory disorder, 
as obesity alone is unlikely to account for such marked impairment 
in lung volumes.
Alterations in static lung volumes occur much more commonly in 
individuals with an abdominal (android) pattern of obesity than in 
those with a gynoid fat distribution, largely because of the greater 
mechanical effect on diaphragmatic motion (9). Vital capacity 
(VC), forced vital capacity (FVC), and forced expiratory volume in 
one second (FEV₁) are typically mildly reduced in people with 
obesity, which results in a normal or near-normal FEV₁/FVC 
ratio(6,7,10)
In contrast, the mid-expiratory flow rate (FEF₂₅–₇₅% of FVC) may 
be markedly decreased (7), reflects reduced lung volumes (FVC)

with proportionate decrements in FEV1, maintaining a normal

remodeling secondary to adipokine-mediated inflammation is 
retained but appropriately qualified as speculative. Diffusing 
capacity is usually preserved or even increased, consistent with 
the enhanced pulmonary blood flow characteristic of obesity(11).
-Chest Wall Mechanics
The effects of obesity on lung volumes are largely driven by the 
influence of chest compliance. In individuals with obesity, chest 
wall compliance is reduced because excess adipose tissue over the 
chest and abdomen mechanically restricts chest wall expansion 
and limits diaphragmatic movement(11,12)
Furthermore, the obesity-related reduction in functional residual 
capacity (FRC) may increase alveolar surface tension, which 
further impairs lung compliance(13). Nevertheless, evidence 
suggests that decreased lung compliance plays a smaller role 
compared with the dominant effect of reduced thoracic (chest 
wall) compliance(14).

-Diaphragmatic Function
Accumulation of adipose tissue around the thoracic and abdominal 
cavities raises pleural pressure, thereby restricting rib cage 
movement and diaphragmatic excursion. Consequently, the work 
of breathing increases, while the elastic energy available for 
passive exhalation declines. 
Studies have reported variable findings regarding respiratory 
muscle strength: some demonstrate values within normal limits, 
while others show increased strength in morbidly obese 
patients(15,16). In contrast, respiratory muscle endurance—the 
capacity to sustain repeated contractions against resistance—is 
consistently reduced compared with normal-weight 
individuals.(17)
Both inspiratory and expiratory times appear shortened in obesity, 
contributing to an increased respiratory rate from approximately 
10–12 breaths per minute in normal subjects to 15.3–21 breaths 
per minute in obese individuals (18). This tachypnea compensates 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
for the reduced tidal volume, resulting in normal or increased 
minute ventilation, particularly in the supine position.(19,20)
-Upper Airway Effects
Airway narrowing with a consequent rise in resistance is common 
in obesity, even when the FEV₁/FVC ratio remains preserved(21) . 
Body plethysmography has shown a progressive increase in 
airway resistance with higher BMI, while forced oscillation studies 
demonstrated nearly double the resistance in obese individuals 
compared with controls(22,23).
The increased thickness of the airway smooth muscle layer in 
obesity has been attributed to persistently low functional residual 
capacity (FRC), which causes continuous smooth muscle 
shortening and adaptation for greater strength at shorter 
lengths(24).
 In addition, obese subjects exhibited increased ΔXrs (the 
difference between inspiratory and expiratory reactance), 
independent of reduced FRC, indicating the presence of additional 
mechanisms contributing to airflow limitation.(25)

The mechanisms underlying airway narrowing in obesity can be 
broadly divided into mechanical factors and airway 
hyperresponsiveness related to a pro-inflammatory state that will 
be discussed in the following section .
3.2 Inflammatory and Metabolic Pathways
-Systemic Inflammation
Obesity is characterized by a systemic pro-inflammatory state, 
marked by elevated leukocytes, cytokines, and chemokines (26), 
which contributes to several complications, including 
cardiovascular disease (27), glucose intolerance (28), and 
malignancy (29). This inflammatory milieu may also underlie 
airway hyperresponsiveness in obese individuals. Since airway 
eosinophil and neutrophil levels are not significantly elevated, the 
inflammation appears primarily systemic rather than localized to 
the airways (21).
Serum tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α levels are increased in 
obesity. TNF-α receptors are present on airway smooth muscle, 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
and in vitro exposure enhances smooth muscle contractility (30). 
Leptin, elevated in obesity, exerts pro-inflammatory effects 
including promotion of Th2 polarization, enhancement of 
eosinophil survival, and upregulation of IL-4 and IL-13 in airway 
epithelial cells. These effects promote bronchial 
hyperresponsiveness and airway remodeling. (31).
-Adipokine Dysregulation
         
Adiponectin, another hormone produced by adipose tissue, is 
typically reduced in obese individuals (32). Because adiponectin 
inhibits TNF-α and exerts anti-inflammatory effects by 
suppressing NF-κB signaling, as well as inhibiting mast cell 
activation, decreased adiponectin levels may enhance airway 
smooth muscle contractility. (33). Notably, bariatric surgery 
reduces airway hyperresponsiveness and is associated with 
increased serum adiponectin and decreased leptin levels (34). 
The leptin/adiponectin imbalance characteristic of obesity thus 
creates a pro-asthmatic milieu.

Obesity-related hypertrophy and hyperplasia of airway smooth 
muscle are mainly observed with coexisting asthma, where high 
mobility group box protein 1 (HMGB1) plays a key role. 
Intranuclear HMGB1 regulates gene transcription, whereas 
extracellular HMGB1 promotes inflammatory and immune 
responses. Its expression is significantly increased in the adipose 
tissue of obese individuals (35) and has been shown to enhance 
airway smooth muscle thickness in murine asthma models (36). 
This effect is mediated through stimulation of smooth muscle cell 
proliferation via RAGE receptor activation and downstream 
ERK/NF-κB signaling pathways (37). Additionally, HMGB1 
promotes epithelial–mesenchymal transition by reducing tight 
junction protein expression (38). Collectively, molecular pathways 
involving HMGB1, RAGE, and ERK/NF-κB have been implicated in 
adipokine-mediated airway remodeling; however, these 
mechanisms are primarily characterized in experimental (murine 
and in vitro) models and still require validation in human studies.

contribute to airway hyperresponsiveness in obesity and may be

3.3 Metabolic Dysfunction 
-Insulin Resistance and Hyperinsulinemia
           Insulin resistance in obesity leads to compensatory 
hyperinsulinemia, which exerts direct pro-contractile and pro-
fibrotic effects on the airways. Insulin stimulates airway smooth 
muscle proliferation and enhances bronchial hyperresponsiveness 
by increasing intracellular calcium signaling. Additionally, insulin 
resistance impairs nitric oxide–mediated vasodilation, 
contributing to pulmonary microvascular dysfunction. 
Hyperinsulinemia also upregulates insulin-like growth factor-1 
(IGF-1), promoting extracellular matrix deposition and airway 
remodeling. These mechanisms are particularly relevant in obese 
asthma phenotypes and may exacerbate airflow limitation and 
reduce responsiveness to inhaled corticosteroids.(40)

-Dyslipidemia
   
          Obesity-related dyslipidemia, characterized by elevated 
triglycerides, increased LDL cholesterol, and reduced HDL 
cholesterol, contributes to chronic lung disease through 
lipotoxicity and inflammation. Accumulation of oxidized lipids in 
pulmonary tissues activates macrophages and induces the release 
of pro-inflammatory mediators. Cholesterol crystals can stimulate 
the NLRP3 inflammasome, leading to IL-1β–mediated airway 
inflammation. Reduced HDL impairs its normal anti-oxidant and 
anti-inflammatory functions, further enhancing oxidative injury. 
These processes promote endothelial dysfunction, impaired 
alveolar repair, and accelerated decline in lung function, 
particularly in COPD and obesity-associated asthma.(41)
-Oxidative Stress
              Obesity is associated with increased production of 
reactive oxygen species (ROS) due to mitochondrial dysfunction,

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
chronic inflammation, and reduced antioxidant capacity. In the 
lungs, excessive ROS cause epithelial and endothelial injury, 
inactivation of antiproteases, and increased mucus production. 
Oxidative stress activates redox-sensitive transcription factors 
such as NF-κB, perpetuating inflammatory cascades. It also 
contributes to corticosteroid resistance by impairing histone 
deacetylase-2 (HDAC2) activity, a key mechanism in severe 
asthma and COPD. Persistent oxidative injury accelerates airway 
remodeling, emphysematous changes, and disease progression in 
chronic lung disorders.(42)
3.4 Effects on Ventilatory Control
     To maintain adequate tidal volume and alveolar ventilation for 
normal gas exchange, obese individuals require a greater 
neuromuscular respiratory central drive to sustain the inspiratory 
muscles against increased elastic and resistive workloads. In most 
obese individuals, this compensatory drive is sufficient. However, 
a subset — due to genetic or non-genetic factors including low 
growth hormone levels, reduced circulating leptin, or central 
leptin resistance — fails to mount an adequate ventilatory

response. This leads to progressive hypoventilation and CO2 
retention, initially during REM sleep, subsequently during NREM 
sleep, and ultimately when awake, culminating in established 
primary OHS. (18).
4- Obesity and Asthma: A Complex Relationship
4.1 Epidemiological Evidence
The strongest association between obesity and respiratory disease 
has been observed with asthma (43-45). Adult-onset asthma is 
more common in individuals with BMI >25 kg/m² compared with 
those of lower BMI (46). Asthma prevalence increases with rising 
BMI, with a 12.5% increase in individuals with BMI 25–29.9 kg/m² 
and a markedly higher prevalence in those with BMI ≥50 kg/m². 
Asthma severity is also greater in obese individuals, reflecting a 
dose-dependent relationship between obesity and asthma. Obesity 
further increases the likelihood of a persistent and severe asthma 
phenotype (47).
This association is independent of sociodemographic factors, 
physical activity, and dietary habits. One study identified a linear 
relationship between asthma severity and BMI(48).

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Two meta-analyses published in 2013 and 2019 examined the 
obese asthma phenotype and confirmed a positive association 
between asthma and childhood overweight or obesity (49,50). The 
first meta-analysis, including six prospective studies, reported a 
20% increase in asthma incidence among overweight children and 
a twofold higher risk in obese children, with a stronger association 
in boys before puberty (49). The second analysis, which included 
18 prospective studies, found a 20% increased risk in overweight 
children and a 40% increase in obese children (50).
Body mass index (BMI) is the most commonly used measure to 
assess weight status in adults and children because of its 
simplicity. In children, overweight is defined as BMI above the 
85th percentile and obesity as BMI above the 95th percentile (51). 
However, BMI does not differentiate between fat and lean mass or 
between subcutaneous and visceral fat, although fat distribution 
is a stronger predictor of asthma development (52).

-Atopic Phenotype
Early-onset asthma  EOA resembles allergic, eosinophilic Th2-
driven asthma and responds to corticosteroids. EOA is 
accompanied by greater airway obstruction and positively 
correlated with BMI. The patients had greater (airway 
hyperresponsiveness)  AHR and the risk was 4–6 fold higher for 
hospitalization.(53)
-Insulin-Resistant Phenotype
Typically late-onset and associated with metabolic dysfunction, 
this phenotype is marked by airway hyperresponsiveness and 
remodeling driven by hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance. 
Inflammation is often non-eosinophilic, and patients may exhibit 
reduced responsiveness to corticosteroids.(54) 
-Dyslipidemic Phenotype
Associated with abnormal lipid profiles, this phenotype involves 
lipotoxic airway inflammation mediated by oxidized lipids and 
macrophage activation. Oxidative stress and inflammasome

activation contribute to airflow limitation and poorer asthma

-Non-Th2 Inflammatory Phenotype
Late-onset nonallergic (LONA) asthma is characterized by 
Th1/Th17 responses and neutrophilic inflammation and poorly 
responds to corticosteroid treatment. It affected more women 
than men, was characterized by lower AHR, more persistent 
airway remodeling, poorer symptom control and more frequent 
exacerbations. (55)
4.3 Mechanistic Considerations
Mechanical and inflammatory mechanisms converge in obesity-
related asthma. Obese individuals breathe just above RV favoring 
the occurrence of tidal expiratory flow limitation at rest and near 
to the closing volume promoting the functional closure of 
dependent small airways (both phenomena occurring mainly in the 
supine position) which further impair airways function. 
Supporting this mechanism, interventions that increase lung 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
volume, such as continuous positive airway pressure, reduce 
airway reactivity in experimental models and asthma patients 
(56).
Visceral adipose tissue releases metabolic mediators and 
adipokines, especially leptin, which are elevated in obese 
asthmatics and correlate with airway reactivity. Although 
systemic inflammatory markers are increased, this phenotype is 
not always linked to greater airway inflammation, suggesting 
direct adipokine effects on airway structure and function.
Overall, mechanical restriction and adipose-derived inflammatory 
signaling explain the obesity–asthma relationship, though 
behavioral, hormonal, and genetic factors may also contribute 
(57).
4.4 Clinical Implications
         Asthma in obese patients is associated with more severe 
symptoms, poorer disease control, and increased exacerbations 
despite often modest airway inflammation. Reduced lung volumes,

contribute to disproportionate dyspnea and reduced quality of life.
Obese asthma is frequently less responsive to standard asthma 
therapies, particularly inhaled corticosteroids, highlighting the 
need for phenotype-based management. Addressing obesity and 
associated conditions through weight reduction and 
multidisciplinary care can significantly improve asthma outcomes 
and treatment responsiveness.
5. Obesity Hypoventilation Syndrome
5.1 Definition and Diagnostic Criteria
Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS) is characterized by 
obesity (body mass index ≥30 kg/m²), sleep-disordered breathing, 
and daytime hypercapnia (arterial PaCO₂ ≥45 mmHg at sea level) 
during wakefulness, without an alternative neuromuscular, 
mechanical, or metabolic cause of hypoventilation (58). About 90% 
of patients with OHS have obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), defined

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
by an apnoea–hypopnoea index (AHI) ≥5 events/h, and nearly 70% 
present with severe OSA (AHI ≥30 events/h) (59).
5.2 Epidemiology and Risk Factors
Obesity hypoventilation syndrome occurs in about 8–20% of obese 
patients with sleep-disordered breathing and is strongly linked to 
severe obesity (BMI ≥35 kg/m²). Risk rises with central adiposity, 
male sex, older age, and coexisting obstructive sleep apnea. Other 
contributors include reduced lung volumes, leptin resistance, 
physical inactivity, and cardiopulmonary disease. OHS is often 
underrecognized, frequently misdiagnosed as COPD or heart 
failure, resulting in delayed treatment and higher morbidity and 
mortality (60).
5.3 Pathophysiology
-Mechanical Load
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
In OHS, the increased mechanical load from thoraco-abdominal 
adiposity results in elevated airway resistance and respiratory 
muscle inefficiency. These changes increase airway resistance, 
may cause dependent airway closure leading to ventilation-
perfusion mismatch and basal atelectasis, and — by favoring tidal 
expiratory flow limitation — may generate intrinsic positive end-
expiratory pressure [PEEPi]. Patients with obesity hypoventilation 
syndrome show greater impairment in respiratory mechanics and 
muscle function than similarly obese individuals without OHS, 
resulting in increased work of breathing and higher ventilatory 
demand (61).
-Impaired Ventilatory Drive      
Most obese individuals maintain normal CO₂ levels by increasing 
respiratory drive to offset the higher breathing workload. When 
this compensation fails, hypoventilation occurs, initially during 
REM sleep, when muscle atonia increases reliance on 
diaphragmatic function and central drive. In obesity 
hypoventilation syndrome, mechanical diaphragmatic restriction 
and impaired central drive cause REM-related hypoventilation.

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Leptin resistance contributes by reducing ventilatory stimulation 
and promoting cardiometabolic dysfunction. A typical OHS 
phenotype includes morbid obesity, severe mechanical 
impairment, marked hypercapnia, and prominent REM 
hypoventilation, often responsive to noninvasive ventilation (62).
-Sleep-Disordered Breathing
            
Obesity-related physiological changes worsen during supine 
sleep, explaining the high prevalence of obstructive sleep apnea 
in obese individuals. Fat accumulation around the upper airway 
and reduced lung volumes narrow the pharynx and increase 
airway collapsibility, promoting obstruction(63). Nocturnal rostral 
fluid shift from the legs to the neck further constricts the airway, 
especially in fluid overload states.
In obesity hypoventilation syndrome, obstructive events are often 
prolonged and poorly compensated because of impaired central 
respiratory drive, causing nocturnal carbon dioxide retention and 
daytime hypoventilation (64). A distinct OHS phenotype—marked 
by predominant OSA without REM-related hypoventilation or

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
respiratory muscle dysfunction, usually in less severely obese 
patients—generally responds well to continuous positive airway 
pressure therapy(65).
5.4 Diagnosis and Severity Grading
The American Academy of Sleep Medicine defines adult sleep 
hypoventilation as PaCO₂ >55 mmHg for over 10 minutes, or a rise 
>10 mmHg from awake levels to >50 mmHg for the same 
duration. Although this reflects daytime abnormality, nocturnal 
polysomnography or respiratory polygraphy is required to assess 
sleep-related hypoventilation and distinguish obstructive from 
non-obstructive patterns, guiding appropriate positive airway 
pressure therapy.
The European Respiratory Society also proposed an OHS severity 
classification, identifying early disease by elevated serum 
bicarbonate and/or sleep hypoventilation, and severe disease by 
daytime hypercapnia with cardiovascular and metabolic 
comorbidities (62).

5.5 Treatment and Outcomes
          First-line treatment includes positive airway pressure 
therapy, with continuous positive airway pressure for patients 
with predominant obstructive sleep apnea and noninvasive 
ventilation for those with persistent hypercapnia. Weight 
reduction through lifestyle modification or bariatric surgery is the 
only disease-modifying intervention and significantly improves 
ventilatory function and survival. Untreated OHS is associated 
with high cardiovascular morbidity and mortality; however, early 
recognition and appropriate therapy markedly improve gas 
exchange, quality of life, and long-term outcomes.
6. Obstructive Sleep Apnea
6.1 Epidemiology and Risk Factors
Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), defined by an apnea–hypopnea 
index ≥5, showed a mean prevalence of 22% (9–37%) in men and 
17% (4–50%) in women across eleven epidemiological studies 
(1993–2013). OSA with excessive daytime sleepiness affected 6% 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
of men and 4% of women. Prevalence rose over time, reaching 
37% in men and 50% in women in later studies (2008–2013). 
Overall, OSA is more common in men and increases with age and 
obesity(66).
Excessive daytime sleepiness is considered the main symptom of 
obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), yet only some individuals with AHI 
>5 report it, and one study found no association between sleep 
apnea and daytime sleepiness in women(66).
Obesity is a major risk factor for snoring and OSA, with most 
affected patients being overweight(67). Weight reduction through 
caloric restriction or bariatric surgery decreases OSA severity, 
while higher BMI increases disease risk; a 10% weight gain raises 
OSA risk six-fold(68). Other risk factors include male sex, older 
age, craniofacial structure, increased neck circumference, and 
comorbidities such as insulin resistance and hypothyroidism. 
Weight gain worsens OSA, whereas weight loss significantly 
reduces disease burden.
6.2 Pathophysiology
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
            
Obesity contributes to OSA mainly through two mechanisms: 1) 
fat deposition in the anterior neck physically narrows the airway 
during sleep, and 2) reduced FRC decreases tracheal tug and 
upper airways stiffness, increasing their collapsibility (69). 
OSA-related poor sleep may further raise BMI by lowering serum 
leptin, an appetite suppressant(70), which can increase appetite 
and promote weight gain(71).
6.3 Cardiovascular and Metabolic Consequences
Obesity-related OSA causes intermittent hypoxia, sympathetic 
activation, and systemic inflammation, raising risks of 
hypertension, coronary disease, heart failure, stroke, and 
arrhythmias. It also promotes insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and 
type 2 diabetes, with obesity and OSA together amplifying 
cardiometabolic risk(72).
6.4 Treatment Considerations

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
          CPAP therapy remains the gold standard for OSA. Weight 
reduction through lifestyle intervention, bariatric surgery, and 
emerging pharmacological therapies effectively reduces apnea-
hypopnea index [AHI], oxygen desaturation burden, and daytime 
symptom burden, and reduces cardiovascular risk. Adjunctive 
therapies include positional therapy, oral appliances in selected 
patients, and management of contributing comorbidities. A 
multidisciplinary approach integrating sleep medicine, 
respiratory care, and metabolic management is essential for 
optimal outcomes.(73)
7. Obesity and Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease: The 
Obesity Paradox
7.1 Prevalence and Relationship
         The interaction between obesity and COPD is less dose 
responsive. Although the prevalence of COPD does increase with 
increasing BMI , the severity of COPD adds complexity. In a study 
from the Netherlands, among patients with Global Initiative for

Chronic Obstructive Lung Disease (GOLD) stage 1 and 2 COPD the 
prevalence of obesity was 16–24%, whereas in patients with GOLD 
stage 4 COPD the prevalence of obesity was 6%. This was 
compared with a national prevalence of 10% (74, 75)
7.2 The Obesity Paradox
Studies of mechanisms explaining the interaction between COPD 
and obesity are scarce, aside from sleep-disordered breathing. 
Both impact pulmonary mechanics and ventilatory demand, likely 
amplifying each other. An “obesity paradox” is reported in COPD: 
increased BMI appears protective against all-cause mortality. 
Annual FEV1 decline in men was lower in obese versus non-obese 
COPD patients.(76). This reflects the link between low BMI and 
mortality. The paradox varies with COPD phenotypes: in advanced 
COPD, obesity is protective, while in mild COPD, it may increase 
cardiovascular risk, a greater mortality contributor than low 
BMI.(77)

Obesity may protect in COPD through the “obesity paradox.” 
Greater nutritional reserves buffer catabolic stress during 
exacerbations, and preserved skeletal and respiratory muscle 
supports ventilation, exercise capacity, and resilience in advanced 
disease. Earlier presentation, treatment tolerance, and intensive 
management may also help.(78)
Clinically, obesity poses a dual challenge: it can worsen dyspnea, 
reduce lung volumes, and limit activity, yet may improve prognosis 
in advanced COPD by lowering FRC, so increasing inspiratory 
capacity and limiting dynamic pulmonary hyperinflation.(78)
7.4 Clinical Implications
          The interaction between obesity and COPD has important 
clinical consequences. Clinical management should therefore 
focus on body composition rather than BMI alone, aiming to 
preserve muscle mass while avoiding excess fat accumulation. 
Individualized strategies incorporating pulmonary rehabilitation, 
nutritional optimization, and careful weight management are

essential to balance symptom burden with potential protective 
effects of obesity in COPD.
8. Obesity, Respiratory Infections, and ARDS
8.1 Viral Respiratory Infections
Although obesity induces a systemic pro-inflammatory state, its 
link to respiratory infections was unclear before COVID-19 due to 
conflicting data. Animal studies of influenza showed obesity 
weakens antiviral responses (79,80), aligning with observations 
made during H1N1 influenza that obesity increases disease 
severity (81-83), ICU admission, and death (84) . COVID-19 
confirmed obesity as a poor prognostic factor, raising risks of 
pneumonitis (85), hospitalization, and mortality(86).
Multiple mechanisms link obesity to COVID-19, notably its effect 
on the angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) receptor. ACE2 
serves as the entry point for SARS-CoV-2 via its spike protein(87). 
Present on lung epithelial cells and adipocytes, ACE2 expression 
is increased in obese individuals, facilitating viral entry(88).
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Adipocytes can serve as reservoirs and replication sites for SARS-
CoV-2(89), potentially prolonging viral shedding, as seen with 
influenza(90). Obesity also amplifies COVID-19 severity via 
interleukin-6 (IL-6) (91), a key risk factor, since adipose tissue 
secretes IL-6 (92). Additionally, leptin, overexpressed in obesity, 
promotes pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-α and IL-6, 
contributing to the cytokine storm linked to morbidity and 
mortality(93,94). Other molecular mechanisms are still under 
investigation
In summary, obesity creates a pro-inflammatory state that 
amplifies COVID-19 inflammation. Obese individuals also more 
commonly have chronic conditions—hypertension, cardiovascular 
disease, chronic kidney disease, and type 2 diabetes—that further 
increase COVID-19 morbidity and mortality.(95)
8.2 Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Evidence linking obesity to bacterial pneumonia remains unclear. 
Some studies report higher self-reported respiratory infections 
(96) and hospitalization risk (97), yet many highlight an obesity 
survival paradox. A meta-analysis of 12 studies (1.5 million 
patients) (98) and subsequent studies (99,100), showed obesity 
increases infection risk but lowers mortality. Mechanistic 
explanations for differences between viral and bacterial infections 
are lacking. While many theories have been speculated, none are 
yet supported by compelling evidence (101). Obesity is also an 
independent risk factor for acute respiratory distress 
syndrome.(102)
Obesity raises the risk of ARDS during severe infection, trauma, 
or sepsis through increased systemic inflammation and reduced 
lung reserve. Mechanical changes, such as lower functional 
residual capacity and less chest wall expandability, complicate 
ventilation, often requiring higher airway pressures and careful 
lung-protective strategies. Despite these challenges, obesity does 
not consistently worsen short-term ARDS outcomes.

8.3 The Obesity Survival Paradox in ARDS
Obesity has been linked to equal or better survival compared with 
normal BMI, illustrating the “obesity survival paradox,” where 
higher BMI ICU patients may have lower mortality(103-105). 
Explanations include greater nutritional reserves, altered 
inflammation, earlier ICU admission, and more intensive care. 
Reduced chest wall expandability may also protect lungs during 
ventilation. Though not fully understood, this paradox underscores 
the importance of tailoring ARDS management to physiology and 
body composition rather than BMI alone.
9. Obesity and Lung Cancer
9.1 Epidemiological Relationships
Excess adiposity may promote cancer by increasing inflammation, 
angiogenesis, metastatic potential, and apoptosis evasion (106). 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Obesity is linked to higher mortality in several cancers, including 
colorectal, liver, gallbladder, pancreatic, and kidney (107). 
However, an “obesity paradox” exists in lung cancer: meta-
analyses show high BMI independently predicts lower risk, 
improved treatment outcomes, and longer overall survival (108) , 
contrary to the usual harmful effects of excess fat.
9.2 Variability by Demographics
          The association between obesity and lung cancer varies by 
sex, age, smoking status, and ethnicity (109). Stronger inverse 
associations have been observed in current and former smokers, 
whereas relationships are less consistent in never-smokers.(110). 
Sex-specific differences may reflect hormonal influences, fat 
distribution patterns, and differences in immune responses(111) 
.Age-related changes in body composition and metabolic health 
further modify lung cancer risk and prognosis.(112)
9.3 Potential Mechanisms
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
The link between obesity and lung cancer involves interactions 
among adipose tissue, inflammation, hormones, and metabolism. 
Adipose tissue acts as an endocrine organ, releasing pro-
inflammatory cytokines and adipokines that promote tumor 
initiation. Insulin resistance and hyperinsulinemia drive 
carcinogenesis via insulin and IGF-1 pathways (113) . Altered sex 
hormone metabolism, especially increased estrogen, and obesity-
related oxidative stress further contribute to DNA damage and 
genomic instability, collectively increasing lung cancer risk.(113)
The “obesity paradox” refers to the surprising observation that, in 
some populations, overweight or obese individuals have lower 
mortality than those with normal weight.(114).
In cancer, proposed mechanisms include protective effects of 
adipokines with anti-inflammatory and immunomodulatory roles 
(115) , greater metabolic reserves aiding tolerance to 
treatments(116) , and obesity-related genetic and molecular 
changes—such as altered insulin-like growth factor IGF signaling, 
chronic inflammation, PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway modulation, and 
estrogen metabolism—that may influence tumor biology and

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
therapy response. Metabolic reprogramming, hypoxia-driven 
angiogenesis, and epigenetic modifications may also affect 
outcomes (117,118) . The complex interplay between obesity, 
cancer biology, and treatment response requires further 
investigation.
9.4 Clinical Implications
The interaction between obesity and lung cancer underscores the 
importance of considering body composition, metabolic health, 
and immune status in risk assessment and treatment planning. 
While obesity may confer protective effects in certain contexts, it 
can also complicate cancer management through comorbidities 
and altered pharmacokinetics. 
Personalized therapeutic strategies incorporating nutritional 
assessment, metabolic optimization, and careful dosing of 
anticancer therapies are essential to improve outcomes in obese 
patients with lung cancer.

such as inhaled therapies for asthma or COPD and positive airway 
pressure for OSA and OHS, should be optimized based on disease

10.3 Special Considerations
             Obese patients require careful perioperative respiratory 
assessment due to increased risks of hypoventilation, atelectasis, 
and postoperative pulmonary complications. During pregnancy, 
obesity increases the risk of asthma exacerbations and sleep-
disordered breathing, necessitating close monitoring and 
individualized management. In pediatric populations, obesity-
related respiratory disorders such as asthma and OSA may have 
long-term developmental consequences, highlighting the 
importance of early identification, family-centered interventions, 
and prevention strategies.(61)
10.4 Emerging Pharmacological Therapies for Obesity and 
Respiratory Outcomes
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
GLP-1 receptor agonists and dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonists 
have emerged as transformative therapies for obesity with direct 
and indirect benefits on respiratory function. Semaglutide (STEP-
1 trial) achieved mean weight reduction of approximately 14.9%, 
with secondary improvements in OSA severity, exercise capacity, 
and systemic inflammation. (120) Liraglutide demonstrated 
significant reductions in AHI in the SCALE Sleep Apnea trial. (121) 
Tirzepatide (SURMOUNT-1 trial) achieved weight reductions of 
up to 22.5%,(122) and the SURMOUNT-OSA trial demonstrated 
significant AHI reductions (approximately 29 events/hour in the 
non-PAP group) independent of weight loss, suggesting GLP-1 
receptor-mediated effects on upper airway muscle tone and 
inflammation. (123)
In asthma, GLP-1 receptor agonists may reduce exacerbation 
frequency through both weight-mediated and direct anti-
inflammatory mechanisms, though large prospective trials are 
pending. These agents represent a paradigm shift in the 
management of obesity-related respiratory disorders and should 
be considered in the integrated treatment plan of obese patients 
with significant respiratory comorbidities.(124)

11. Discussion
This review has synthesized the evidence for obesity's impact on 
the full spectrum of respiratory diseases, with a focus on 
mechanistic pathways, clinical phenotyping, and emerging 
therapies. The unifying theme is that obesity generates a chronic 
state of mechanical disadvantage and pro-inflammatory 
dysregulation that affects every level of the respiratory system — 
from upper airway patency (OSA) to peripheral airway function 
(asthma) to ventilatory control (OHS) to alveolar mechanics 
(ARDS).
A key insight is that not all effects of obesity are detrimental: the 
obesity paradox in COPD and ARDS reflects genuine mechanistic 
benefits as shown in figure 3  -- FRC reduction improving 
dynamic hyperinflation in COPD, and preserved metabolic reserve 
potentially limiting organ failure in ARDS. Clinicians should resist 
reducing obesity to a uniformly negative variable and instead 
recognize context-specific effects.
The adipokine axis — particularly the leptin/adiponectin 
imbalance — offers a promising therapeutic target. Beyond

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
mechanical and pharmacological weight reduction, targeting 
adipokine signaling may provide respiratory benefits independent 
of absolute weight loss. GLP-1 receptor agonists may represent 
the first class of agents capable of simultaneously addressing the 
metabolic, inflammatory, and mechanical dimensions of obesity-
related respiratory disease
12. Future Directions and Research Priorities
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
Future research should prioritize refined phenotyping of obesity-
related respiratory disorders to support precision medicine 
approaches, as reliance on body mass index alone fails to capture 
the heterogeneity of adiposity, fat distribution, and metabolic 
status influencing disease expression. Standardization of obesity 
phenotyping—distinguishing visceral from subcutaneous fat and 
accounting for metabolic health—along with integration of 
clinical, physiological, metabolic, inflammatory, and imaging 
biomarkers, may improve identification of disease subtypes and 
prediction of therapeutic response. Further mechanistic studies 
are needed to elucidate the complex pathways linking obesity to 
respiratory dysfunction, including adipokine signaling, metabolic 
inflammation, ventilatory control abnormalities, airway and 
parenchymal remodeling, and the gut–lung microbiome axis. 
These efforts may also clarify paradoxical observations, such as 
the apparent survival benefit of obesity in advanced COPD and 
ARDS.
Therapeutic innovation remains a key priority, particularly 
through targeted anti-inflammatory and metabolic interventions 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
and combination strategies that integrate pharmacologic 
treatments with structured weight reduction. In this context, large 
prospective trials of GLP-1 and dual GLP-1/GIP agonists with 
respiratory outcomes as primary endpoints are warranted, 
alongside sex-stratified analyses to better define differential 
responses. Bariatric surgery continues to show significant benefits 
in asthma, OSA, and ventilatory mechanics, though further 
research is needed to optimize patient selection and long-term 
outcomes. Additionally, pediatric cohort studies are essential to 
determine whether early-life factors, such as dysanapsis in obese 
children, predispose to adult respiratory disease. At the 
population level, public health strategies focusing on obesity 
prevention, early intervention, multidisciplinary care, and 
integration of respiratory outcomes into obesity management 
programs will be critical to reducing disease burden.
13. Limitations
This narrative review has several inherent limitations. First, as a 
narrative rather than systematic review, selection bias cannot be 
fully excluded despite the SANRA-guided approach. Second, the 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
epidemiological literature is predominantly cross-sectional, 
limiting causal inference. Third, heterogeneity in obesity 
definitions (BMI cut-offs, body fat distribution measures) across 
studies complicates comparisons. Fourth, most studies have been 
conducted in Western adult populations; data from pediatric and 
non-Western populations are limited. Fifth, evidence hierarchies 
are not always available — some mechanistic statements are 
derived primarily from experimental models and require human 
validation
14. Conclusions
Obesity-related respiratory disorders represent a multifaceted 
clinical challenge requiring integrated, multidisciplinary 
approaches. A mechanistic understanding of the distinct 
phenotypes — grounded in the adipokine axis, chest wall 
mechanics, and ventilatory control physiology — is essential for 
personalized therapeutic strategies. Weight reduction remains the 
cornerstone of management, but emerging pharmacological 
therapies including GLP-1 receptor agonists offer new possibilities

for addressing both the metabolic and respiratory dimensions of 
obesity. Future work should focus on sex-specific phenotyping, 
microbiome interactions, and the translational potential of 
adipokine-targeted therapies.
