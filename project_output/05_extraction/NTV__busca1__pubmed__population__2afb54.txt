Abstract
Introduction Although some migrant origin groups in Europe show elevated risk for 
cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) and type 2 diabetes (T2D), information is sparse and 
fragmented. This study examines availability and possibilities for harmonization of 
health examination survey (HES) data on major determinants of CVD and T2D among 
migrant origin and ethnic minority groups in EU Member states and EU4Health 
associated countries (Norway, Iceland, Ukraine, Moldova, Montenegro).
Methods We conducted a scoping review, following PRISMA-ScR in PubMed and Web of 
Science, supplemented by targeted grey literature searches of national health 
institutes, to identify HESs covering core risk factors for CVD and T2D among migrant 
origin and ethnic minority groups, older than 18 years and living in EU Member states 
and selected associated countries. Studies were published between 2014 and 2026. 
Altogether 2537 peer-reviewed records and 348 grey literature reports were screened. 
Following full-text screening, 57 peer-reviewed papers were included. 
Results In total, 24 individual HESs in 10 countries were identified. The majority (n=38, 
67%) of the surveys were conducted in the Netherlands or Sweden. None of the HESs 
used nationally representative samples, having been conducted regionally. There was 
notable heterogeneity in how persons with a migration background and ethnic 
minorities were defined and grouped. Reported risk factors included obesity, 
hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia. Measurement methods of 
these risk factors varied by the migrant origin group and country where the study was 
conducted.
Conclusion: We did not identify any eligible publications on CVD or T2D risk factors in 
migrant origin or ethnic minority groups in 69% of EU Member States/selected 
associated countries, indicating substantial knowledge gaps among these population 
groups in Europe. The heterogeneity in measurement methods makes cross-country

comparison and data harmonization challenging. Data availability and cross-country 
comparability should be improved to support effective evidence-based health promotion

 Key Messages 
What is already known on this topic 
￿National health examination surveys (HESs) are important tools for monitoring 
cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) and type 2 diabetes (T2D).
What this study adds
￿This study identified major gaps in the availability of HES data on measured risk 
factors for CVD and T2D among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups and 
indigenous people across EU Member states and selected associated countries. 
￿Major methodological heterogeneity was observed in how migrant origin and 
ethnic minority groups were defined, as well as in how key CVD and T2D risk 
factors (obesity, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia) were 
measured. 
How this study might affect research, practice or policy 
￿This study underlines the need for nationally representative data, standardized 
definitions, and uniform methods to support evidence-based policy and practice 
and enable cross-country comparisons.
￿Based on our findings, we recommend coordinated guidance at the European 
level on improving data availability and inclusion of migrant origin and ethnic 
minority groups and indigenous people in population-based screening initiatives.

Introduction
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) and type 2 diabetes (T2D) are among the leading 
causes of morbidity and mortality globally [1]. Common modifiable behavioral risk 
factors include smoking, excess alcohol use, unhealthy diet, and low physical activity 
[2,3]. Moreover, physiological risk factors encompass high systolic blood pressure (SBP), 
high body-mass index (BMI), and high low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels 
[3]. Diabetes is also a common comorbidity of CVDs, as well as a major risk factor for 
CVDs, resulting in higher CVD morbidity and mortality in people with T2D [4]. Early 
identification of high-risk individuals for preventive care is essential [5]. 
In recent decades, high-income countries (HICs) in Europe have become increasingly 
culturally and ethnically diverse. As a result, societies now include longstanding ethnic 
minorities, indigenous people, and a growing population of migrant origin, reshaping 
demographics, and social structures [6]. In this review, we define ethnic minorities as 
individuals of migrant origin as well as other cultural and linguistic minority groups, such 
as the Roma population. Indigenous people refer to populations that have inhabited the 
given country or region at the time of conquest, colonization, or establishment of current 
state borders. In the European Union (EU), the Sámi people are the only indigenous 
group [7]. Migrant-origin groups include individuals who have migrated across national 
borders, as well as their descendants. Although there has been significant progress in 
CVD and T2D risk management, diagnostics and treatment across Europe over recent 
decades, the benefits have not been distributed equally across different population 
groups [3]. Studies have shown significant variations in CVD and T2D incidence, 
prevalence, and mortality between persons of migrant origin and ethnic minority 
groups, and indigenous people as compared to majority populations. For example, 
stroke is more prevalent among people of sub-Saharan African origin than among 
majority populations [8], and T2D occurs 10–20 years earlier in many migrant-origin 
groups [9]. Migrant origin groups also face a higher risk of delayed diagnosis,

contributing to more severe disease progression [10]. To address these inequities, more 
detailed information on the distribution of risk factors within and between ethnic 
minority groups are needed. Such data support better-targeted prevention strategies, 
resource allocation, and equitable healthcare provision [11]. Robust and inclusive 
screening initiatives are also essential for early detection and improved prevention [12].
National health examination surveys (HESs) provide key information on the prevalence 
of risk factors for CVD and T2D, essential for identifying high-risk groups and planning 
health promotion measures both at risk group and population level [13]. Over the past 
decade, 12 EU Member States, including the UK, have conducted national HESs to collect 
essential objective data, such as blood pressure, anthropometric measurements and 
biological samples, complementing self-reported information from health interviews and 
registers [13–15]. These surveys are considered highly externally valid due to their 
randomized, representative sampling methods [16]. Compared to self-reported data, 
HESs substantially reduce reporting bias and measurement error by applying 
standardized protocols and calibrated instruments, thereby improving reliability and 
comparability across populations and over time [15,16].  The inclusion of biological 
samples further enhances the capacity to identify biomarkers and undiagnosed 
conditions, positioning HESs as a methodological gold standard for population health 
monitoring [17].
Despite their strengths, existing HESs vary in terms of target populations and types of 
measurements [18], and most do not systematically include migrant origin and ethnic 
minority groups, who are often at higher risk for CVD and T2D. This exclusion limits the 
ability to monitor health disparities and develop equitable prevention strategies. 
Moreover, there is a lack of research critically examining the methodological challenges 
of using HES data in this context, particularly regarding data availability, 
representativeness, and cross-country comparability. These gaps hinder a nuanced

understanding of risk factor distribution and challenge efforts to formulate cross-
nationally consistent public health responses.
Recent reviews have examined cardiometabolic health among migrant and ethnic 
minority populations in Europe; however, they have not evaluated the availability, 
quality, or comparability of data needed to monitor these disparities. Earlier work 
pointed to major gaps and inconsistencies in ethnically relevant CVD and diabetes data, 
including variation in how ethnicity and health outcomes were defined and recorded 
[19]. More recent reviews have summarized cardiometabolic patterns among migrant 
populations, yet they do not assess the underlying data infrastructure, nor whether 
existing data sources can support cross-country comparisons [11,20–22]. Therefore, the 
aim of this scoping review is to identify and describe studies that have explored the use 
of HES data on key risk factors for CVD and T2D among migrant-origin, ethnic minority, 
and indigenous people in Europe.
Methods
Design
We carried out a scoping review following Levac and colleagues’ update of Arksey & 
O’Malley’s methodological framework [23]. This framework includes five main 
stages: (1) identifying the research questions; (2) identifying relevant studies; (3) study 
selection; (4) charting the data; and (5) collecting, summarizing, and reporting results. 
We followed the PRISMA-ScR guidelines [24].
Identifying the Research Questions
The following research questions were identified: (1) Which countries in Europe have 
conducted a HES covering key risk factors for CVD and T2D among migrant origin and 
ethnic minority groups, and indigenous people? In this review, we defined Europe as the 
EU Member States and the EU4Health-associated countries (Norway, Iceland, Ukraine,

Moldova, Montenegro), as HESs are predominantly implemented and coordinated within 
the EU and EU4Health framework; (2) How are these HESs designed?; (3) How are 
migrant origin and ethnic minority groups, and indigenous people identified within these 
HESs; and (4) How is data collected within these HESs?.
Search strategy and Eligibility Criteria: Identifying Relevant Studies
Literature searches were conducted in PubMed and Web of Science on September 2, 
2024, and updated on February 12, 2026, according to a predetermined search strategy 
(see Additional file 1). We used Boolean operators and modified our search strategy 
according to the database. We used a combination of keywords for ‘migrant’, 
‘immigrant’ and ‘ethnic minority’, as well as search terms for selected risk factors for 
CVD and T2D (i.e. obesity, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia). 
Additional file 2 provides an overview of all keywords and search terms used in this 
scoping review. We anticipated that the term ‘health examination survey’ may only be 
discussed in the full-text of articles, and that other terms may have been used to 
describe it. As such, this keyword was excluded from our search strategy to allow for a 
more thorough search of all the studies published in the field. Consequently, this 
information was assessed during the title, abstract, and full-text screening.
To reduce publication bias and identify relevant national health reports not indexed in 
bibliographic databases, we extended our search to grey literature on February 12, 
2026. We conducted targeted website searches of National Public Health Institutes and 
Ministries of Health across the included European countries (e.g. Robert Koch Institute 
in Germany, Sciensano in Belgium, Finnish Institute for Health and Welfare (THL) in 
Finland). These searches were performed using Google Advanced Search operators (e.g. 
site:gov.xx and filetype: pdf) combined with native-language keywords for migrants and 
selected risk factors (see Additional file 1). To minimize the influence of personalized 
search algorithms and ensure reproducibility, these searches were performed using a

computer with a clean browser (deleted cookies and history) and without being logged 
into a Google account. Additionally, to manage potentially overwhelming volume of 
search engine results, we screened a maximum of 10 pages (approximately 100 hits) 
for each search combination. 
Studies and grey literature files were included if they: 
￿reported outcomes measured within an HES
￿used at least one of the core physical measurements and biological samples of 
national HESs (i.e. height, weight, blood pressure, and blood samples)
￿investigated at least one of the selected risk factors for CVD and T2D, that were 
obesity, hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia
￿were based in at least one of the 27 European Member States or the associated 
non-EU countries of the EU4Health Programme (Norway, Iceland, Ukraine, 
Moldova, Montenegro)
￿were specifically designed to assess risk factors among migrant origin and ethnic 
minority groups or indigenous people
￿included participants older than 18 years old and living in Europe
￿were in English or one of the official languages of the countries included
￿were published between 2014 and 2026.
Studies and grey literature files were excluded if they:
￿involved participants already diagnosed with CVD or T2D
￿focused on gestational diabetes
￿reported on outcomes measured solely through interviews or register data
￿did not have a reference group of majority population in the same region or 
country of origin
The above exclusion criteria were applied to maintain focus on prevention and risk 
factors among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups or indigenous people within

comparable contexts. To ensure comparability within a common HES context, we 
included only studies that reported data for migrant origin, ethnic minority, or 
Indigenous groups alongside a majority-population reference group from the same 
region and HES. An internal comparator allowed assessment of whether sampling 
strategies, measurement procedures, and fieldwork protocols were applied consistently 
across groups, thereby reducing confounding from cross-survey or cross-regional 
variation. Without such a reference, observed differences may reflect methodological or 
contextual differences rather than true group-specific patterns. This criterion therefore 
ensured that included studies provided a meaningful basis for comparison and allowed 
the evaluation of whether HESs were designed to capture minority populations relative 
to the local majority groups. In addition, we concentrated specifically on physiological 
risk factors for CVD and T2D, as these indicators are measured objectively and 
consistently within Health Examination Survey (HES) frameworks. HES studies provide 
standardized biometric data (such as blood pressure, lipid levels, glucose metabolism 
markers, and anthropometric measurements) which enable reliable cross study 
comparison and reduce heterogeneity. Focusing on risk factors that HES data can assess 
robustly also minimized variability arising from self-reported behavioral or psychosocial 
measures, which are less comparable across countries and surveys. 
Study selection
All database search results were imported in Zotero and deduplicated before screening. 
The remaining references were then uploaded into Rayyan QCRI, a web-tool facilitating 
collaborations on systematic and scoping reviews [25]. Two reviewers (SMHL and FM) 
independently screened all titles and abstracts against the inclusion and exclusion 
criteria. The full texts of the remaining studies were screened based on the eligibility 
criteria by SMHL and FM. Differences in interpretations were resolved by discussion 
between the reviewers. Hand searches were conducted after the initial literature search 
to identify additional relevant studies. This process involved systematically reviewing

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
the reference lists of all studies that met the inclusion criteria and screening these 
citations to identify additional eligible studies. Relevant studies identified through this 
manual process were subsequently included following the same criteria. For grey 
literature files, we first manually assessed titles, executive summaries, and table of 
contents to determine if the document potentially constituted a HES relevant to our 
target group. Documents deemed potentially relevant were then retrieved for full 
screening. This full-text assessment employed a two-step verification process. Given the 
complexity and length of national health reports, the initial screening of these 
documents was facilitated by an AI-powered document analysis tool (NotebookLM, 
Google). Specific prompts were used to screen the full-texts in accordance with the 
eligibility criteria. Documents flagged as potentially relevant by the AI tool were then 
manually verified in full by two reviewers (SMHL and FM) to confirm inclusion. Any 
uncertainty was resolved through discussion between the reviewers. 
Charting the data
The two reviewers (SMHL and FM) independently extracted the data of the included 
records using a standardized data extraction spreadsheet in Microsoft Excel (see 
Additional file 3). Both reviewers assessed the complete data extraction spreadsheet, 
and any disagreement was resolved through discussion between them.
Collating, summarizing, and reporting the results
The synthesis of the charted data was conducted on two levels. Initially, a numerical 
analysis was performed to assess the scope, characteristics, and distribution of the 
studies included in the review. This involved generating tables and charts to visualize 
the distribution of studies by geographic location, study design, migrant origin and 
ethnic minority groups, indigenous people, and majority populations included in the 
studies. These insights guided the identification of key research gaps. Subsequently,

the textual data, organized in Excel, were thematically categorized based on risk factors 
for CVD and T2D.
Results
The database search yielded a total of 2400 publications. After removing duplicates, 
2062 titles and abstracts were screened leaving 70 articles for full-text screening. A 
total of 49 publications that met the eligibility criteria were included. Additionally, hand 
searches resulted in the further inclusion of six publications. The update of our database 
search yielded 534 publications. A total of 475 studies were screened by title and 
abstract, and 11 full-texts were screened. The updated search resulted in the inclusion 
of two publications. Finally, the extended grey literature search identified 348 records 
from targeted websites. However, following screening, no grey literature reports were 
included. The main reasons for exclusion were incorrect outcomes, lack of migrant origin 
groups/ethnic minorities inclusion, or not meeting the criteria of a HES. Thus, the 
complete search resulted in a final total of 57 peer-reviewed articles included in the 
scoping review [26–82]. Figure 1 presents the PRISMA flowchart.

Discussion
In this scoping review, we examined 57 articles and identified 24 HESs covering key risk 
factors for CVD and T2D among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups in ten 
European countries (the Netherlands, Sweden, Norway, Finland, Italy, Slovakia, Estonia, 
Portugal, Bulgaria, and Germany). All of these HES were regional, and no nationally 
representative HESs were identified. This regional concentration of HESs targeting 
migrant origin and ethnic minority groups likely reflects their urban clustering, enabling 
efficient survey logistics but potentially compromising national representativeness.
Design, populations studied, and definitions of migrant origin and ethnic minority 
groups, and indigenous people varied across the HES.  We also found substantial 
heterogeneity in whether included key risk factors for CVD and T2D (obesity, 
hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia) were assessed and how these 
were measured and defined, which complicates comparability across studies. Despite 
the clear public health importance of CVD and T2D, our findings suggest a lack of 
standardized, inclusive, and coordinated efforts to monitor these conditions among 
migrant origin and ethnic minority groups, and indigenous people at the national level.
Fragmented HES efforts among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups 
Previous research has shown the crucial role of HESs in generating high-quality data 
that supports early detection, prevention strategies, and informed policymaking at the 
national level [16,17,83]. However, as many as 67% (n=38) of the included studies were 
conducted in the Netherlands and Sweden, including the largest scale Dutch studies

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
HELIUS and RODAM. In contrast, many other European countries had conducted only a 
single HES involving persons of migrant origin or ethnic minority groups, or none. This 
uneven distribution highlights significant geographic disparities in the implementation 
of population-based screening efforts. Notably, countries such as Germany, France, or 
Spain host some of the largest and most diverse migrant populations in Europe, 
reflecting broader demographic shifts across the continent [84]. To address this 
imbalance, future public health research should prioritize expanding HES coverage in 
underrepresented regions, particularly those with substantial migrant origin and ethnic 
minority groups. This includes allocating funding, building local research capacity, and 
fostering cross-country collaboration to ensure that health data collection reflects the 
diversity of Europe’s population.
Inconsistent definitions of migrant origin groups and ethnicity 
A key finding of this review is the significant variation in how migrant origin, ethnic 
minority groups, and indigenous populations are defined and identified across HESs. 
This reflects both a lack of conceptual and methodological standardization across 
Europe and differences in the availability of migration and ethnicity related data in 
population registers commonly used for sampling. Such variation is further shaped by 
national legal frameworks, which determine what types of data can be collected and 
accessed for these purposes. Definitions ranged from country of birth and self-reported 
status to administrative data and community-based identification. Some studies also 
lacked clarity on how migrant origin groups or ethnicity were defined. This variability 
reflects broader challenges documented in the literature. Different data sources, such 
as country of birth, self-identified ethnicity, language, or religious beliefs, are often used 
as proxies for migrant origin groups, but each comes with important limitations. For 
instance, country of birth becomes an increasingly crude measure over time, 
particularly for second- or third-generation migrants, while nationality or citizenship may 
not reflect cultural identity or health-related behaviors [85–88].

Ethnicity itself implies shared origins, culture, customs, language, or religion [89], yet 
these dimensions are difficult to capture consistently and meaningfully in HESs. 
Moreover, the relationship between migrant origin groups and disease risk is complex 
and shaped by multiple factors across the life course. These include genetic 
predispositions, early-life exposure to poverty, malnutrition, or infection, cultural 
practices maintained after migration, and the quality of care and structural conditions 
encountered in the host countries [6,8].  Second-generation individuals (i.e. those born 
in the host country to migrant parents) can still face elevated risks for T2D and CVDs 
[11]. This raises important questions about how long and on what basis people should 
be classified as belonging to a migrant origin group. Researchers increasingly call for 
more reflexive, inclusive approaches that avoid rigid stigmatizing categories and 
instead reflect lived experiences and structural factors [90,91]. Nevertheless, without a 
shared understanding of what constitutes ‘migrant origin’ or ‘ethnicity’, it becomes 
difficult to interpret differences between population groups.
Heterogeneity in risk factor measurement and diagnostic criteria 
There was substantial heterogeneity in the methods used to measure and define the 
four selected CVD and T2D risk factors (i.e. obesity, hypertension, 
hypercholesterolemia, and hyperglycemia). Studies varied in indicators, diagnostic 
thresholds, and sample types, leading to a lack of consistency and transparency. These 
methodological differences can result in risk misestimation, missed diagnoses, or 
inappropriate follow-up, potentially leading to unequal access to preventive care, 
delayed treatment, and inefficient resource allocation. Previous studies have 
highlighted the need for harmonization of measurement protocols in HESs to enable 
valid cross-country comparisons and support coordinated public health action at the 
European level [17,83,92]. Our findings reaffirm this need but also extend it by 
highlighting a critical gap: existing calls for harmonization rarely address how

standardized methods should be adapted or applied in the context of population 
diversity.
Implications and future directions
It is crucial that HESs reflect the full diversity of the population, including persons of 
migrant origin and ethnic minority groups, who are often underrepresented in routine 
data systems [85]. Given that these populations may comprise a small share of national 
sampling frames, representativeness requires deliberate design choices, including 
targeted oversampling or dedicated supplementary sampling.  In this regard, migrant 
origin ethnic minority groups, and indigenous people should be integrated as a 
supplementary sample in national HESs, rather than in smaller regional HESs specifically 
focused on these groups. The supplementary sample has to be large enough to conduct 
meaningful sub-group analyses. Such an approach could increase analytic power and 
improve the precision of subgroup estimates across Europe. Importantly, it must be kept 
in mind that one-size-fits-all approach does not equal to successful integration. There 
should be flexibility acknowledging diverse knowledge systems and cultural norms when 
designing the questionnaires and health examination protocols. Potential barriers to 
participation should be considered, and recruitment methods may need to be adapted 
to support the participation rate. Thus, our findings underscore the urgent need for 
coordinated guidance at the European level on how to include those people in 
population-based screening initiatives.
This review shows the inconsistent identification, inclusion of migrant origin or ethnic 
minority groups, and risk factors assessment across Europe, adding complexity to this 
issue. While harmonizing definitions and measurement protocols is essential, it is 
equally important to develop practical guidelines for the inclusion of migration 
background and ethnicity in HESs. Such guidance should specify which dimensions of 
diversity need to be examined, provide methodological recommendations on how to

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
sample for diversity within HESs, and establish consistent definitions for cross-country 
minorities, such as those of ‘migrant origin’ or ‘ethnic minorities’. Including these 
dimensions in HESs is not only justified by the known disparities in CVD and T2D risk 
[6], but also necessary for designing effective, equitable interventions.
Future research should examine how HESs can further serve as a general tool to identify 
gaps in healthcare, thereby informing the design of targeted approaches to reach high-
risk groups. Ultimately, greater consistency in representing migrant origin and ethnic 
minority groups in health monitoring systems is essential to ensure that efforts 
effectively reach high-risk groups and help reduce health inequalities across Europe. 
Moreover, although this review focused exclusively on HES-based measurement 
approaches, several European countries maintain high-quality national registers with 
migration-stratified cardiometabolic data. Integrating insights from these register 
systems with HES findings offers an important avenue for future research and could 
provide a more comprehensive understanding of cardiometabolic health across diverse 
populations. Finally, future reviews could extend this work by incorporating behavioral, 
environmental, socioeconomic, and structural determinants (such as air pollution, traffic 
noise, neighborhood deprivation, green space availability) alongside physiological risk 
factors and clinical outcomes to capture the full complexity of cardiometabolic health in 
migrant-origin groups and ethnic minorities. 
Strengths and limitations
A key strength of this study is the use of a transparent and rigorous scoping review 
methodology, which enabled a systematic and structured overview of HESs targeting 
persons of migrant origin and ethnic minority groups in Europe. The review process 
followed established guidelines, ensuring clarity and reproducibility. To maximize 
comprehensiveness and mitigate publication bias, our search strategy encompassed 
two major academic databases and an extensive multilingual grey literature search of 
ACCEPTED MANUSCRIPT
ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
national public health websites. Although this grey literature search yielded no eligible 
reports, it clearly highlighted the scarcity of HES data for migrant origin groups and 
ethnic minorities, pointing to a substantial gap in publicly accessible health examination 
information for these populations. It also revealed structural challenges: many 
documents lacked sufficient methodological detail to verify HES eligibility, did not report 
usable migrant/ethnicity identifiers, or were non-HES reports (e.g., interview surveys, 
dashboards) without standardized examination measures. Nonetheless, some 
limitations should be considered. The retrieval of grey literature remains subject to 
opaque search engine algorithms. Furthermore, some relevant information and data 
may exist within national health institutes but remain unpublished or restricted for 
internal government use.  
A further limitation of this review is its exclusive focus on physiological risk factors. 
Although this approach elucidates underlying biological mechanisms, it potentially 
narrows the scope of risk assessment by omitting behavioral, environmental, and 
socioeconomic factors that are critical and modifiable contributors to the development 
of CVD and T2D. This is particularly relevant for migrant origin populations, who often 
undergo acculturation-related changes in diet and physical activity that elevate 
cardiometabolic risk [93]. Environmental and structural factors such as air pollution, 
traffic-related- noise, and neighborhood disadvantage further contribute to this risk and 
disproportionately affect these groups [94].
Finally, the definition of European countries was based on the EU4Health Programme 
(2021-2027), which may not fully reflect the diversity of health monitoring practices 
across the broader European region. Despite these considerations, the review offers a 
robust and informative overview of current practices and gaps in the monitoring of 
cardiometabolic risk among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups, and indigenous 
people.

Conclusion
This scoping review reveals substantial gaps in monitoring CVD and T2D risk factors 
among migrant origin and ethnic minority groups and indigenous people in EU Member 
States and associated countries. While several regional HESs exist, the lack of nationally 
representative data, standardized definitions, and consistent methods hampers reliable 
cross-country comparisons. The heterogeneity in risk factor assessment and defining 
target populations complicates data harmonization and limits policy relevance. To 
support equitable and effective health promotion and disease prevention strategies, 
improved data availability, methodological alignment, and coordinated European efforts 
are essential. Developing shared guidelines for the inclusion and assessment of migrant 
origin and ethnic minority groups and indigenous people in health surveillance systems 
will be essential to better address the growing burden of CVD and T2D in increasingly 
diverse European societies.
