Abstract
Background Heart failure is a complex clinical syndrome characterized by structural and/or functional abnormalities 
that impair cardiac output, leading to alterations in cardiac biomarkers, metabolic disturbances, and gut dysbiosis. 
Lifestyle interventions, particularly dietary strategies such as time-restricted eating (TRE) and healthy diets like 
the sustainable diet (SD), along with probiotic supplementation, have been proposed as key components in the 
management of heart failure. Thus, the present study aims to evaluate the effects of co-administration of SD and TRE, 
either alone or in combination with Lactobacillus plantarum 299v supplementation, compared to control group, on 
cardiovascular function in overweight or obese patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction.
Methods We will conduct a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, three-arm clinical trial over 60 days. 
Participants will be randomly assigned in a 1:1:1 ratio to one of three groups for 60 days: (A) co-administration of SD 
and TRE with probiotic supplementation (n = 25); (B) co-administration of SD and TRE with placebo supplementation 
(n = 25); and (C) control group with placebo supplementation (n = 25), serving as the control group. The primary 
outcomes will be changes in N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide and left ventricular ejection fraction. 
Secondary outcomes will include changes in fasting blood glucose, lipid profile, liver and kidney function markers, 
anthropometric indices, arterial stiffness, electrocardiogram indices, echocardiographic indices, sleep quality, appetite, 
energy intake, quality of life, exercise tolerance, and Zonulin and Trimethylamine N-oxide as gut-related biomarkers. 
All outcomes of this study will be evaluated before and after the intervention.

Introduction
Heart Failure (HF)
 HF is a complex, life-threatening clinical syndrome char-
acterized by structural and/or functional cardiac abnor -
malities (inability to fill and/or pump blood) that impair 
cardiac output, resulting in inadequate circulation at nor-
mal physiological pressure [ 1]. The global prevalence of 
HF is estimated to be 64.3 million people [ 2]. According 
to the most recent guidelines [2, 3], HF is classified based 
on left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) into three 
phenotypes: reduced (HFrEF, ≤ 40%), mildly reduced 
(HFmrEF, 41–49%), and preserved (HFpEF, ≥ 50%), 
with HFrEF accounts for approximately half of all HF 
cases. N-terminal pro-B-type natriuretic peptide (NT-
proBNP) is an inactive and stable amino acid fragment 
associated with LVEF, secreted by cardiac myocytes in 
response to neurohormonal stimulation, transmural wall 
stress, inflammation, and ischemia [ 3, 4]. Elevated lev -
els of NT-proBNP indicate left ventricular remodeling, 
which is associated with major adverse cardiovascular 
events (MACE), reduced quality of life, an increased risk 
of rehospitalization, and higher cardiovascular mortality 
rates [5].
Modified EAT-Lancet diet
Overweight and obesity, characterized by excessive fat 
accumulation [ 6], are common comorbidities that play 
a critical role in the pathogenesis of HF through hemo -
dynamic and morphological cardiac changes [ 7, 8]. Life-
style interventions, particularly dietary modifications and 
physical activity, are the cornerstone of any obesity man -
agement program, especially among HF patients [ 9, 10]. 
To date, extensive evidence has highlighted the benefits 
of “heart-healthy” dietary patterns, such as the Mediter -
ranean diet (Med diet), the Indo-Mediterranean diet, 
and the Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension diet 
(DASH), all of which have been consistently proven to be 
cardioprotective [11– 19]. Additionally, diet has a signifi -
cant influence on both human health and the well-being 
of the planet [ 20]. The EAT-Lancet Commission pro -
posed the EAT-Lancet universal healthy reference diet in 
2019 as a “Sustainable Diet (SD)” , which not only prevents 
diet-related diseases but also promotes environmental 
sustainability [20]. The SD emphasizes the consumption 
of whole grains, vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts, and 
fish, along with moderate amounts of dairy and poultry, 
while limiting the intake of red meat, tubers and starchy 
vegetables, processed and prepackaged foods, added fats, 
and sugars [21]. Higher adherence to SD has been associ-
ated with a reduced risk of HF [21].
Time-Restricted Eating (TRE)
In addition to dietary interventions, the timing of food 
consumption has been found to play a promising role in 
cardiometabolic health, independent of weight loss [ 22]. 
Intermittent fasting (IF) is a well-tolerated eating pattern 
involving periods of voluntary abstinence from calories, 
alternated with periods of calorie consumption [ 22]. Fur-
thermore, IF can promote a metabolic switch by increas -
ing the utilization of ketone bodies (KBs) during fasting 
in highly metabolically active organs, such as the heart 
[23]. IF is a general term for various dietary modalities, 
including periodic prolonged fasting (e.g., fasting for 2–7 
days), alternate-day fasting (ADF) (fasting day alternated 
with ad libitum consumption day), the 5:2 fasting regi -
men (fasting for 2 days per week), religious fasting (e.g., 
Ramadan), and TRE (which limits daily food consump -
tion to a window of 4–12 h, followed by a fasting window 
of 12–20 h per day) [ 22, 24]. There is still a knowledge 
gap regarding the optimal dietary intervention for 
improving HF. Given this, the combination of TRE with 
distinct dietary interventions (namly, SD) may improve 
and reverse cardiovascular events among HFrEF patients 
who are overweight or obese.
Gut hypothesis and Gut-Heart Axis (GHA)
On the other hand, the “gut hypothesis” of HF proposed 
that the microbial composition of the gut plays a crucial 
role in the pathogenesis of HF [ 25]. Recent studies have 
shown that dysbiosis of the gut microbiota increases the 
production of trimethylamine-N-oxide (TMAO), a key 
metabolite generated by the gut microbiota from dietary 
choline, betaine, and L-carnitine [ 25, 26]. Elevated 
TMAO levels are associated with increased NT-proBNP 
levels, greater severity of left ventricular dysfunction, left 
ventricular dilation, poor functional status (as defined 
by the New York Heart Association [NYHA] classifica -
tion), and adverse clinical outcomes among HF patients 
echocardiographic markers in overweight and obese patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction 
compared to control individuals.

Probiotics
Long-term treatment with Lactobacillus helveticus (L. 
helveticus) has been shown to effectively reduce systolic 
and diastolic blood pressure in patients with hyperten -
sion [ 29]. Additionally, administration of L. plantarum 
299v ( Lp299v) improves endothelium-dependent vaso -
dilation and reduces systemic inflammation in patients 
with stable coronary artery disease (CAD) [ 30]. More -
over, probiotic-fermented purple sweet potato yogurt 
(PSPY) (containing L. acidophilus ; L. delbrueckii ; and 
L. gasseri ) demonstrated an inhibitory effect on cardiac 
hypertrophy in 6-week-old mice [31]. The findings of pre-
vious studies on the effects of probiotic administration in 
HF have been contradictory. For instance, Shoaei Matin 
et al. reported a favorable effect of multi-strain synbiotic 
therapy on NT-proBNP levels in HFrEF patients [ 27]. 
Moreover, L. rhamnosus GR-1 attenuates HF in rats [ 32]. 
However, another study found no significant difference 
between the consumption of probiotic-enriched yogurt 
and regular yogurt in HF patients [33].
Rationale and aim of the study
Probiotics have been recognized as an innovative strat -
egy to enhance the effectiveness of dietary interventions 
for both weight management and clinical improvements 
[34]. It should also be noted that the selection of Lp299v 
is based on preclinical and clinical evidence demonstrat -
ing its gastrointestinal survival, adherence to the intesti -
nal epithelium, contribution to gut health, and beneficial 
effects on iron absorption, immune function, and car -
diovascular outcomes [ 30, 32, 35, 36]. To the best of our 
knowledge, no clinical trial has investigated the effects of 
co-administration of SD and TRE with probiotic supple -
mentation in HF patients with obesity or overweight. 
Therefore, the present study aims to assess the effects 
of co-administration of SD and TRE, either alone or in 
combination with Lp299v supplementation, compared to 
control group, on anthropometric indices, clinical signs, 
echocardiographic parameters, arterial function, and bio-
chemical markers in overweight or obese HFrEF patients. 
In fact, in our study, the combination of diet and Lp299v 
may synergistically exert favorable effects on nutritional 
and inflammatory biomarkers, and clinical outcomes.
Subjects and methods
Study design
This placebo-controlled, three-parallel-arm, random -
ized, double-blind clinical trial is designed to investigate 
the effects of co-administration of SD and TRE, either 
alone or in combination with Lp299v supplementation, 
compared to control group, on cardiovascular func -
tion in patients with HFrEF. As a double-blind study, the 
experts responsible for examining patients and measur -
ing laboratory data will be blinded to the intervention 
agents and group assignments. The study was approved 
by the Medical Ethics Committee of Mashhad University 
of Medical Sciences (IR.MUMS.REC.1402.259; approved 
on December 16, 2023). Additionally, the study protocol 
has been registered with the Iranian Registry of Clinical 
Trials (IRCT20231225060520N1; registered on January 
23, 2024). This trial will be conducted at Imam Reza Hos-
pital, Mashhad, Iran. Written informed consent will be 
obtained from all participants before their involvement in 
the study. The study protocol was prepared in accordance 
with the Standard Protocol Items: Recommendations 
for Interventional Trials (SPIRIT) 2025 checklist (Addi -
tional file 1). The study flow chart for enrolment, alloca -
tion, follow up, and analysis is presented in Fig.  1. The 
SPIRIT diagram for the trial schedule is shown in Fig.  2. 
Any amendments to the study protocol regarding patient 
safety, benefits, protocol deviations, or unintentional 
modifications that do not affect participants’ rights, study 
risk or benefit, data integrity, or overall safety and wel -
fare will be implemented after review and approval by the 
Department of Clinical Nutrition and the Medical Ethics 
Committee of Mashhad University of Medical Sciences 
(MUMS), before the study’s initiation. Any changes to 
the study protocol will also be submitted to the Nutrition 
Journal ( h t t p  s : /  / n u t  r i  t i o  n j .  b i o m  e d  c e n t r a l . c o m /).
Study duration
The follow-up period will be 60 days. The 60-day dura -
tion is set based on evidence from prior nutritional 
interventions in HF patients [ 37– 39], validation of NT-
proBNP as a surrogate endpoint within this timeframe 
[40, 41], alignment with routine clinical care, and consid -
erations of feasibility and adherence to the intervention.
Study setting
Participants will be recruited from the Cardiac Outpa -
tient Clinic at Imam Reza Hospital, affiliated with Mash -
had University of Medical Sciences. Selection will include 
HFrEF patients who have been previously diagnosed by 
an expert cardiologist based on both echocardiographic 
indices (LVEF) and the NYHA classification (II and III). 
Patients expressing interest will be invited to undergo the 
screening process.
Study participants and enrollment
Before recruitment, an experienced cardiologist spe -
cializing in the diagnosis of HFrEF will identify eligible 
patients based on LVEF and the NYHA functional class. 
Following diagnosis, eligible patients with HFrEF will 
be referred to the researcher responsible for patient

selection and enrollment. After receiving a detailed 
explanation of the study, patients who provide informed 
consent and meet the inclusion criteria will be enrolled 
in the study.
Inclusion criteria
The inclusion criteria are as follows: men and women 
aged 30 to 75 years with an LVEF ≤ 40% (as diagnosed by 
a cardiologist), who have received standard treatments 
for HF for at least one month, are classified in NYHA 
functional class II or III by a cardiologist, and have a BMI 
between 25 and 35 kg/m².
Non-entry criteria
Patients will not be eligible if they meet any of the fol -
lowing criteria: have experienced an acute cardiac event 
within the last month; have an intolerance to probiotic 
supplementation; have an active inflammatory or infec -
tious disease at the time of enrollment; have chronic kid -
ney disease (eGFR ≤ 30 mL/min/1.73 m²) or are receiving 
renal replacement therapy (dialysis); have liver failure or 
cirrhosis (Child–Pugh class C); have gastrointestinal dis -
eases (e.g., short bowel syndrome, inflammatory bowel 
disease) or conditions that affect probiotic absorption 
(e.g., ileostomy); have cancer; are candidates for cardiac 
surgery during the study period; are taking antibiotics at 
the time of enrollment (but may be eligible if treatment is 
stopped and the patient recovers after one month); con -
sume fermented products or foods containing probiotics; 
are unable to fast; are night shift workers; are drug abus -
ers; are on a special or prescribed diet for any reason; 
are receiving antidiabetic therapy with sulfonylureas or 
insulin; are participating in other interventional studies; 
are pregnant or breastfeeding; are reluctant or mentally 
unprepared to participate in the study.
Baseline eligibility criteria
Participants will be eligible if they do not meet any of the 
following criteria: have received antibiotics for at least 
one month before the intervention; known allergy to the 
probiotic; current use of prohibited supplements/medi -
cations (take nutritional supplements or any medication 
affecting weight, appetite, carbohydrate, or lipid metabo -
lism during the study); and unstable HF therapy.
Post-randomization discontinuation criteria
Post-randomization discontinuation criteria will be: 
starting antibiotics during the intervention; protocol-
defined poor adherence (i.e., consuming less than 80% 
of the supplements during the study period); adverse 
reaction to the supplement; systolic blood pressure < 90 
mmHg [42]; or disturbances in serum electrolyte levels.

Study interventions
Participants will be randomly assigned to one of three 
groups (with an allocation ratio of 1:1:1) for 60 days. The 
study groups include: (A) co-administration of SD and 
TRE with probiotic supplementation; (B) co-administra -
tion of SD and TRE with placebo supplementation; and 
(C) a control group with placebo supplementation as the 
control. In this study, a 14:10 TRE regimen will be imple -
mented, with a 14-hour fasting window followed by a 
10-hour eating window. During the eating window, par -
ticipants will be instructed to follow the SD diet, which 
will be tailored to each individual by a dietitian. Partici -
pants will select one of the following time ranges for their 
eating period: [ 1] from 9:00 AM to 7:00 PM daily; [ 2] 
from 10:00 AM to 8:00 PM daily; or [ 3] from 11:00 AM 
to 9:00 PM daily. The fasting period will be from 7:00 PM 
to 9:00 AM the following day, from 8:00 PM to 10:00 AM, 
or from 9:00 PM to 11:00 AM, respectively. During the 
14-hour fasting period, participants will only be allowed 
to drink water, tea, black coffee (without sugar or milk), 
or any other calorie-free beverage.
SD will be designed based on the EAT-Lancet diet, as

(1) Definition of EAT-Lancet diet
 Food systems have the potential to promote human 
health and support environmental sustainability; 
however, they are currently threatening both. 
With the growing global population, providing 
healthy diets through sustainable food systems is 
increasingly challenging. Over 820 million people 
suffer from food insufficiency, and many others 
adopt unhealthy eating patterns that contribute 
to diet-related obesity and non-communicable 
diseases (NCDs), such as diabetes, stroke, HF, and 
premature death. Given the above, the global burden 
of NCDs is expected to worsen. At the same time, 
the environmental impact of food production—on 
greenhouse gas emissions (GHGE), nitrogen and 
phosphorus pollution, biodiversity loss, and water 
and land use—will further destabilize the Earth’s 
system [20].
 To ensure alignment with the UN Sustainable 
Development Goals (SDGs) and the Paris 
Agreement, the EAT-Lancet Commission convened 
19 commissioners and 18 coauthors from 16 
countries in 2019, representing diverse fields such 
as human health, agriculture, political science, 
and environmental sustainability. The goal was to 
develop global scientific targets based on the best 
available evidence for healthy diets and sustainable 
food production. The proposed reference diet 
primarily consists of vegetables, fruits, whole grains, 
legumes, nuts, and unsaturated oils, with a moderate 
amount of seafood and poultry, and minimal to 
no red meat, processed meat, added sugar, refined 
grains, and starchy vegetables. The global average 
intake of healthy foods is substantially lower than 
the reference diet, while overconsumption of 
unhealthy foods continues to rise. The widespread 
adoption of the reference dietary pattern would yield 
significant health benefits, including a substantial 
reduction in total mortality. Moreover, achieving 
healthy diets through sustainable food systems for 
all will require substantial shifts towards healthier 
dietary patterns, significant reductions in food 
losses and waste, and major improvements in food 
production practices [20].
(2) Definition of EAT-Lancet diet index
 The food components in the EAT-Lancet diet index 
[43] are based on the EAT-Lancet diet introduced 
by Willett et al. in 2019 [20]. The index was 
previously validated by Stubbendorff et al., based 
on its association with mortality [43]. The food 
components of the EAT-Lancet diet are categorized 
as either “emphasized foods” or “limited foods, ” 
as described in previous accounts of the diet [20, 
43]. Emphasized foods include vegetables, fruits, 
unsaturated oils, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and 
fish. Limited foods consist of beef and lamb, pork, 
poultry, eggs, dairy, potatoes, and added sugar [20, 
43]. Table 1 provides detailed information about the 
14 components of the EAT-Lancet diet.

 Second, the total energy expenditure (TEE) for each

  • Lightly active (1.375): Light exercise or sports 1–3

 Third, this daily caloric requirement will be 
divided into seven meals, including three main 
meals and four snacks, with a macronutrient ratio 
of 50% carbohydrates, 35% fat, and 15% protein. 
Additionally, these meals will adhere to the EAT-
Lancet diet index. A dietitian will carry out all of the 
above-mentioned steps.
(4) Food intake conversion
 In the study by Stubbendorff et al., the 14 food 
components in the EAT-Lancet index are presented 
in grams per day [43]. Target and reference values 
from the EAT-Lancet diet were presented based on 
an energy intake of 2500 kcal, expressed in grams. 
For lower calorie levels, we will calculate the ratio. 
For instance, in the SD with 2000 kcal, we will divide 
2000 by 2500 to obtain the ratio of 2000 kcal SD 
to 2500 kcal SD. This ratio will then be multiplied 
by the target grams for each food component in 
the 2500 kcal EAT-Lancet diet. Finally, based on 
the calculated grams, the 2000 kcal EAT-Lancet 
diet will be prepared. For other calorie levels, the 
steps mentioned above will be applied. Since pork 
is forbidden in Iran based on Islamic religious 
teachings, it has been replaced with lean red meat 
in the design of a SD. Furthermore, in this study, we 
use the term “SD” as a substitute for the EAT-Lancet 
diet, with both terms being equivalent. Table 2 
presents detailed information about the SD.
(5) Preparing the individualized food menu and 
providing detailed explanations to participants
 Iranian dietary preferences will be taken into 
account when preparing individualized food menus 
based on the SD. Additionally, to develop a SD 
menu, we will incorporate multicultural dietary 
models, including the Indo-Mediterranean and 
HEART diets, both of which are rich in antioxidant 
components [14– 17]. Individualized menus will 
be designed based on these principles, and the 
intervention will be implemented using a culturally 
adapted EAT-Lancet framework (modified EAT-
Lancet Diet). Additionally, in designing the SD diet, 
we pay particular attention to biodiversity, namely 
both inter- and intra-food group diversity, in terms 
of the variety of plant and animal species.
 In the SD menu, whole grains will be emphasized 
(e.g., incorporating 50% wheat porridge into 
wheat flour for bread preparation), while refined 
grains will be completely excluded. To reduce the 
glycemic index, the addition of bran to rice prior 
to consumption will be considered, along with 
planning combinations of rice with different types of 
legumes. These measures are in line with reducing 
the glycemic index, improving biodiversity, and 
lowering carbon emissions. Moreover, to ensure 
palatability, commonly used spices in Iranian cuisine 
will be recommended for incorporation into main 
meals.

Randomization and blinding
Randomization will be performed before participant allo-
cation. A stratified permuted block randomization (with 
a fixed block size of n = 4) will be conducted using com -
puter-generated random numbers by the study method -
ologist through the online website  (   h t t p : / / w w w . s e a l d e n v e 
l o p e . c o m     ) . In this method, eligible participants who meet 
the study inclusion criteria will be randomly allocated 
to one of three groups, with stratification by BMI (25–
30  kg/m² and 30–35  kg/m²): SD + TRE with a probiotic 
supplement, SD + TRE with a placebo, and control group 
with a placebo. Random allocation will be based on a ran-
dom number list, with the letters A, B, and C assigned to 
an equal number of participants in the list (the allocation 
ratio is 1:1:1 for groups A, B, and C).

placebo and probiotic supplements will be retained by 
the company until the end of the study, ensuring blind -

Supplement administration
During the study period, participants will take two pro -
biotic or placebo supplement capsules daily—one an 
hour after breakfast and the other two hours after dinner, 
accompanied by a glass of water. Each probiotic capsule 
contains 1.4 × 10¹⁰ CFU of Lactobacillus plantarum 299v 
(Lp299v), along with Avicel®, magnesium stearate, and 
colloidal silica. The placebo capsule contains croscarmel-
lose, Avicel®, mannitol, colloidal silica, and flavoring, but 
no probiotic.
Probiotic selection rationale
Among the diverse probiotic genera commonly utilized 
in dietary supplements and functional foods, Lactoba-
cillus represents one of the most extensively studied 
and clinically relevant groups. For instance, a preclinical 
study demonstrated that administration of L. rhamnosus 
GR-1 attenuates myocardial hypertrophy and HF fol -
lowing myocardial infarction, suggesting that probiotics 
may hold promise as a potential therapeutic strategy for 
improving HF (probiotic-mediated cardiac improvement) 
[32]. While several species are adapted to specific eco -
logical niches, L. plantarum  is widely distributed across 
dairy products, meat, fermented foods, and vegetables. 
The strain Lp299v is recognized as safe and has been 
applied in both food and pharmaceutical contexts, with 
reported health benefits in a range of conditions, includ -
ing neurodegenerative and metabolic disorders, cancer, 
urogenital and hepatic diseases, anemia, and hyperten -
sion [35, 36].
Given the broad cardiovascular therapeutic potential 
of probiotics [ 35], it is important to elucidate the strain-
specific benefits and characteristics of Lp299v in the 
context of HFrEF. However, evidence-based guidance 
regarding the optimal strain and dosage of probiotic sup -
plementation in patients with HFrEF is currently lack -
ing. Therefore, we selected this specific strain of Lp299v 
and its dosing regimen based on previously mentioned 
studies [32, 35, 36] and also the study that demonstrated 
the favorable impact of Lp299v supplementation on 
improved vascular endothelial function, reduced circu -
lating levels of interleukins 8 and 12, and increased pro -
pionic acid production among men with stable coronary 
artery disease (CAD) [30].

Measurements and assessments
Demographic characteristics Demographic charac -
teristics of patients, including age, level of education, 
occupation, marital status, duration of disease diagnosis, 
sleep time (time to sleep and time to wake up), alcohol 
consumption, smoking history, use of nutritional supple -
ments, use of herbal medicines, and history of various dis-
eases, will be collected at baseline using a checklist during 
an interview.
Nutritional assessment To assess food intake, a 3-day, 
24-hour dietary recall (including two working days and 
one weekend day) will be obtained through an interview 
with a trained nutritionist. Participants will be asked to 
accurately describe their consumption of food, alcoholic, 
and non-alcoholic beverages over the previous three days 
(two working days and one weekend day). The interviewer 
will inquire about meal times, types, and serving sizes 
of food, as well as the ingredients of the consumed food 
items. Additionally, dietitians would use food measures, 
food portions and food models to assess food intakes. The 
24-hour dietary recall for the first day will be completed 
during the first visit, and the trained nutritionist will con-
duct the second and third recalls via phone. The 24-hour 
dietary recalls will be collected on a monthly basis. After 
each recall, the dietary data will be reviewed by a nutrition 
expert. Dietary intake will be analyzed using Nutritionist 
IV software (First Databank, San Bruno, CA, USA), modi-
fied for Iranian foods.
Physical activity assessment We will assess physical 
activity using the long-form International Physical Activ -
ity Questionnaire (IPAQ) through face-to-face inter -
views. The validity and reliability of the IPAQ have been 
previously established in the Iranian population [ 46]. The 
IPAQ evaluates habitual physical activity (PA) over the 
past seven days and consists of 27 items across four PA 
domains: occupational (7 items), transportation (6 items), 
household/gardening (6 items), and leisure-time activities 
(6 items). Additionally, it includes two questions about 
the time spent sitting, serving as an indicator of seden -
tary behavior. The questionnaire records the number of 
days per week and the duration per day spent walking, 
as well as engaging in moderate- and vigorous-intensity 
activities across all four domains. To improve accuracy, 
culturally relevant examples of moderate- and vigorous-

intensity activities are provided. IPAQ data are converted 
into metabolic equivalent (MET) scores (MET-min/
week) by multiplying the time spent on each activity by 
its corresponding MET value. One MET represents the 
energy expenditure at rest, approximately 3.5 ml O₂/kg/
min in adults. Based on the IPAQ scoring system, physi -
cal activity levels are categorized into three groups: inac -
tive, minimally active, and health-enhancing physically 
active. Additionally, sufficient vigorous activity is defined 
as engaging in vigorous-intensity exercise for at least 20 
min per day on three or more days per week. Similarly, 
sufficient moderate and walking activities are defined as 
performing moderate-intensity exercise or walking for at 
least 30 min per day on five or more days per week [47].
Quality of life assessment In the present study, we will 
use the reliable and validated Minnesota Living with 
Heart Failure Questionnaire (MLHFQ) to assess how HF 
impacts various aspects of a patient’s life [48]. This ques-
tionnaire focuses on six domains: signs and symptoms, 
social well-being, physical activity, sexual health, work, 
and emotional well-being. The MLHFQ is a self-report 
instrument that employs a Likert-type response scale, 
allowing respondents to indicate the level of agreement or 
impact they experience. The scale ranges from 0 (indicat-
ing no effect on quality of life) to 5 (indicating the highest 
level of influence on quality of life). In this context, higher 
scores indicate poorer quality of life, suggesting that the 
individual is experiencing significant challenges related to 
their health condition.
Appetite assessment Appetite will be assessed using 
the original version of the Council on Nutrition Appe -
tite Questionnaire (CNAQ), developed by Wilson et al. 
[49]. This questionnaire demonstrates strong psycho -
metric properties and is suitable for measuring appetite 
in patients with HF [50]. The CNAQ includes eight items 
that evaluate different aspects of appetite: general appe -
tite (item 1), satiety (item 2), hunger (item 3), taste per -
ception (items 4 and 5), quantity of food intake (item 6), 
nausea (item 7), and mood (item 8). Each item is rated 
on a five-point Likert scale [ 1– 5], with total scores rang -
ing from 8 to 40. Lower scores indicate reduced appetite, 
and a CNAQ score of ≤ 28 suggests poor appetite and an 
increased risk of significant weight loss over six months. A 
shorter version of the questionnaire, the Simplified Nutri-
tional Appetite Questionnaire (SNAQ), includes four 
items from the CNAQ (items 1, 2, 4, and 6) and yields a 
total score ranging from 4 to 20. A SNAQ score of ≤ 14 
also indicates poor appetite and a risk of notable weight 
loss within six months [ 49]. Both the CNAQ and SNAQ 
will be used in the present study.
Depression, anxiety, and stress assessment The DASS-
21 (Depression, Anxiety, and Stress Scale) questionnaire 
[51] will be used to quickly detect potential risks related 
to the severity of core symptoms of depression, anxiety, 
and stress. The questionnaire consists of 21 items and is 
a shortened version of the DASS-42. Each scale measures 
specific characteristics, as outlined below:
  • The depression scale evaluates emotional and 
cognitive symptoms such as dysphoria, anhedonia, 
hopelessness, self-deprecation, a lack of interest 
or engagement in life, and an overall sense of life 
devaluation.
  • The anxiety scale assesses signs of autonomic 
arousal, situational anxiety, and subjective 
experiences of anxiousness.
  • The stress scale evaluates symptoms of chronic, 
non-specific arousal, including difficulty relaxing, 
heightened nervous tension, and tendencies to 
become easily upset, irritable, overreactive, or 
impatient.
Each item is rated on a 4-point Likert scale, reflecting the 
extent to which the statement applied to the respondent 
over the past week:
  • 0 = Did not apply to me at all (never).
  • 1 = Applied to me to some degree or some of the 
time (sometimes).
  • 2 = Applied to me to a considerable degree or a good 
part of the time (often).
  • 3 = Applied to me very much or most of the time 
(almost always).
Each of the three DASS-21 scales contains seven items, 
which are divided into subscales with similar content. 
Scores for depression, anxiety, and stress are calculated 
by summing the scores of the relevant items:
  • D (depression), including: 3, 5, 10, 13, 16, 17, and 21.
  • A (anxiety), including: 2, 4, 7, 9, 15, 19, and 20.
  • S (stress), including: 1, 6, 8, 11, 12, 14, and 18.
To calculate the final score, each DASS-21 subscale 
score must be multiplied by 2. The resulting scores for 
depression, anxiety, and stress are then categorized into 
five severity levels: normal, mild, moderate, severe, and 
extremely severe, as follows:
  • “Normal” is defined as D: 0–9, A: 0–7, S: 0–14.
  • “Mild” severity is defined as D: 10–13, A: 8–9, S: 
15–18.
  • “Moderate” severity is defined as D: 14–20, A: 10–14, 
S: 19–25.

Clinical assessment
NYHA classification  In the present study, the NYHA 
classification will be assessed at baseline and after the 
60-day intervention by a cardiologist. The cardiologist 
assigns participants to one of four functional classes, 
ranging from no physical limitation during activity (class 
I) to symptoms present at rest (class IV). Participants with 
an NYHA classification of class II or III at the study’s start 
will be included.
Edema The cardiologist will assess edema using the pit -
ting test, which involves gently pressing a finger on the 
medial malleolus, the bony portion of the tibia, and the 
dorsum of the foot for 5 to 15 s. After releasing the pres -
sure, a dimple (pit) will appear in the skin, indicating the 
presence and extent of edema. The edema grading scale 
measures the rebound after the pitting test and is as fol -
lows: Grade 1: immediate rebound with a 2 mm pit (+ 1); 
Grade 2: rebound in less than 15 s with a 3 to 4 mm pit 
(+ 2); Grade 3: rebound greater than 15 s but less than 60 s 
with a 5 to 6 mm pit (+ 3); and Grade 4: rebound between 
2 and 3 min with an 8 mm pit (+ 4).

Arterial stiffness assessment

performed in the supine position, with the head turned 
45° away from the side being scanned. Pulse wave veloc -

side of each participant. Blood pressure will be measured 
after at least 15 min of rest on the right upper arm. Addi -

Electrocardiogram (ECG)
All ECGs will be performed in accordance with the stan -
dard operating procedure. In brief, patients will be in a 
resting, supine position for at least 5 min before the 
ECG is recorded, with the electrodes placed in the stan -
dard anatomical positions. During the measurements, 
patients will be instructed to remain still and relaxed. The 
recorded 12-lead ECGs will be analyzed using a medical 
ECONET device. The PR interval will be measured as the 
time from the beginning of the P wave to the beginning 
of the QRS complex; the QT interval will be calculated 
from the beginning of the QRS complex to the end of the 
T wave; and the Tp-e interval will be measured from the 
peak to the end of the T wave. The QTc interval will be 
calculated using Bazett’s formula [QTc = QT/√(RR inter -
val)]. P wave dispersion will be calculated as the abso -
lute difference between the maximum and minimum P 
wave durations, QT dispersion as the absolute difference 
between the maximum and minimum QT intervals, and 
T wave dispersion as the absolute difference between the 
maximum and minimum T wave durations. The frontal 
QRS-T angle will be calculated as the absolute difference 
between the QRS axis (ventricular depolarization) and T 
axis (ventricular repolarization). If the result is greater 
than 180°, it will be corrected by subtracting it from 360°. 
Additionally, the QRS duration will be measured.
Echocardiography assessment
Echocardiography will be performed to evaluate the 
mechanisms related to standard cardiac structure and 
function assessment. Transthoracic cardiac ultrasound 
will be conducted using a Samsung Medison SonoAce 
R7 Ultrasound Machine. A single expert cardiologist 
will be blinded to intervention/control groups and per -
form all measurements, and all images will be analyzed 
by one operator who is blinded to the groups, too. The 
parameters to be measured include: LVEF, left ventricu -
lar end-diastolic volume (LVEDV), left ventricular end-
systolic volume (LVESV), diastolic dysfunction (DD), and 
the ratio of early diastolic mitral inflow velocity to early 
diastolic mitral annulus velocity (E/e’ ratio), as a marker 
of left ventricular diastolic pressure.
Exercise tolerance assessment
In the present study, exercise tolerance of the participants 
will be assessed using the 2-Minute Step Test (2MST). To 
perform the 2MST, the number of steps completed in 
place within two minutes will be recorded. Upon hearing 
the “start” command, participants will begin marching in 
place, raising their knees to the appropriate height (mid -
way between the patella and iliac crest). Participants will 
be instructed to complete as many steps as possible while 
ensuring their knees reach the predefined height within 
a two-minute time frame. Before the actual test, par -
ticipants will be allowed to practice a few steps to refine 
their technique and confirm their ability to complete the 
task. For safety, participants will be allowed to hold onto 
a stable table to maintain balance during the test. Par -
ticipants are free to march at their own pace, with the 
option to slow down or pause if necessary, before resum -
ing until the test is completed. The researcher will record 
the number of completed steps, inform participants of 
the remaining time, and encourage them to achieve their 
best possible performance. The final test result will be 
the number of steps in which the right knee reaches or 
exceeds the predefined height [53].
To facilitate a large number of assessments—includ -
ing anthropometric measurements, biochemical tests, 
echocardiography, arterial stiffness, ECG, physical func -
tion evaluations, and multiple questionnaires—these 
procedures will be coordinated with participants’ routine 
clinical care visits, such as cardiology follow-ups, echo -
cardiography appointments, and scheduled blood tests. 
This strategy minimizes additional visits, travel, and time 
burden, thereby enhancing recruitment feasibility, adher-
ence to the intervention, and would also strengthen the 
trial’s pragmatic relevance.

Data monitoring and oversight
Given the minimal risk associated with the interven -

Statistical analysis
All analyses will be conducted using the Intention-to-
Treat (ITT) method. Thus, all participants who entered 
the study (regardless of whether they completed the 
60-day study period) will be included in the analysis. 
Missing data will be replaced by multiple imputations. 
Statistical analyses will be performed using the Statistical 
Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS) software, Version 
24 (SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA). A Q-Q plot and the 
Shapiro-Wilk test will be applied to assess the normality 
of the continuous variables. Also, continuous variables 
will be presented as mean ± SD or median (25–75 inter -
quartile range) for normally distributed or skewed data, 
respectively, and categorical variables will be presented 
as percentages. Additionally, the chi-square test will 
be used to compare categorical variables. The One-way 
ANOVA or Kruskal-Wallis test will be applied to com -
pare continuous variables between groups. In contrast, 
the paired sample t-test or Wilcoxon rank-sum test will 
be used for within-group comparisons. To compare dif -
ferences in primary and secondary outcomes between 
the three study groups at the end of the trial and adjust 
for potential confounders, the mixed linear effects model 
will be applied. Furthermore, between-group differences 
will be assessed by ANCOVA adjusting for baseline val -
ues. Additionally, we plan to use regression models to 
adjust for potential confounding variables that may dif -
fer across the three groups. In addition, subgroup analy -
ses will be conducted based on diabetic status and BMI 
(if heterogeneity exists). To correct for multiple com -
parisons (among the predefined secondary outcomes) 
and reduce the risk of Type I error, p-values will be cal -
culated using the Bonferroni method (Bonferroni correc -
tion). Moreover, A Per-Protocol (PP) analysis will also be 
undertaken to complement and contrast the Intent-to-
Treat (ITT) results, and will serve as a sensitivity analysis. 
Statistical significance will be defined as p-values < 0.05.

Discussion
HF represents a significant public health challenge, with 
high morbidity and mortality rates [ 7]. Overweight and 
obesity can be seen as a “perfect storm” for cardiovascu -
lar disease (CVD), serving as a leading cause of HF [ 55]. 
Page 15 of 18
Rahbarinejad et al. Nutrition Journal           (2026) 25:15 
Furthermore, due to its metabolic and inflammatory 
abnormalities associated with obesity, obesity further 
complicates HF treatment, particularly in patients with 
HFrEF [ 56, 57]. Additionally, disruptions in circadian 
rhythm, poor diet, intestinal microbial dysbiosis, and 
fluctuations in NT-proBNP levels can play pivotal roles 
in the pathogenesis of HF. Moreover, the function of the 
gut is fundamentally altered in patients with HF. Dimin -
ished intestinal blood flow, resulting from decreased 
cardiac output, correlates with both the aggravation of 
gut dysbiosis and the severity of HF [ 58]. These findings 
have emerged GHA, defined as a bidirectional connec -
tion between gut microbiota and cardiovascular health 
[59], as a key area of research for diagnosing and treat -
ing the HF population [ 58]. While the management of 
both HF and obesity is interconnected on multiple levels, 
most recent HF clinical trials have focused on medica -
tions and devices and neglected lifestyle modifications, 
dietary interventions, or the modification of intestinal 
microbiota [ 57, 60]. To the best of our knowledge, no 
clinical trial has examined the effect of combining an 
IF diet with probiotic supplementation in patients with 
HFrEF. We hypothesized that the co-administration of 
SD + 10-hour TRE, alone or in combination with probi -
otic supplementation, in overweight and obese patients 
with HFrEF would lead to weight loss and improvements 
in metabolic, hormonal, and echocardiographic markers 
compared to control individuals.
A growing body of research has demonstrated that diet 
has a significant influence on the GHA, a key factor in 
determining cardiovascular health [ 61, 62]. For example, 
previous studies have demonstrated the favorable effects 
of IF as an intervention strategy, including the restora -
tion of gene expression involved in the body’s circadian 
clock [ 63], improvement in intestinal microbial compo -
sition, regulation of metabolism [ 63], as well as reduc -
tions in blood pressure [ 64], body weight [ 64], body fat 
index [64], fasting blood glucose [ 65], and lipid oxidation 
[65], as well as a reduction in insulin resistance [ 66]. In 
addition, IF improves walking speed [ 67], mental and 
physical function [ 67], and sleep quality, while reducing 
inflammation markers such as TNF-α and IL-1β [65]. The 
INTERFAST-MI Trial, conducted among patients with 
ST-segment elevation myocardial infarction (STEMI), 
revealed that TRE (8:16) was safe and improved left 
ventricular (LV) function over 4 weeks, with further 
improvements observed up to 6 months post-myocardial 
infarction (MI) [ 42]. Compared with a diet without a 
fixed fasting regimen, TRE also reduced diastolic blood 
pressure and BMI [ 42]. In the mentioned study, TRE 
reduced serum NT-proBNP concentrations compared to 
the control group, but this difference was not statistically 
significant [42].
In addition to the timing of eating, the types of food 
consumed have received considerable attention in recent 
studies. Frequent consumption of ultra-processed foods 
is associated with excessive calorie intake, weight gain, 
metabolic syndrome, cancer, and cardiovascular dis -
eases [ 68]. High-fat diets, on the other hand, are linked 
to changes in the composition of gut microbiota towards 
the predominance of pro-inflammatory bacteria to 
beneficial bacteria [ 61]. In contrast, diets rich in fiber, 
plant-based foods, and prebiotics promote the growth of 
beneficial gut bacteria, such as Lactobacilli spp., which 
have been associated with positive cardiovascular out -
comes [ 69]. Furthermore, gut bacteria function as a 
metabolic organ, influencing the host’s metabolism and 
energy balance [70]. The health outcomes of a diet, along 
with its low environmental impact, affordability, acces -
sibility, and alignment with socio-cultural preferences, 
should all be considered [ 71, 72]. Due to these factors, a 
SD—similar to the Mediterranean diet but with a stron -
ger focus on environmental impact—would be the best 
choice for intervention programs aimed at promoting 
sustainable behaviors [ 73]. SD is linked to the prolifera -
tion of butyrate-producing bacteria, such as Akkerman-
sia muciniphila, in the gut microbiota that are associated 
with anti-inflammatory effects, improved regulation of 
glucose and lipid profiles, increased glucose sensitivity, 
reduced insulin resistance, and regulation of blood pres -
sure [71, 74].
In a previous study, supplementation with Saccharo-
myces boulardii improved LVEF among HF patients [ 75]. 
To the best of our knowledge, only one study examined 
the effect of synbiotic therapy on NT-proBNP levels in 
HF patients. This study found that 10 weeks of daily syn -
biotic administration significantly reduced NT-proBNP 
levels; however, no significant differences were observed 
in high-sensitivity C-reactive protein (hsCRP) levels or 
blood pressure values [ 27]. Other benefits of probiot -
ics in terms of improving cardiometabolic health can be 
exerted through supporting weight management, partic -
ularly when combined with IF or other calorie-restricted/
healthy diets. If successful, this intervention may provide 
nutritionists in clinical settings with a new eating pattern 
(SD + TRE), either alone or in combination with probi -
otic supplements, as a safe and effective treatment for 
patients with HF.
Limitations
The limitations of this study are addressed as follows: 
First, while double-blinding is feasible and implemented 
for the probiotic versus control groups, blinding par -
ticipants to the dietary intervention (SD + TRE) is inher -
ently unfeasible. Second, this is a single-center study 
with an estimated sample size, which may limit the gen -
eralizability of the findings. Nevertheless, generating 
Page 16 of 18
Rahbarinejad et al. Nutrition Journal           (2026) 25:15 
proof-of-concept data is clinically and ethically essential 
before planning larger-scale interventions. Should the 
results from this study prove promising, a multicenter 
trial will be planned to validate the findings. Third, due 
to financial and practical limitations, 16  S rRNA analy -
sis is not possible to perform, limiting our ability to fully 
characterize gut microbiota composition. Fourth, due to 
the lack of evidence from studies with similar interven -
tions and patient populations, the study duration is set at 
60 days. This decision was based on findings from previ -
ous studies—which, although not directly related to the 
intervention or patient population—along with consid -
eration of the common duration of lifestyle modification 
studies to enhance patient adherence. Consequently, it is 
possible that changes in some outcomes, may not reach 
clinical significance within this timeframe. Fifth, adher -
ence to the TRE schedule may pose a challenge; to miti -
gate this, participants will receive regular reminders via 
phone calls and instant messages indicating the start and 
end times of their eating window. Finally, loss to follow-
up is anticipated across all three study groups; to address 
this, a 20% dropout rate has been incorporated into the 
sample size calculation.
