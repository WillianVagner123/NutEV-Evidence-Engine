Abstract
Background  
The proliferation of fast-food outlets (FFOs) in residential areas has 
raised growing concerns about their impact on community health, safety, 
and wellbeing. This qualitative study examined both pre-
development community consultation submissions and post-
development resident experiences surrounding the development of a 
McDonald’s outlet in a newly established suburb on the urban fringe of 
Perth, Western Australia (WA).  
Methods 
Using a qualitative descriptive design, the study analyzed 183 community 
submissions lodged with the local council prior to development and 
conducted semi-structured interviews with 11 residents living 
within 300 meters of the outlet after construction.  
Results 
Content analysis revealed strong opposition, with 168 (92%) submissions 
objecting to the development. Key concerns included health, FFO density,

proximity to schools, traffic congestion, safety, pollution (noise, litter, 
light, odor), antisocial behavior, neighborhood amenity and property 
devaluation. Despite these concerns, all objections were dismissed by the 
council on planning grounds. Post-development interviews revealed 
that residents’ lived experiences reflected all of the very issues they had 
predicted, expressing frustration with poor council transparency and 
perceived apathy.  
Conclusion 
The findings reveal a clear disconnect between what communities want and 
what planning systems deliver, highlighting the need for planning reform in 
WA that prioritizes public health through exclusion zones, 
density restrictions and mandatory health impact assessments. As WA 
councils prepare to implement mandated public health plans under the 
Public Health Act 2016, these findings provide timely evidence 
to strengthen community engagement and support health-promoting urban 
planning.

Our modern food environment has become increasingly saturated with fast 
food outlets (FFO) (1), particularly in disadvantaged areas surrounding 
schools (2, 3). This is of considerable concern given the positive association 
between FFO around schools and increased purchasing of discretionary 
foods (4, 5) and increased prevalence of obesity (6). The continued 
proliferation of these outlets therefore represents a major challenge for 
communities seeking to promote healthier environments for children.
Despite strong community opposition to new FFO developments (7) and 
repeated calls for local, state and national governments to prioritize 
healthy, livable and sustainable cities (8-10), expansion continues at pace. 
In 2023 alone, 227 new Fast Food and Quick Service Restaurant chains 
opened in Australia, including 25 new McDonald’s outlets (11). McDonald’s 
has explicitly stated its goal of ensuring most Australians live within a five 
minute drive of one of its stores (1). This corporate expansion strategy 
highlights the tension between commercial interests and public health 
goals, a tension that plays out most visibly within local planning systems 
and demonstrates how the commercial determinants of health leverage 
influence within systems, practices and pathways of our residential 
suburbs.(12)
Local Government Authorities (an equivalent internationally would be called 
a district, county, borough or municipality and is herein referred to as the 
local council or council) are central to these planning and development

processes. Councils manage statutory consultations, interpret planning 
instruments, and provide advice or determinations on development 
applications. However their ability to reject FFO developments is often 
constrained by state planning frameworks, which can limit the extent to 
which public health concerns are considered (13). From June 2026, all WA 
Local Governments will be mandated under the Public Health Act 2016 
(WA) (14) to produce public health plans, formally embedding community 
health into their remit. Understanding how these councils balance planning 
legislation, public health responsibilities and community expectations is 
increasingly important for shaping future policy responses. 
Public consultation has become a key mechanism through which councils 
engage communities in planning decisions (15). However, once 
developments are approved and constructed, opportunities to capture 
resident’s lived experiences and reflections are rare. Much research has 
mapped the proliferation of FFO (16-18), yet the personal experiences of 
community members most affected by these developments remain absent 
from academic literature. Exploring these firsthand accounts can provide 
valuable insights into the social and environmental consequences of FFO 
expansion, as well as the perceived shortcomings of current planning 
processes.  
Methods

ARTICLE IN PRESSARTICLE IN PRESS
ECU Internal Information
This study aimed to explore the experiences and perceptions of residents 
living in close proximity to a newly developed McDonald’s outlet, and to 
examine the community consultation submissions lodged with the local 
council prior to its construction. By analyzing both pre-development 
objections and post-development lived experiences, this study provides 
novel insights into how corporate expansion strategies intersect with local 
planning processes, and how residents perceive the health, social, and 
environmental impacts of FFOs in their neighborhoods.
This study employed a qualitative descriptive design incorporating two data 
sources: (i) a document analysis of community consultation submissions to 
the local council prior to the development of a McDonald’s FFO; and (ii) 
semi-structured interviews with residents living in close proximity to the 
outlet following its completion. The Consolidated Criteria for Reporting 
Qualitative Research (COREQ) guided the reporting of this study. (19)
Setting
A McDonald’s FFO was built on the urban fringe of Perth, WA, in December 
2023. With a dual lane drive through, on an area of 2,581m2 and co-located 
with a 7-Eleven service station, the McDonald’s abuts a residential street, is 
550m from the local high school (students 13 to 18 years), 600m from an 
early learning center (children aged up to five years) and 950m from the 
nearest primary school (students from five to twelve years). 
Sampling and Recruitment
Community Consultation

The local council advertised the development application for community 
consultation on 23 December 2021, to conclude on 14 January 2022. The 
normal period of consultation is 14 days however, due to the 'holiday period' 
and an additional request from council, the consultation period was 
extended to 21 days. Advertising of the consultation was by way of notice in 
the local newspaper and writing to 271 nearby landowners/occupiers within 
approximately 350 meters of the proposed development. The development 
plans and all supporting documentation were also published on the council's 
website. Residents were invited to submit comments on the planning 
proposal using an online form and submissions were publicly available for 
download upon closure of the submission period. (20) The FFO opened on 9 
December 2023.
Resident Interviews
A total of 3,495 residents living within a 3km radius of the new FFO 
development were purposively sampled through a postal survey of a larger 
study which included an invitation to participate in a follow-up interview. 
The purpose of the larger umbrella study was to explore the impact of the 
FFO development on the health of residents living near to the FFO and 
included questions on dietary intake, food purchasing behaviors, food and 
taste preferences and various perceptions of the neighborhood. A total of 
171 adults provided informed consent and completed the survey, and 
residents living within 300m of the new FFO development that responded 
positively to the follow-up interview invitation formed the sample for this

present study (n=11). These 11 participants were emailed a participant 
information form, which outlined the process of the qualitative study 
interview, study approval details and informed consent process. They then 
completed an online consent form and were subsequently contacted via 
email or telephone to arrange a convenient time for interview. Interviews

development on their community, but were otherwise unknown to

Data collection
Community Consultation
The schedule of community submissions was downloaded on 6 June 2023 
from the planning Website (20) for categorizing and interpretation.
Resident Interviews
A semi-structured interview guide consisting of eight questions was 
developed for this study by a team of researchers with expertise in 
nutrition, food environments and qualitative research methods (see 
Supplementary File 1). The guide explored residents’ perspectives of the 
new McDonald’s outlet approval process, construction and subsequent 
impact. Participants also provided demographic and personal information, 
including gender, presence of school-aged children in the home, and 
frequency of accessing the new McDonald’s. Individual interviews were

conducted by CW and JB between January and May 2024. Study 
participants were given the option of a video conference or telephone 
interview. Interviews ranged between 10 and 40 minutes and were recorded 
and transcribed with participants’ permission. Data collection was 
conducted in parallel with data analysis, with recruitment ceasing once 
interviews stopped generating new codes.
Data Analysis
Interview transcripts were analyzed with the assistance of the qualitative 
research software package, QSR NVivo, Version 12. Qualitative content 
analysis was undertaken to capture participants’ findings in their own 
words (21). A research team member familiarized themselves with the data 
by re-reading transcripts and developing initial codes. Open coding was 
used to assign the data to codes, which were then refined to create sub-
themes and themes. Themes and sub-themes were discussed with the co-
authors before a consensus was reached. 
Community consultation submissions were analyzed independently of the 
interview data using a similar qualitative content analysis approach. Key 
issues from the community submissions were coded inductively to identify 
recurrent themes. These themes and sub-themes were then compared with 
the qualitative interview findings to explore areas of overlap and 
divergence. If a submission contained more than one issue, then each issue 
was coded and analyzed independently.

2.1. Approval Process and Construction
Residents’ perspectives on the FFO approval process and construction were 
divided into two categories: i) mode of communication; and ii) perception of 
council.
Mode of Communication 
Residents learnt that a FFO was being constructed in their neighborhood 
via three modes of communication: i) community word-of-mouth or social 
media; ii) local council communication; and iii) construction site signage.

Some participants noted that they had not received any formal notification 
from the council that construction of a FFO had been proposed or approved, 
instead learning about the development via word-of-mouth or social media: 
We hadn't moved into the home yet because we were still under 
construction…. so we didn't find out until we saw it on a Facebook 
page. (Participant #10) 
Only some residents reported receiving direct communication from the 
council via a leaflet in their letterbox or during a council meeting where 
support and objections were discussed. Participants also noted 
discrepancies between the information they received about commercial 
developments when purchasing their property and the eventual outcome:
When we signed the contract for the land, we asked what it was likely 
to be and were told a doctors or a physio or cafe, something like that, 
so when the information came through from [named] Council about the 
objections and submitting our grievances, we were just as surprised as 
everybody else in the estate. (Participant #1)
For other participants, the first indication of the FFO presence was the 
appearance of signage on the construction site:
We found out when we just saw the buildings, so when we saw the 
McDonald's sign being put up. (Participant #8)
Perceptions of Council

Participants’ perceptions of how the council managed the outlet’s approval 
process and development were predominantly negative and included: i) 
disappointment in council conduct; and ii) perceived council apathy. 
Many residents expressed disappointment in the council’s perceived 
disregard for residents’ objections. Participants noted that some residents 
were unaware of the FFO and were not given an opportunity to object to the 
development as their homes were still under construction:
I was just disappointed in [named] council’s blatant disregard for what 
the residents had to say. I mean, there was so few of us to contest it.  
(Participant #1)
Some participants also reported perceived apathy from council 
representatives, who participants felt were indifferent to community 
objections:
I was kind of impartial, but if my say actually mattered, I would say 
maybe no [to the fast-food outlet], but I knew they were gonna[sic] do 
what they were gonna[sic] do regardless. (Participant #9)
One participant also expressed their disappointment about the changing 
nature of the neighborhood given the council’s support of FFO: 
I just really, really feel very negative about how this neighborhood is 
turning into a fast-food hub. (Participant #10)

Residents’ perspectives on the impact of the operational FFO on the local 
community were divided into five categories: i) health; ii) location; iii) 
safety; iv) pollution; and v) finances.
Health
Many participants spoke about the real and perceived health impacts of 
the FFO on community members. Some participants expressed concern 
about the long-term physical health effects of eating unhealthy food from 
the FFO, particularly for school children who now have greater access to 
unhealthy food options:
Have we not got a duty of care to look after the children? It's ultimately 
going to have a massive impact on the health system when we've got 
the obesity problem and diabetes and they're all having to attend 
clinics, because [fast food] is cheap. (Participant #1)
In addition to the long-term impacts on children’s health and the larger 
health care system, one participant noted the short-term, negative health 
impact of eating unhealthy food:
It's really unhealthy and it makes you feel disgusting after. (Participant 
#7)
Participants also noted that the presence of the FFO was inconsistent 
with their expectations of a healthy, family-friendly neighborhood:

We moved to this area because we thought it was going to be a great 
area to raise a family, raise kids. It's extremely disappointing and I'm

In addition, some participants reported experiencing stress caused by 
safety concerns associated with the outlet, particularly regarding the

[Safety] is at the back of my mind, especially with them being able to

yeah, it is a concern.  (Participant #1)

Many participants were disappointed in the location of the FFO,

The financial impact of the FFO was also noted by participants,

Discussion
This is the first known study of both community consultation submissions 
and residents’ lived experiences following the development of a McDonald’s 
FFO in their neighborhood. While previous research has explored how new 
FFOs influence eating behaviors (22), none have examined this issue from 
the dual perspective of pre-development objections and post-development 
realities. By combining these data sources, this study offers unique insights 
into the disconnect between local decision-making processes and 
community health priorities.
Despite 168 (92%) formal submissions opposing the FFO development, the 
Council dismissed all objections as being outside of valid planning 
considerations. The perceived lack of responsiveness by Council, developers 
and the McDonald’s corporation was a central concern among residents,

who described feeling ignored and powerless. This finding mirrors national 
reports of similar developments in which local authorities have overridden 
community opposition (23, 24), reinforcing a broader pattern of community 
disempowerment in urban planning decisions. The contrast between 
residents’ pre-development concerns, such as outlet density, pollution, 
safety and children’s health, and their post-development lived experiences 
underscores the tangible impacts of planning decisions on daily life.
The residential area in this study was purported to be a highly rated ‘green 
star’ sustainable community, designed to promote walkability, family-
friendly living and access to schools and recreational spaces. Residents 
viewed the introduction of a 24-hour McDonald’s outlet as fundamentally 
misaligned with this vision, citing noise, litter, odor, traffic congestion and 
safety concerns as evidence of policy incoherence. Rather than contributing 
to community wellbeing, the FFO was perceived as diminishing the area’s 
livability and undermining its sustainability goals. This highlights the 
growing gap between policy rhetoric promoting healthy, sustainable 
neighborhoods and planning frameworks that continue to facilitate the 
establishment of unhealthy food environments.
Under the WA Public Health Act 2016 (14) local governments are required 
to develop Public Health Plans aimed at building safe, sustainable, livable, 
healthy connected communities. Yet, the findings from this study reveal a 
striking contradiction between these legislative aims and the practical

Consequently, even well-intentioned councils may be unable to act on 
residents’ health concerns. This policy misalignment demonstrates how 
current planning systems can inadvertently perpetuate health inequities by 
prioritizing corporate and economic interest over local wellbeing. 
Although residents proposed educational strategies, such as school-based 
programs and parental engagement to mitigate the influence of FFOs, 
education alone cannot address the broader structural drivers at play. 
Residents’ concerns extended beyond dietary behavior to include systematic 
issues of planning failures, lack of consultation, neighborhood safety and 
affordability. These findings align with Pillar 1 of the Nutrition Decadal Plan 
(26), which recognizes that diet-related health outcomes are shaped by 
complex social and environmental determinants, including urban design and 
commercial influence. Accordingly, education should be complemented by 
structural reform that tackles the underlying policy and planning conditions 
which normalize unhealthy food access.
International evidence offers a roadmap for such reform. In England, for 
instance, planning authorities have implemented exclusion zones around 
schools and parks, limited outlet density (including the number of 
consecutive outlets), have controls to minimize amenity impacts (noise,

litter, signage, traffic), and require health impact assessments for takeaway 
developments (27). Similar strategies in Australia could strengthen local 
planning frameworks, empowering councils to balance economic 
development with public health objectives. Adopting these evidence-based 
measures would help reduce the proliferation of FFOs, address community 
concerns and promote healthier, more equitable neighborhoods. 
Limitations
This qualitative study captured the depth and complexity of residents’ lived 
experiences, providing insights that can be difficult to obtain via 
quantitative methods alone. Despite the small sample size (n=11), most 
interviews lasted 30 to 40 minutes, providing rich data that enabled the 
identification of diverse themes and perspectives. However, given the 
proximity of participants to the FFO, it is possible that only those residents 
who were aggrieved by the lack of opposition to the McDonald’s 
development participated in the interviews and those residents who were in 
support were underrepresented. Nevertheless, the overwhelming number of 
objections (168 of 183 submissions) and widespread community resistance 
to similar developments nationally (24, 28, 29) suggest that these 
perspectives are representative of a broader social concern.
Conclusion
This study is the first internationally to document both the anticipated and 
realized experiences of residents living near a multinational FFO. Despite

strong opposition, residents felt ignored by the local Council during the 
consultation process and are now left to live with the ongoing consequences 
of a development they did not want. Notably, residents only reported 
negative impacts post-development, including concerns related to health, 
safety, pollution and property devaluation and described feeling 
disempowered and disregarded by the planning system. Only residents 
living within 300m of the McDonald’s restaurant were interviewed and 
consequently most likely experiencing the negative impacts reported, and 
therefore generalization to other communities is with caution. Nevertheless, 
these findings highlight the urgent need for planning reform that embeds 
health considerations within statutory decision-making, such as the 
introduction of management zones to limit FFO proliferation near schools 
and residential areas. Strengthening legislative and policy levers would 
enable local governments to protect community wellbeing and resist undue 
corporate influence, ensuring that the design of Australian neighborhoods 
supports rather than undermines public health.
