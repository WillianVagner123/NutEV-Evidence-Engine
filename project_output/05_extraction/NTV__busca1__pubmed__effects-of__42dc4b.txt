Abstract 
Purpose 
Personalized nutrition (PN) shows promise for improving cardiometabolic health; 
however, evidence on its summarized methodologies and effectiveness remains limited 
and inconsistent.  This review evaluates the effects of PN interventions on 
cardiometabolic outcomes in adults with overweight or obesity.  
Methods 
We searched five databases for randomized controlled trials (RCTs) published up to 
March 2026. Eligible RCTs included at least one biological component to guide 
personalized dietary advice. Data were pooled using a random -effects meta -analysis, 
with heterogeneity assessed by Cochran’s Q statistic and quantified using I².  Risk of 
bias and certainty of evidence were assessed.  
Results 
Fifteen RCTs  met the inclusion criteria, each comparing PN with standard, non -
personalized dietary interventions. PN implementation varied substantially in the 
quantity and type of individual -level features, algorithms applied, and personalization 
strategies used. No significant differences were observed between PN and controls in 
energy intake or macronutrient composition. 46.7% employed specific dietary patterns 
as controls or withheld nutritional counseling. Pooled analyses showed s lightly greater 
reductions in body weight and body fat in the PN group compared with controls, but no 
effects on body mass index, waist circumference, lipid profile, or glycemic markers. 
GRADE certainty ranged from moderate to very low across the assessed outcomes.  
Conclusion 
PN interventions modestly reduce body  weight and body fat . Importantly, the absence 
of a standardized PN framework hampers evaluation and comparison of the 
effectiveness of nutritional personalization strategies.   Future research should aim to 
establish standardized PN definitions, harmonized individual -level features, and 
transparent reporting.

Introduction 
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) was the leading cause of global mortality in 2021 [1], and 36.7 
% of CVD mortality was attributable to dietary risk factors  [2]. Traditionally, dietary 
guidelines have been conveyed to the general population through simple visual aids designed 
to address broad health outcomes [3]. Although population-level dietary guidelines provide 
an essential foundation for public health nutrition, they may not fully account for 
interindividual variability in biological, behavioral, and environmental factors that can 
influence dietary responses  [4, 5]. This variability, compounded by broader sociocultural 
influences, poses significant challenges to advancing diet -based strategies for CVD 
prevention and care.  In response, the concept of personalized nutrition (PN) has emerged, 
extending the principles of precision medicine through advanced biological and technological 
tools to tailor dietary recommendations to individual-level features [5].  
A universally accepted definition of PN remains elusive, with the concept being described in 
various ways across the literature. Terms such as "precision nutrition," "individualized 
nutrition," and "nutritional genomics" are often used interchangeably, reflecting overlapping, 
yet distinct, frameworks [6, 7] . Despite these terminological differences, the primary 
objective of all these approaches is consistent: to address individual variability by integrating 
diverse, multi-dimensional data in order to characterize an individual’s health status, assess 
disease risk, and deliver personalized dietary recommendations aimed at optimizing health 
outcomes. Over the past two decades, research in PN has increasingly focused on integrating 
lifestyle factors with biomedical parameters , chiefly genetic and blood biomarkers, to 
personalize dietary advice [4]. More recently, the inclusion of microbiome profiling has 
expanded the scope of genotype -based analyses [4, 8] . Parallel to these developments, 
advances in biomarker discovery, the proliferation of wearable technologies, and innovations 
in noninvasive monitoring devices have dramatically increased the volume and real -time 
nature of data available for personalizing dietary advice. Hitherto, the application of artificial 
intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) has advanced PN by integrating complex health

data to predict individual metabolic responses and to generate and deliver highly specific, 
data-driven dietary and lifestyle recommendations [9]. 
Although PN shows potential for improving cardiometabolic outcomes through personalized 
approaches [9, 10], the current body of evidence remains limited, and existing findings are 
often inconsistent or inconclusive . For instance, the ZOE  2022 algorithm-based PN, which  
integrates food characteristics, postprandial glucose and triglyceride  responses to foods , 
microbiome composition, cardiovascular disease risk, and health history, has demonstrated 
improvements in body weight, waist circumference, HbA1c, and microbiome diversity [11]. 
Conversely, the PREVENTOMICS study, which employed a PN framework based on five 
core physiological processes —lipid metabolism, carbohydrate metabolism, systemic 
inflammation, oxidative stress, and microbiota status —did not show significant reductions in 
fat mass or improvements in lipid profiles or insulin sensitivity compared to control arm [12]. 
Furthermore, a recent review found that PN interventions with dietary counseling improved 
CVD risk factors in at -risk individuals [10]. However, the review did not outline minimum 
requirements for which individual -level features constitute PN in the included trials, leaving 
the quality and strength of evidence uncertain.  In practice, interventions labeled  as PN can 
range from basic tailoring based on self -reported dietary habits to advanced algorithms that 
integrate genetic, metabolic, or microbiome data. Establishing minimum requirements helps 
distinguish PN from general dietary advice and enables more reliable comparisons of its 
cardiometabolic effects across trials.  In addition, no meta -analysis of PN in overweight or 
obese populations has yet been conducted,  restricting the ability to translate current findings 
into actionable dietary guidelines and policy recommendations.  To address th ese gaps, by 
establishing minimum requirements for PN, our systematic review and meta -analysis 
evaluate whether PN interventions lead to greater improvements in anthropometric measures, 
lipid profiles, and glycemic indices compared with control diets, synthesizing evidence from 
randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in overweight and obese adults. 
 
Materials And Methods 
This systematic review was conducted following the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic 
Reviews of Interventions and is reported in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items 
for Systematic Reviews and Meta -Analyses (PRISMA) statement. The study protocol was 
registered with PROSPERO (CRD420251134855). 
Eligibility criteria 
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
The eligibility criteria were developed based on the Population, Intervention, Comparator, 
and Outcome (PICO) framework, as detailed in Supplementary S1 . Eligible RCTs were 
defined as those implementing a personalized dietary intervention tailored to participant 
characteristics, with a comparison group receiving standard care, no intervention, or a non -
personalized dietary consultation. All comparator groups are herein referred to as the “control 
group”. Participants had to be free -living adults (≥18 years) living with overweight or 
obesity, as defined by the criteria used in each individual study. 
Given the absence of a universally accepted definition of PN , we adopted the following 
operational definition: PN aims to maintain or improve health by providing tailored dietary 
and lifestyle recommendations based on genetic, phenotypic, medical history, dietary intake, 
lifestyle behaviors, nutritional status, and the composition of the gut microbiome  [4, 8, 13] . 
Because there is currently no consensus on which and how many individual-level features are 
required, we specified that eligible RCTs must include at least one biological component—
such as a genetic marker (e.g., fat mass and obesity -associated [FTO] gene), a metabolic 
biomarker (e.g., blood glucose, cholesterol), or a gut microbiome profile —to guide the 
development of personalized dietary advice, whether generated by ML algorithms, healthcare 
professionals, or both. This criterion ensures a minimum level of biological personalization, 
distinguishing such interventions from standard dietary recommendations based solely on 
lifestyle or behavioral factors. Additionally, RCTs may incorporate these biological 
components alongside other individual -level characteristics, such as anthropometric 
parameters (e.g., height, weight) and lifestyle factors (e.g., dietary intake, medical history, 
physical activity). 
Search strategy 
A systematic search was conducted across f our electronic databases—PubMed, Scopus, Web 
of Science, and ClinicalTrials.gov—from their inception to March 01, 2026, to identify RCTs 
evaluating the effects of PN on anthropometric outcomes, glycemic control, and lipid 
profiles. In addition, preprint servers such as MedRxiv were screened to capture unpublished 
or ongoing studies, and the reference lists of included articles and relevant systematic reviews 
from the initial search were manually examined . No language restrictions were applied.  The 
complete search strategy, including all terms and search strings, is detailed in 
Supplementary S2. 
Data extraction and collection 
References were imported into a reference management software (Zotero Desktop) for 
eligibility assessment. Following both automatic and manual removal of duplicates, two 
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
investigators (NTHH and NDK) independently screened titles and abstracts, followed by full-
text articles, based on predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria. Discrepancies were 
resolved by consultation with a third reviewer (TQD). 
Data from eligible studies were extracted using a standardized spreadsheet template 
(Microsoft Excel, Microsoft Corp.) and included the following variables: first author, year of 
publication, participant age, total sample size, sex distribution, study country and population, 
study duration, conflicts of interest, funding source, individual -level features (physical 
activity, microbiome profiling, metabolomic profiling, medical history, genetic markers, 
dietary intake, anthropometric parameters), use of ML algorithms, adherence evaluation, use 
of digital applications to support the PN intervention,  energy intake (kcal), macronutrient 
composition (carbohydrate, protein, fat),  and summary statistics for outcome measures at 
baseline and at the end of the intervention. Where available, change-from-baseline values and 
corresponding standard deviations (SDs) for both the PN and control groups were used to 
calculate the overall effect size as mean difference (MD). When outcomes were reported as 
medians with interquartile ranges or means with 95% confidence intervals (CIs), the 
respective means and SDs were estimated using appropriate statistical methods  [14, 15]. If 
essential data were missing and could not be calculated from the reported information, study 
authors were contacted to obtain the required data. Studies were excluded if no data could be 
retrieved. For trials with multiple publications, data were extracted from the report with the 
longest follow -up duration or the available outcomes most relevant to this review . All 
extracted data were independently cross -checked by two investigators, and any discrepancies 
were resolved through consensus. 
Risk of bias assessment 
Risk of bias (RoB) in eligible studies was independently assessed by two investigators ( DDK 
and NVK) using the Cochrane RoB 2.0 tool  [16]. Discrepancies between investigators were 
resolved through discussion with a third reviewer (TQD). RoB assessments were summarized 
and presented using the robvis data visualization tool [17]. 
Data synthesis 
Study and intervention characteristics of all included trials were summarized in tables and 
figures. Significant differences in age, sample size, sex distribution, study duration, and 
changes in caloric and macronutrient intake were assessed using a two -tailed t-test. Prior to 
meta-analysis, all outcome data were standardized to uniform units, with the exception  of 
BMI (kg/m²), body weight (kg), body fat (%), and insulin (µIU/mL), which did not require 
conversion. Glucose values were converted from mmol/L to mg/dL using the facto r 18.02

ARTICLE IN PRESS
(mg/dL = mmol/L × 18.02). Triglyceride  values were converted using the factor 88.57 
(mg/dL = mmol/L × 88.57). Total cholesterol, LDL -C, and HDL -C values were converted 
using the factor 38.67 (mg/dL = mmol/L × 38.67) [18].  
A meta-analysis was performed when quantitative data for an outcome were available from at 
least two studies with sufficiently comparable measures to allow pooling. Given the 
anticipated heterogeneity in populations (e.g., ethnic background, variation in the 
combinations of individual -level features, and the modes of delivery of personalized diets), 
effect sizes were pooled using the inverse -variance method with a random -effects model. To 
account for  both within - and between -study variance, the Sidik –Jonkman estimator with 
Hartung–Knapp adjustment was applied. MDs in outcomes from baseline, along with their 
95% confidence intervals (CIs), were pooled. Statistical heterogeneity was assessed using 
Cochran’s Q (Chi²) test and quantified using the I² statistic; the p -value for heterogeneity 
shown in the forest plots was derived from Cochran’s Q test, with p < 0.10 considered 
statistically significant in accordance with Cochrane Handbook guidance  [19]. I² was 
interpreted as follows: 0 –40% as minimal, 30 –60% as moderate, 50 –90% as substantial, and 
75–100% as considerable heterogeneity [19].  
The performance of the meta -analysis may be limited when only a small number of trials are 
included. Consequently, following the rule of thumb used in small -study effect testing, 
sensitivity analyses were conducted by sequentially removing individual studies and 
recalculating effect sizes, but only when at least ten studies were available for a given 
outcome [20]. Similarly, potential sources of heterogeneity were examined through 
prespecified subgroup analyses, conducted when a minimum of ten studies contributed to the 
outcome [21]. Differences between subgroups were assessed using tests for subgroup 
interaction, and residual heterogeneity (I²) was examined within subgroups.  The subgroup 
factors included key features of PN interventions: physical activity (yes/no), gut microbiome 
profiling (yes/no), metabolomics profiling (yes/no), medical history (yes/no), genetic markers 
(yes/no), dietary intake (yes/no), and anthropometric parameters (yes/no). Additional 
subgroup analyses were conducted based on the use of machine learning algorithms to guide 
PN development (yes/no), adherence evaluation (yes/no), digital applications (yes/no), 
dietary control (specific vs. non -specific dietary patterns), economic status (developed vs. 
developing countries), study duration (≥20 vs. <20 weeks), and study region (Asia vs. non -
Asia).  
In accordance with the Cochrane Handbook, visual inspection of funnel plots and Egger’s test 
were used to assess reporting bias when at least 10 studies were included for a given outcome

[22]. When publication bias was suspected (i.e., funnel plot asymmetry or a significant 
Egger’s test, P < 0.05), the Duval and Tweedie trim-and-fill method was applied. 
Statistical significance was set at two -sided P < 0.05, unless otherwise stated.  Analyses and 
visualization were conducted in RStudio (version 2025.05.0 Build 496; RStudio, Boston, 
MA, USA) using the ggplot2, meta, and metafor packages. 
Certainty of the evidence 
The Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development, and Evaluations (GRADE) 
framework was applied independently by two investigators ( DDK and TTT) to assess the 
certainty of evidence from the meta -analyses [23]. Disagreements were resolved by 
consensus and, if necessary, through consultation with a third reviewer (TQD). The certainty 
of evidence for each outcome was classified into one of four levels: high, moderate, low, or 
very low  [23]. The magnitude of effect was further interpreted for each outcome by 
considering the size and direction of the pooled estimate alongside the certainty of evidence 
and a minimally important difference, and was expressed using GRADE -consistent 
descriptive statements [24]. 
 
Results 
Study selection 
Supplementary S3 illustrates the PRISMA flow diagram. The search yielded 543 unique 
records after removal of duplicates. Following title and abstract screening and supplementary 
hand searching, 5 9 full-text articles were assessed for eligibility. Ultimately, 1 5 studies met 
the inclusion criteria and were included in this review  [11, 12, 25 -37]. Detailed reasons for 
full-text exclusions are provided in Supplementary S4.

The characteristics of the included studies are summarized  in Figure 1A -E and 
Supplementary S5. A total of 15 RCTs, published between 2016 and 2025, were included, 
comprising 2,351 participants. The mean age was 47.2 ± 9.7 years in the PN groups and 4 8.5 
± 9.4 years in the control groups. PN group sizes ranged from 4 to 233 participants, while 
control group sizes ranged from 5 to 177. In terms of sex distribution, 978 participants were 
male and 1,281 were female; one study did not report sex -specific data [36]. Trial duration 
varied by geographic context: studies conducted in developing countries had a longer average 
duration (approximately 30 weeks) compared to those in developed countries (approximately 
21 weeks) . Trials were geographically diverse, with most conducted in the United States 
(n = 5), and others carried out in Europe, Asia, and North America, including India, Canada, 
South Korea, the Netherlands, the Philippines, Romania, Mexico, China, and Poland. Overall, 
no statistically significant differences were observed across studies in age, sample size, sex 
distribution, or study duration. 
We also identified and summarized several methodological features of PN delivery , as 
presented in Figure 1F-J and Supplementary S5. First, 67% of studies assessed participant 
adherence, and 47% incorporated digital applications to support intervention delivery. 
Second, 33% of trials implemented a defined dietary pattern —such as a low -fat diet or 
calorie restriction—as the control condition  [25, 27, 30, 33, 36] , while 13.3% did not offer 
any dietary counseling to control participants  [28, 29] . Third, 40% of studies employed 
machine learning algorithms to generate personalized dietary recommendations, using 
varying combinations of individual-level features. Genetic markers were the most commonly 
used (67%), whereas gut microbiome profiles and medical history were least frequently 
incorporated, each reported in only 13% of trials . Fourth, the most commonly targeted genes 
were APOE, FTO, and GCKR (see Supplementary S6  for details ). Finally, four primary 
approaches to PN implementation were identifie d, classified by the source of

personalization—whether algorithm -based, practitioner -guided, or other . Specifically, 
Approach 1 was reported in one study  [30], Approach 2 in eight studies [27-29, 31, 32, 34 -

Finally, we summarized the changes in total energy and macronutrient intake between the PN

Risk of bias in included trials 
Of the 15 included trials, six (40%) were judged to have an overall low risk of bias, while 
nine (60%) were rated as having some concerns.  The risk of bias assessment across the five 
domains for each included study is presented in Supplementary S8 A, while the overall 
summary of RoB judgments is shown in Supplementary S8B. 
 
Anthropometrics outcomes 
Figure 2 presents the effects of PN on anthropometric outcomes. Eighteen effect sizes were 
pooled for body weight, thirteen for BMI, twelve for body fat, and sixteen for waist 
circumference. The meta -analysis showed that PN interventions significantly reduced body 
weight (MD: –1.27 kg; 95% CI: –2.38 to –0.16; p = 0.03 ; I² = 94.3%) and body fat 
percentage (MD: –0.44%; 95% CI: –0.76 to –0.12; p = 0.0 1;  I² = 78.9%). In contrast, 
reductions in BMI (MD: –0.35 kg/m²; 95% CI: –0.92 to 0.22; p = 0.21; I² = 90.5%) and waist 
circumference (MD: –1.09 cm; 95% CI: –2.73 to 0.55 ; p = 0 .18; I² = 90.8%) were not 
statistically significant.

Subgroup analyses 
The results of the subgroup analyses are presented in Supplementary S9. For body weight, 
no significant subgroup differences were identified, although significant reductions were 
observed in studies that did not incorporate anthropometric parameters, medical history, or 
microbiome profiling as guiding features. For BMI, no subgroup category showed a 
significant pooled reduction, although between -subgroup differences were observed for 
digital applications, metabolomic profiling, and genetic markers. For body fat, significant 
subgroup differences were observed for economic status, digital applications, and 
metabolomic profiling. Significant reductions in body fat were observed in studies conducted 
in Asia or developing countries and in interventions lasting fewer than 20 weeks. Similar 
reductions were also seen in trials that did not use digital applications, dietary intake, medical 
history, microbiome profiling, machine learning algorithms, as well as in studies 
incorporating genetic markers, and trials using non-specific dietary patterns as controls . For 
waist circumference, significant reductions were observed in studies incorporating medical 
history or microbiome information and in interventions lasting fewer than 20 weeks, whereas 
only genetic markers showed evidence of a significant between-subgroup difference.

or the direction of the pooled effects for BMI and waist circumference  (Supplementary 
S10). However, the effect on body weight was no longer statistically significant after omitting 
the study by Caitlin E. Martinez  [30] (MD: –0.98 kg; 95% CI: –2.03 to 0.07; I² = 92.6%).

Visual inspection of funnel plots ( Supplementary S11) showed no evidence of asymmetry, 
and Egger’s test likewise indicated no publication bias for body weight (p = 0. 25), BMI (p = 
0.25), body fat (p = 0. 29), or waist circumference (p = 0. 15). Given the limited number of 
studies—fewer than ten —sensitivity analysis and publication bias assessment were not 
conducted for glycemic and lipid profile outcomes. 
 
GRADE assessment 
According to the GRADE framework, the certainty of evidence was rated as moderate for 
body fat, total cholesterol, and LDL-C; low for body weight, waist circumference, and fasting 
glucose; and very low for BMI, insulin, HbA1c, HDL -C, and triglycerides. In terms of 
magnitude of effect, PN likely results in a slight reduction in body fat percentage and likely 
results in little to no difference in total cholesterol and LDL -C. PN may result in a slight 
reduction in body weight and may result in little to no difference in waist circumference or 
fasting glucose. For BMI, insulin, HbA1c, HDL -C, and triglycerides, the evidence is very 
uncertain. These ratings were downgraded mainly because of inconsistency across studies, 
particularly for outcomes with substantial to very high heterogeneity, and for imprecision in 
outcomes with wide confidence intervals.  The summary of findings for the GRADE 
assessment is available in the Supplementary S12. 
 
Discussion 
Principal findings 
To our knowledge, this is the first meta -analysis to evaluate the effects of PN on 
cardiometabolic biomarkers in adults with overweight or obesity. Based on 15 RCTs 
including 2,351 participants, we identified several key findings. First, PN was associated with 
statistically significant, albeit modest, reductions in body weight and body fat compared with 
control interventions . Second, PN demonstrated no significant effects on BMI, waist 
circumference, lipid profile, or glycemic markers. Third, the included RCTs varied 
considerably in their approaches to PN implementation, particularly in the individual -level 
features considered, the ML algorithms applied, and the strategies developed and delivered 
for dietary personalization. Finally, nearly half of the trials (46.7%) either implemented 
specific dietary patterns or withheld dietar y counseling from the comparison group . Taken

ARTICLE IN PRESS
together, these findings indicate that the current evidence for PN remains mixed across 
outcomes and is shaped by substantial methodological variation across trials. 
 
Challenges in Defining Appropriate Comparators for Personalized Nutrition Trials 
A relevant consideration in interpreting the cardiometabolic findings is the variability in 
comparator conditions across the included RCTs. These control groups were not standardized 
but instead spanned a continuum of intensity, from minimal exposure such as generic dietary 
advice or educational materials to structured regimens involving prescriptive diets and 
guideline-based counseling. This variability is scientifically significant because the nature of 
the comparator fundamentally defines the research question being answered —whether PN 
demonstrates superiority over minimal guidance, or whether the personalization algorithm 
provides incremental benefit beyond an already established, evidence -based lifestyle 
intervention. 
The first level consisted of minimal guidance, where control groups received passive, 
informational materials without active, individualized counseling. This approach included 
providing participants with educational pamphlets, a single leaflet outlining national food -
based dietary guidelines [26, 29, 31, 34], or standard, non-personalized digital resources such 
as online materials and videos  [11, 28, 32]. Examples of such national guidelines include the 
Dietary Guidelines for Chinese Residents  [34], the Nutritional Guidelines for Filipinos  [32],  
the food-based dietary guidelines published by the Netherlands Nutrition Centre  [31], and the 
United States Dietary Guidelines for Americans (2020–2025) [11]. In these RCTs, the control 
condition represented a comparison against usual care or basic health education. The second 
level involved more structured, standard counseling where participants in the control group 
received a specific  or generic diet plan. For example, one control group was prescribed a 
standard balanced diet as part of the established MOVE! weight management program for 
veterans [35], while others were instructed to follow a standardized low -fat diet and received 
group counseling sessions [25, 27, 33, 36]. The third and most intensive level involved high -
intensity standard -care controls , where participants received structured and well -supported 
dietary and behavioral programs that closely approximated the level of intervention delivered 
in the PN arm  [30]. In the PREVENTOMICS trial, for example,  both the PN and control 
groups received easy -to-prepare meal boxes twice weekly, covering approximately 60% of 
total food intake. Notably, the authors reasoned that the control diet was likely healthier than 
the diet followed by participants in the PN group. The difference was that control participants 
received only non-personalized app -based messages [12]. Similarly, in the digital twin –
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
enabled PN trial in type 2 diabetes, control participants received comprehensive standard 
diabetes care, including regular physician visits and structured coaching at quarterly intervals 
[37]. The use of such active and well -supported control groups may have reduced the 
observable incremental effect of personalization in some trials, particularly for 
cardiometabolic outcomes , as the standard care provided was already a high -quality 
intervention [12, 25, 26, 32, 35]. 
Taken together, variability in the heterogeneity and intensity of control arms may have 
contributed to differences in observed effect sizes across trials.  Clearer differentiation and 
standardization of comparator types are needed in future trials to enable valid interpretation 
of effect sizes, synthesis of evidence, and assessment of the true added value of PN beyond 
standard dietary interventions. 
 
Divergent Strategies for Personalized Nutrition Delivery Across Trials 
The implementation of PN across the 15 studies revealed notable differences in how 
personalization was applied, presenting distinct challenges and offering insights for future 
research. First, both the type and quantity of individual-level features employed to inform PN 
advice differed markedly across studies. Some trials, such as Food4Me and MyGeneMyDiet, 
tailored dietary recommendations using genetic and metabolic data to provide individualized 
nutrition guidance  [26, 32] . However, other studies, like those using CGM -based 
personalization, provided feedback primarily based on participants' real -time metabolic 
responses to food  [11, 25, 29, 30] . Additional approaches incorporated microbiome profiles 
[11, 25] , anthropometrics [25, 26, 31, 32, 34, 37] , physical activity  [25, 26, 31 -33, 37] , 
medical history  [11, 25] , and dietary habits  [11, 26, 31, 32, 34, 37]  as guiding features . 
Notably, the number of each type of individual -level feature included in algorithmic models 
also varies considerably. For instance, as shown in Supplementary S6, Celis-Morales et al. 
and Jianheng Zheng et al. included only a single FTO gene [26, 34] , whereas Mary S. 
McCarthy et al. incorporated 80 genes, and Mona A. Aldubayan et al. used 47 genes  [12, 28]. 
This diversity reflects the absence of common criteria for selecting and integrating 
individual-level features, making it difficult to determine which personalization strategies 
yield meaningful effects or to synthesize findings across trials. In fact, one RCT suggests that 
the impact of the genotypic results may have been diluted by the large volume of other 
information provided  [26]. This challenge is compounded by the inconsistency of reports 
across commercial nutrigenetic companies and the high cost of omics technologies, which 
further complicates the research landscape [35, 38] . Moreover, this lack of standardization 
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
obscures mechanistic insights into how individual -level features interact with diet to shape 
cardiometabolic health. Future research should provide explicit justificat ion for the selection 
of biological and behavioral features and undertake systematic comparisons of 
personalization strategies to identify approaches that generate the most robust evidence and 
hold the greatest potential for clinical and public health application. 
Second, the delivery mode of PN interventions varied significantly across studies.  Delivery 
modalities ranged from primarily digital, algorithm-driven applications that sent automated 
food-score nudges or decision tree prompts  [11, 25, 34], to hybrid approaches that combined 
apps with scheduled counseling sessions or genotype -based feedback delivered by dietitians 
in person or via telehealth  [32, 37] , and even to resource -intensive models that supplied 
participants with up to 60% of their personalized food intake  [12]. Interventions that layered 
frequent healthcare worker contact—such as weekly counseling, regular coaching calls, or in-
person sessions—typically offered stronger implementation support and enhanced retention.  
However, these designs also introduced interpretive challenges,  as differences in the amount 
of counseling contact between PN and control arms may confound the attribution of effects to 
personalization itself  (e.g., mHealth  pilot and digital -twin studies that combined intensive 
human coaching with sensor feedback) [30, 37]. This raises an interpretive issue: whether the 
observed effects reflect  the personalization of individual -level features through algorithms, 
the intensity of delivery and support, or the interaction of both . Future PN trials will need to 
disentangle these elements by standardizing delivery intensity across arms or explicitly 
testing the added value of personalization beyond the effects of counseling. 
Third, the duration of PN interventions varied substantially, ranging from a few weeks  [12, 
29, 33, 34] to year-long trials [32, 37]. Intervention length may influence whether PN effects 
are observed only in the short term or maintained over longer follow -up. For example, a 12 -
month trial found that PN produced greater weight loss at 3 and 6 months, but this advantage 
was not maintained at 12 months compared with standard care  [37]. A similar pattern was 
observed in a 12 -month study, in which early weight -loss progress was not sustained by the 
end of the trial  [32]. Conversely, improvements in other clinical outcomes, such as HbA1c, 
were observed only in a 12 -month trial, whereas a 24 -week intervention yielded no such 
effects [30, 35] . This suggests that, for any short -term effects of PN on weight -related 
outcomes, longer follow-up is required to evaluate their sustained effects on cardiometabolic 
biomarkers and overall clinical relevance. 
Collectively, PN implementations diverged in what individual-level features informed dietary 
personalization (e.g., genotype, biomarkers, behavioral habits, or multi -omic profiles), how 
ARTICLE IN PRESS
ARTICLE IN PRESS
advice was delivered (e.g., app only, app plus human coaching, or counselor -led), and how 
long and intensively participants were supported. These differences may have contributed to 
variation in efficacy, adherence, and the comparability of trial outcomes. 
Strengths and limitations of the review 
This meta-analysis has several key strengths. All included studies were RCTs, providing 
methodological reliability. The use of predefined inclusion criteria, the GRADE 
approach, and subgroup and sensitivity analyses contributed to transparency in the 
evaluation process.  Most trials were conducted in free -living, remote settings, 
enhancing the generalizability of findings to real -world conditions compared to 
traditional, tightly controlled clinical trials. 
However, several limitations must be acknowledged when interpreting the results. First, 
although subgroup analyses were conducted across multiple study -level 
characteristics, substantial residual heterogeneity remained for body weight, BMI, and 
waist circumference, where residual I² remained high across most subgroup categories. 
For body fat, heterogeneity was reduced in selected subgroups but not consistently 
across all categories, suggesting that the examined variables explained only part of the 
between-study variability. This likely reflects differences in personalization 
methodologies across trials, which are difficult to avoid, as well as additional 
unmeasured or incompletely reported factors beyond the subgroup characteristics 
assessed that may have contributed to the heterogeneity observed in the main 
analyses. Second, restricting the review to peer -reviewed studies published in English 
may have excluded relevant non -English or unpublished evidence.  Third, limited 
reporting on data collection methods a nd algorithms used for personalization reduced 
the ability to assess how PN strategies were designed, implemented, and evaluated. For 
example, one RCT noted that the details of its algorithm could not be disclosed 
because of a pending intellectual property rights application  [12]. Finally, adherence to 
dietary interventions was inconsistently defined, measured, and reported across 
studies, with assessments ranging from self -reported dietary compliance to 
engagement with digital tools or attendance at counseling sessions, while several trials 
provided limited adherence data. As personalized nutrition depends not only on 
intervention design but also on the extent to which participants follow the prescribed

ARTICLE IN PRESS
advice, this variability may contribute to differences in the magnitude of observed 
effects and their interpretation. 
 
Conclusion 
In summary, compared with controls, PN produced greater reductions in body weight and 
body fat but no additional benefits for BMI, waist circumference, lipid, or glycemic markers.  
Variability in the individual-level features guiding personalization, the modes of delivery, and 
the duration and intensity of participant support limits comparability across trials and hinders 
the identification of effective approaches. Currently, PN faces obstacles such as limited 
reproducibility, methodological inconsistencies in study design, and the complexities of 
analyzing and interpreting high-dimensional data. While advances in sequencing technologies 
are expected to lower costs, the absence of a clear, standardized definition of PN and a robust 
framework for its application remain key barriers to its effective integration into clinical 
practice. To improve methodological consistency and interpretability, future research should 
establish consensus definitions of PN and develop classification frameworks that specify 
individual-level features, personalization mechanisms (e.g., algorithmic vs. clinician -guided), 
intervention intensity and duration, the use of well -matched comparative control groups , and 
transparent reporting of data collection methods and personalization algorithms.  In light of 
current evidence, routine clinical implementation of PN cannot be broadly recommended 
until further studies establish which strategies are both effective and scalable for improving 
cardiometabolic health.
