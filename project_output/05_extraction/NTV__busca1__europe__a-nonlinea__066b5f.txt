Abstract
Background Pediatric hypertension is an increasingly recognized health concern with long-term cardiovascular 
implications. Diet quality is a modifiable factor that may influence blood pressure in youth. However, research on the 
association between diet quality and hypertension in Children and Adolescents remains limited. This study aims to 
assess the potential nonlinear association between diet quality, measured by the Healthy Eating Index 2015 (HEI-
2015) score, and the potential threshold effect with hypertension in US Children and Adolescents.
Methods We conducted a cross-sectional study using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination 
Survey (NHANES) 1999–2020. A total of 17,528 participants aged 8–18 years were included after excluding those with 
missing diet or blood pressure data and other ineligible cases. Diet quality was assessed by Healthy Eating Index-
2015(HEI-2015) scores (range 0-100). Multivariable logistic regression was used to evaluate the association between 
HEI-2015 and hypertension. We examined potential non-linear relationships using a two-piecewise linear spline 
approach to identify any threshold effect. Subgroup and sensitivity analyses were performed to assess the robustness 
of the findings.
Results The analysis included 17,528 children and adolescents aged 8–18 years from NHANES 1999–2020. The 
prevalence of hypertension was 6.14% (n = 1,076). The participants were classified according to HEI-2015 tertiles 
(T1:12.36–41.47; T2:41.47–51.28; T3: 51.28–95.65.). Compared with T2 and T3 group, participants in the lowest tertile 
of the HEI-2015, had higher prevalence of hypertension (6.73% vs. 6.23% and 5.56%). Specifically, below an HEI-
2015 score of 65.13, each 10-point increase in HEI-2015 was associated with a 7% lower prevalence of hypertension 
(adjusted OR = 0.93, 95% CI: 0.87–0.99; p = 0.023). In contrast, above this threshold (HEI-2015 ≥ 65.13), each 10-point 
increase was associated with a much higher (52%) prevalence of hypertension(OR = 0.48, 95% CI: 0.28–0.83; p = 0.009).

Introduction
Hypertension in children and adolescents is a significant 
and growing public health concern [ 1, 2]. Once consid -
ered rare, its prevalence has risen alongside childhood 
obesity [3, 4]. According to updated American Academy 
of Pediatrics (AAP) criteria, about 1 in 7 adolescents (12–
19 years old) now have elevated blood pressure or hyper -
tension, with prevalence varying by weight status, 2% in 
healthy-weight youth versus 14% in severe obesity [ 5]. 
This condition is clinically concerning because childhood 
hypertension often persists into adulthood and is linked 
to early cardiovascular risk markers if untreated [ 6, 7]. 
In response, the 2017 AAP guidelines lowered diagnos -
tic thresholds, and emphasized lifestyle intervention as 
a cornerstone of management, including recommenda -
tions for overall dietary improvement for at risk youth [ 8, 
9]. Poor diet quality, characterized by excessive sodium, 
added sugars, saturated fats alongside insufficient intake 
of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, serve as a modifiable 
risk factor for prevalence of hypertension [10, 14].
The Healthy Eating Index-2015 (HEI-2015) provides 
a validated 100-point composite measure of diet qual -
ity [15, 16], evaluating both adequacy (fruits, vegetables, 
whole grains) and moderation components (sodium, 
added sugars, saturated fats). In adults, higher HEI-2015 
scores are consistently associated with cardioprotective 
effects, including a lower risk of hypertension [ 10, 17, 
21]. Emerging evidence, however, suggests similar ben -
efits in youth, with healthier dietary patterns (HEI-2015/
DASH-based) associated with significantly lower risks of 
elevated blood pressure and metabolic syndrome [ 5, 22, 
23]. However, the existing pediatric evidence remains 
incomplete and occasionally inconsistent. Some cross-
sectional studies support a beneficial association between 
diet quality and blood pressure in youth [24, 25], whereas 
other well-designed prospective cohort studies have not 
found a significant association between early diet quality 
scores and later blood pressure [ 26]. A systematic review 
also noted that the strength of evidence linking dietary 
quality indices to blood pressure in adolescents is mixed 
[27].
However, inconsistencies in prior studies may arise 
from an oversimplified linear modeling approach. This 
approach fails to capture the true complexity of the 
relationship. Emerging evidence indicates that the asso -
ciation between diet quality and health outcomes may 
involve threshold effects rather than a simple linear pat -
tern. For instance, in the general U.S. adult population, 
the relationship between the HEI-2015 and hypertension 
is significantly nonlinear and inverse. Notably, the pattern 
differs by gender, with a U-shaped curve observed among 
males [ 10]. More relevant to pediatric populations, a 
study of school-aged children found that an HEI-2015 
score above the 33rd percentile was linked to a substan -
tially reduced risk of insulin resistance, a key cardio -
metabolic risk factor [24]. These findings suggest that the 
health benefits of improved diet quality in youth may not 
follow a linear pattern, but instead increase in magnitude 
after crossing a particular quality threshold. The potential 
nonlinear association and threshold effect between diet 
quality and hypertension in children remains unclear.
Therefore, we examined the association between HEI-
2015 scores and hypertension prevalence among chil -
dren and adolescents using two decades of NHANES 
data (1999–2020). We aimed to investigate the potential 
nonlinear association and to identify a threshold for the 
HEI-2015 score in relation to hypertension among US 
children and adolescents.
Methods
Data source and study population
We performed a cross-sectional analysis of participants 
from the National Health and Nutrition Examination 
Survey (NHANES), spanning survey years 1999 through 
identified at an HEI-2015 score of ≈ 65. A formal test for threshold nonlinearity was significant (p = 0.012), confirming a 
change in slope at an HEI-2015 score of ≈ 65. Sensitivity analyses yielded consistent results.
Conclusions In this cross-sectional study, a higher HEI-2015 score was nonlinearly associated with a lower prevalence 
of hypertension in US children and adolescents, with an observed inflection point near a score of 65. This finding 
provides a specific, quantifiable hypothesis for future prospective studies investigating dietary thresholds for 
hypertension prevention in children and adolescents.
Contributions to the Literature 
Evidence supporting specific, quantifiable dietary thresholds for hypertension prevention in children and 
adolescents remains limited. The study identifies a specific HEI-2015 score threshold (≈65) that maybe associated 
with a lower prevalence of hypertension in children and adolescents. 
The study provides a data-derived target (HEI-2015≈65), which establishes a hypothesis for future longitudinal 
and intervention studies to validate its predictive value and explore its potential utility in preventing hypertension 
in children and adolescents.

For this study, we aggregated data from all NHANES 
cycles between 1999 and 2020 that included the neces -
sary variables. The study sample was restricted to chil -
dren and adolescents aged > 8 to < 18 years. We set the 
lower age limit at 8 years because reliable dietary recall 
data in NHANES are available starting around age 8 
(younger children’s data often involve proxy reporting). 
We further excluded individuals with missing or incom -
plete data on dietary intake ( n = 6444), blood pressure 
measurements ( n = 657). After applying inclusion and 
exclusion criteria, the final analytic sample consisted of 
N = 17,528 children and adolescents. The study flowchart 
was presented in Fig. 1.
Dietary assessment and HEI-2015 calculation
Detailed dietary intake data were collected from 
NHANES participants through two 24-h dietary recalls, 
which were conducted by a trained interviewer face-to-
face in the Mobile Examination Center (MEC) on the 
first day and a follow-up interview 3–10 days later via 
phone including both weekdays and weekends, to capture 
intra-individual variation in dietary intake. More details 
on dietary surveys and quality control can be found in 
the survey manual.  h t t p  s : /  / w w w  n .  c d c  . g o  v / n c  h s  / n h  a n e

and calculate multiple dietary quality indicators, includ -

nent scores were calculated by the simple HEI scoring 
algorithm using publicly available SAS macros [ 29]. The 
HEI-2015 comprises 13 dietary components: 9 adequacy 
components (Total Fruits, Whole Fruits, Total Vegeta -
bles, Greens and Beans, Whole Grains, Dairy, Total Pro -
tein Foods, Seafood/Plant Proteins, and Fatty Acids ratio) 
and 4 moderation components (Refined Grains, Sodium, 
Added Sugars, and Saturated Fats). Each component 
is scored on a scale (maximum 5 or 10 points) propor -
tional to the amount consumed, with higher intake of 
adequacy components and lower intake of moderation 
components yielding higher scores. Component scores 
are summed to produce a total HEI-2015 score ranging 
from 0 to 100, with 100 indicating perfect adherence to

Blood pressure measurement and hypertension definition
Blood pressure (BP) was measured by trained clini -
cians during the NHANES physical examination follow -
ing standard AHA protocols. Participants rested quietly 
seated for 5 min, and then three brachial blood pressure 
readings were obtained on the right arm (unless contra -
indicated) using an appropriate pediatric cuff size and a 
mercury sphygmomanometer or calibrated oscillomet -
ric device. The average of the three readings was used 
as the individual’s systolic blood pressure (SBP) and dia -
stolic blood pressure (DBP) for classification. If fewer 
than three readings were recorded (e.g. due to measure -
ment difficulty), the available readings were averaged. We 
defined hypertension in children and adolescents accord-
ing to the 2017 AAP Clinical Practice Guideline crite -
ria [ 31] (which updated the Fourth Report from 2004). 
Specifically, for participants aged < 13 years, hyperten -
sion was defined as an average SBP or DBP at or above 
the 95th percentile for their age, sex, and height percen -
tile, based on the updated reference tables derived from 
normal-weight children. Height percentiles for partici -
pants under 13 years were determined based on the CDC 
2000 Growth Charts, per the 2017 AAP guideline. For 
adolescents 13–18 years old, hypertension was defined 
according to the 2017 AAP clinical practice guideline 
as SBP ≥ 130 mmHg or DBP ≥ 80 mmHg (equivalent to 
the 95th percentile for 13 years old children) or higher, 
which uses the same blood pressure thresholds (≥ 130/80 
mmHg for stage 1 hypertension) as the American College 
of Cardiology/American Heart Association (ACC/AHA) 
guidelines for adults [ 32]. In addition, any youth report -
ing a previous physician diagnosis of hypertension or 
current use of antihypertensive medication was classified 
as having hypertension.
Covariates
Covariates were selected based on prior literature indi -
cating their association with both diet quality and 
blood pressure [ 33, 34].These covariates (all assessed via 
NHANES questionnaires or examination) were: sex, age, 
race/ethnicity, poverty income ratio, education level, 
Body Mass Index (BMI) Z-score, total physical activity, 
etc. Given its strong association with both diet and blood 
pressure, BMI Z-score was included in the model to 
account for this clinical correlation, even though it might 
also be a mediator.
Race/ethnicity were divided into five categories: non-
Hispanic White, non-Hispanic Black, Mexican Ameri -
can, Other Hispanic, or other races. The following 
educational levels were included: below high school, 
high school, and above high school. Socioeconomic sta -
tus was approximated by the poverty income ratio (PIR), 
a ratio of household income to the federal poverty level 
(adjusted for family size). PIR were divided into: Poor, 
Nearly poor, Middle income, High income. Body mass 
index (BMI) was calculated as weight (kg) divided by 
height (m) squared, using measured weight and height 
from the exam. For age < 20, BMI was interpreted via per-
centiles, we included BMI Z-score or percentile equiva -
lently to adjust for body size.
Statistical analysis
This study utilized cross-sectional data from NHANES. 
It is important to note that the findings represent obser -
vational associations and cannot establish causality or 
determine the direction of the relationships observed. 
Means and standard deviations were calculated for con -
tinuous variables and proportions for categorical vari -
ables. Dummy variables were used to indicate missing 
covariate values. Baseline characteristics were com -
pared across HEI-2015 tertiles using Kruskal-Wallis H 
test for continuous variables and the chi-square test (or 
Fisher’s exact test) for categorical variables. A logistic 
regression model was performed to estimate the asso -
ciation between the HEI-2015 and hypertension. The 
primary exposure, HEI-2015 score, was modeled both 
as a continuous variable and in categories (tertiles) for 
descriptive purposes. In the main models, we treated 
HEI-2015 as continuous and report the odds ratio (OR) 
for hypertension per a 10-point increment in HEI score, 
which roughly corresponds to a difference that might be 
achieved by making moderate dietary improvements. 
Considering the clinical significance, we adjusted for 
the following covariates: age, sex, race/ethnicity, poverty 
income ratio, education level, and BMI Z-score.
A generalized additive model (GAM) was used to 
explore potential non-linear association between the 
HEI-2015 and hypertension. The spline analysis sug -
gested a change in slope around the mid-range of HEI 
scores. We then used a two-piecewise linear regression 
model to examine the threshold effect of the HEI-2015 
on hypertension [35]. The turning point for the HEI-2015 
was determined using “exploratory” analyses, which is to 
move the trial turning point along the pre-defined inter -
val and pick up the one which gave maximum model like-
lihood. We also performed a log-likelihood ratio test and 
compared the one-line linear regression model with the 
two-piecewise linear model. The bootstrap resampling

Results
Participant characteristics
The baseline characteristics of the research participants 
was shown in Table  1. The analysis included 17,528 chil -
dren and adolescents aged 8 to 18 years from NHANES 
1999–2020. 8134 participants (46.41%) under the age of 
13, and 9394 participants (53.59%) over the age of 13. 
There were 8762 (49.99%) female participants. The racial/
ethnic composition was 27.49% non-Hispanic White, 
28.62% non-Hispanic Black, 35.34% Hispanic (inclusive 
of Mexican American and other Hispanic), and 8.56% 
other races, reflecting the diversity of the US population 
of youth. This group had a prevalence of 6.14% of hyper -
tension. We classified the participants according to their 
HEI-2015 tertiles (T1:12.36–41.47; T2:41.47–51.28; T3: 
51.28–95.65.). There is a significant difference among T1, 
T2, and T3 group in age, sex, family PIR, education level, 
BMI Z-score, total physical activity, etc. ( P<0.05). Com-
pared with T2 and T3 group, participants in the lowest 
tertile of the HEI-2015, had higher prevalence of hyper -
tension (6.73% vs. 6.23% and 5.56%). Survey-weighted 
analyses were performed to account for the NHANES 
complex survey design, and the core findings remained 
consistent, Supplementary Table 1.
Association between HEI-2015 and hypertension
Table  2 shows the results of the multivariate logistic 
regression models. When modeling HEI-2015 as a con -
tinuous variable across its full range, each 10-point 
increase in score was associated with a.
10% lower odds of hypertension (adjusted OR = 0.90, 
95% CI: 0.85–0.95; P = 0.0002) after adjusting for sex, 
age, race/ethnicity, PIR, parental education, and BMI 
Z-score. The P for trend across the model was < 0.05. 
Given the potential non-linear relationship, we exam -
ined the association by categorizing participants into 
HEI-2015 tertiles. Compared to the low HEI-2015 group 
(12.36–41.47), the high and middle HEI-2015 group 
(range 41.47–51.28 and 51.28–95.65) had a lower odds 
of hypertension(OR = 0.83, 95% CI 0.71–0.97; OR = 0.94, 
95% CI 0.81–1.09).
A sensitivity analysis was conducted to assess the pos -
sibility that BMI Z-score, a potential mediator, could 
introduce overadjustment bias and attenuate the true 
association. The association between HEI-2015 and 
hypertension was robust in the sensitivity analysis that 
excluded BMI Z-score from the model (Supplemen -
tary Table 2). The effect estimates remained virtually 
unchanged: the OR for hypertension per 10-unit increase 
in HEI-2015 was 0.90 (95% CI: 0.85, 0.95) in both the pri -
mary and the sensitivity model.
Identification of nonlinear relationship
We observed a nonlinear relationship between the HEI-
2015 and hypertension (Fig.  2; Table  3). Using two-
piecewise liner logistic models, we identified a threshold 
HEI-2015 score of approximately 65.1(95% CI: 60.15–
66.87) as the point at which the association with hyper -
tension changed. When we allowed for different slopes 
below and above HEI = 65.13, the model fit improved 
significantly compared to a single linear term. The Log -
arithm Likelihood Ratio Test (LRT) corroborated the 
non-linear model’s superiority with a significant P-value 
of 0.012, affirming the relevance of threshold effects in 
dietary assessments. Sensitivity analysis was performed 
excluding the adjustment for BMI Z-score, and the result 
was consistent (Supplementary Fig.  1). Survey-weighted 
analyses were performed and the core findings remained 
consistent, Supplementary Table 3.
Below the threshold (HEI-2015 < 65.13): The associa-
tion between diet quality and hypertension was weakly 
inverse. Each 10-point increase in HEI-2015 score was 
associated with a 7% reduction in the odds of hyperten -
sion (adjusted OR = 0.93, 95% CI: 0.87–0.99).
Above the threshold (HEI-2015 ≥ 65.13): The inverse 
relationship between diet quality and hypertension 
became much stronger. For each 10-point increase in 
HEI above 65, the adjusted OR for hypertension was 0.48 
(95% CI: 0.28–0.83; p = 0.0086). This suggests that among 
children already consuming a relatively healthy diet 
(HEI-2015 ≥ 65.13), further improvements in diet quality 
have a large payoff in terms of blood pressure reduction.

Discussion
Our analysis of U.S. children and adolescents observed 
a significant nonlinear association between overall diet 
quality, measured by the Healthy Eating Index-2015 
(HEI-2015), and hypertension prevalence. Specifically, a 
threshold effect was identified at an HEI-2015 score of 
approximately 65. Below this inflection point, the inverse 
association between HEI-2015 scores and hypertension 
prevalence was modest. Above this threshold, however, 
this inverse association was stronger. This indicates that 
the potential benefits of dietary quality on blood pres -
sure may not be uniform but become more pronounced 
once diet quality aligns closely with national dietary 
guidelines. These findings underscore the importance of 
overall dietary patterns, rather than isolated nutrients, in 
relation to cardiovascular health indicators in the pediat -
ric population. To our knowledge, this is one of the first 
studies to report such a nonlinear, threshold association 
between diet quality and hypertension in a nationally 
representative sample of children and adolescents.
Previous studies in adults have consistently shown 
that higher quality diets are linked to lower incidence 
of hypertension [ 23, 36]. Weng et al. analyzed NHANES 
data in adults and found an inverse association between 
HEI-2015 and hypertension prevalence [ 10]. They also 
noted non-linearity and even a U-shaped association in 
men (with extremely high HEI men having slightly higher 
BP , possibly due to other factors). Ducharme-Smith et al. 
(2021) found that African American adolescents who fol -
lowed a DASH-style or Alternate Healthy Eating pattern 
had significantly lower risks of both hypertension and 
metabolic syndrome compared to those with poorer diets 
[23]. Previous pediatric studies have typically assessed 
the diet-hypertension association either as a linear trend

or across broad diet quality categories. These meth -
ods do not formally test for a change in the slope of the 
association. Expanding on these findings, our study used 
piecewise regression and observed a consistent inverse 
association between HEI-2015 and hypertension across 
all demographic subgroups analyzed, including both 
sexes, various age groups, and different racial/ethnic 
backgrounds within a nationally representative pediatric 
sample. A key distinction of our study is the identifica -
tion of a non-linear, threshold association, with a more 
pronounced inverse relationship above an HEI-2015 
score of approximately 65. Furthermore, in contrast to 
the U-shaped pattern observed in some adult males [ 10], 
our analysis in children and adolescents did not indicate 
any adverse association at very high HEI-2015 scores. 
The relationship remained monotonic and beneficial 
after adjustment for confounders. This difference may be 
attributable to differences in dietary behavior, metabo -
lism, and blood pressure regulation between children and 
adults. There are generally less extreme dietary variations 
or differing physiological responses in younger popula -
tions compared to adults.
The mechanisms underlying the inverse non-linear 
threshold association between diet quality and hyperten -
sion remain unclear. Although an HEI score of 65 still 
represents suboptimal diet quality, it likely represents 
a meaningful shift toward a dietary pattern character -
ized by increased consumption of fruits, vegetables, and 
whole grains, coupled with a reduced intake of added 
sugars and sodium [16]. The observed inverse association 
above this threshold may be explained by several factors. 
First, blood pressure may exhibit nonlinear responses to 
nutrients, where sufficiently high potassium intake (from 
fruits/vegetables) combined with sufficiently low sodium 
intake may trigger significant improvements in vascu -
lar endothelial function [ 37, 38]. The HEI-2015 thresh -
old may mark a critical shift, where combined intake of 
beneficial nutrients (e.g., potassium, fiber) reaches a level 
for potential synergistic benefits on vascular endothelial 
function, alongside meaningful reductions in adverse 
factors (e.g., sodium, added sugars). This may provide a 
more plausible biological explanation for the nonlinear

association. These findings align with DASH diet studies 
demonstrating that fruit- and vegetable-rich dietary pat -
terns effectively lower blood pressure in both adults and 
adolescents [ 39, 40]. While our study was not designed 
to test specific biological pathways, these explanations 
remain speculative and are not directly supported by 
biomarker or nutrient-specific analyses within the study. 
This threshold may reflect statistical artifact rather than 
a true physiological inflection point. Second, higher diet 
quality often coexists with healthier lifestyle behaviors 
[41]. Thus, the association above the threshold may par -
tially reflect the synergistic benefits of a healthier over -
all lifestyle, rather than diet al.one. These mechanisms 
remain speculative in the context of our data and warrant 
investigation in future studies with biomarker measure -
ments. Consequently, this identified threshold may pro -
vide a useful reference point for informing future dietary 
guidelines and lifestyle strategies related to cardiovascu -
lar health in the pediatric population.
Consistent with existing study, elevated body mass 
index (BMI) is a well-established risk factor associ -
ated with hypertension [ 42] and increased weight, and 
obesity may be major contributors to the hyperten -
sion burden [ 43]. Our study extends this understanding 
by demonstrating that the inverse association between 
higher HEI-2015 scores and lower hypertension preva -
lence remained significant after adjustment for BMI 
and socioeconomic confounders. This suggests an inde -
pendent association of overall diet quality with blood 
pressure outcomes, irrespective of adiposity status [ 43], 
which in turn affect hypertension risk. Notably, broader 
public health improvements, including reductions in 
sugar-sweetened beverage and sodium consumption, 
have been speculated to contribute to observed declines 
in adolescent blood pressure trends [ 43, 44]. In clinical 
practice, dietary modification, including caloric restric -
tion, forms a cornerstone of first-line therapy for obesity 
and its cardiometabolic comorbidities [45]. From a public 
health perspective, our findings suggest that enhancing 
diet quality toward higher HEI-2015 scores may be con -
sidered in efforts to prevent hypertension in the pediat -
ric population. While the possibility that this threshold 
reflects statistical artifact rather than a true physiologi -
cal inflection point, future prospective and interventional 
studies are required to validate its predictive and causal 
relationship with hypertension risk in children and ado -
lescents. Specifically, we note that the advanced machine 
learning algorithms, such as those applied in chronic dis -
ease prediction [ 46], represent a promising future direc -
tion for validating and refining dietary threshold models 
in pediatric populations.
This study has several limitations. First, the cross-sec -
tional nature of our data prevents us from establishing 
causal relationships. Although we adjusted for multiple 
confounders and the biological plausibility supports diet 
influencing blood pressure, we cannot rule out reverse 
causation (e.g. children with elevated blood pressure 
may modify their diets). Second, the composite nature 
of our hypertension outcome (based on measurement, 
self-report, and medication) is a limitation, as it may 
introduce misclassification. Third, dietary intake was 
assessed using two 24-hour recalls, which, despite being 
standard in NHANES, remain vulnerable to recall bias, 
reporting error, and short-term dietary variability that 
may not accurately reflect habitual intake in children 
and adolescents. Additionally, the use of HEI-2015 as a 
composite exposure obscures the contribution of individ-
ual dietary components, limiting interpretability of the 
observed associations. Fourth, we were unable to adjust 
for important pediatric determinants of blood pressure 
(e.g., pubertal status, family history, or sleep patterns) 
due to data limitations, which may affect the causal inter-
pretation of the observed associations. Our adjustment 
for certain variables, such as BMI-Z score and physical 
activity are not only potential confounders but may also 
be mediators on the causal pathway between diet qual -
ity and hypertension. By adjusting for them, we may 
have partially estimated the direct effect of diet that is 
not mediated through these channels, which could lead 
to an underestimation of the total effect. Regarding BMI 
Z-score adjustment, we acknowledge the theoretical con-
cern of overadjustment bias if BMI acts as a mediator. 
However, our sensitivity analysis (excluding BMI Z-score) 
yielded nearly identical effect estimates, indicating the 
core association was robust. This suggests the diet-hyper-
tension link in our sample may operate through pathways 
largely independent of concurrent BMI, or that BMI’s 
mediating role is minimal.
In contrast, our study has notable strengths. It utilizes 
a large, nationally representative dataset with rigorous 
diet and blood pressure measurement protocols, enhanc -
ing generalizability to the US youth population. The 
HEI-2015 effectively captures overall diet quality, and 
considering non-linear effects adds depth to understand -
ing the diet and blood pressure relationship. Our robust 
associations remained significant despite adjusting for 
various confounders, which is timely given emerging pre-
ventive cardiology strategies emphasizing the importance 
of adopting healthy eating patterns from childhood.
Conclusions
In conclusion, this cross-sectional study of U.S. children 
and adolescents from 1999 to 2020 reveals an inverse 
nonlinear association between higher adherence to 
dietary guidelines, measured by HEI-2015 scores, and 
the prevalence of hypertension in US children and ado -
lescents. Furthermore, our analysis identified a potential 
inflection point around a score of 65, beyond which the

tension risk in children and adolescents.
