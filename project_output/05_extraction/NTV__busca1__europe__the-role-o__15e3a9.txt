Abstract
Background Gut microbiota modulation has been proposed as a potential intervention for managing obesity. This 
meta-analysis aimed to evaluate the effects of prebiotic/probiotic/synbiotic supplementation on metabolic syndrome 
risk factors in obese pediatrics.
Methods A comprehensive search was conducted in databases up to January 2025. Randomized controlled 
trials (RCTs) evaluating prebiotics/probiotics/synbiotics in children and adolescents with overweight/obesity were 
included. The outcomes were body weight (BW), body mass index (BMI), BMI-z score, fasting blood sugar (FBS), 
homeostatic model assessment for insulin resistance (HOMA-IR), insulin, total cholesterol (TC), triglycerides (TG), low-
density lipoprotein-cholesterol (LDL-C), and high-density lipoprotein-cholesterol (HDL-C). Data were pooled using a 
random-effects model.
Results Prebiotic supplementation was associated with significant reductions in weight (SMD = − 0.81; 95% CI: − 1.44 
to − 0.19) and BMI (SMD = − 0.76; 95% CI: − 1.38 to − 0.14), whereas BMI z-scores remained unchanged (p > 0.05). 
Probiotics and synbiotics did not significantly affect weight, BMI, or BMI z-scores (p > 0.05). Glycemic and lipid profile 
parameters were not significantly altered by any biotic supplementation (p > 0.05). Subgroup analyses by intervention 
type, duration, sample size, or baseline BMI did not reveal consistent effects (p > 0.05).
Conclusion Biotic supplementation has not been shown to consistently improve metabolic syndrome risk factors in 
overweight and obese children, except for a modest beneficial effect of prebiotics on weight and BMI (with very-low 
certainly of evidence); however, alternative probiotic organisms or formulations not tested to date may have different 
effects.

Introduction
Childhood obesity is a major global public health con -
cern. In China, the prevalence of obesity between 2015 
and 2019 was reported at 7.9% among children and 3.6% 
among adolescents [ 1]. According to the World Health 
Organization (WHO), the number of overweight or 
obese individuals aged 5–19 worldwide reached nearly

about one-third of children in Europe were overweight or 
obese [2]. In low- and middle-income countries, the dual 
burden of rising obesity among children under five and 
school-aged adolescents, alongside persistent undernu -
trition, poses an additional public health challenge [3, 4].
The gut microbiota has recently been recognized as a 
pivotal environmental determinant in the progression of 
obesity and its associated metabolic disturbances. Con -
ventional strategies, including lifestyle modifications 
and calorie restriction diets, remain the cornerstone of 
weight management, and emerging evidence suggests 
that these approaches may also influence the gastroin -
testinal microbiome [ 5– 9]. Data from animal experi -
ments and meta-analyses of human studies generally 
support the beneficial role of probiotics and synbiotics 
in modulating gut microbiota to promote weight control 
and improve metabolic outcomes. In contrast, evidence 
from clinical trials on prebiotics has been less consistent 
[10]. The gut microbiota contributes to the regulation of 
energy homeostasis and the secretion of appetite-sup -
pressing hormones, making its modulation a promising 
target for obesity prevention [ 11– 14]. A growing body of 
evidence underscores the critical role of gut dysbiosis in 
the development of metabolic disorders, such as obesity 
and type 2 diabetes [15, 16].
The FAO/WHO defines probiotics as live microorgan -
isms that confer health benefits when consumed in ade -
quate amounts, while prebiotics are fermentable dietary 
fibers selectively utilized by host microorganisms to pro -
mote probiotic growth. Synbiotics refer to supplements 
combining both probiotics and prebiotics [ 17, 18]. Pre -
vious studies have suggested that probiotics, prebiotics, 
and synbiotics may positively influence body weight and 
body mass index (BMI) in overweight and obese indi -
viduals [19], with potential additional benefits for glucose 
regulation [20] and lipid metabolism, thereby supporting 
cardiovascular health [ 21]. Existing evidence on effect 
of biotic on anthropometric indices, lipid and glycemic 
profile, in children and adolescence with overweight 
and obesity have shown conflicting results [ 22– 24]. To 
address this gap, we conducted the first GRADE-based 
systematic review and meta-analysis evaluating the 
impact of probiotic, prebiotic, and synbiotic supple -
mentation on anthropometric indices, lipid levels, and 
glycemic markers in both adolescents and children living 
with overweight/obesity.
Methods
Search strategy
Computerized searches were conducted on PubMed, 
Web of Science, and Scopus from inception to July 2025. 
The search strategy included terms related to microbi -
ome-modulating interventions the target population, and 
the study design, such as (‘Probiotic, ’ ‘Lactobacillus,’  ‘Lac-
ticaseibacillus,’  ‘Limosilactobacillus,’  ‘Latilactobacillus,’  
‘Bifidobacterium,’  ‘Enterococcus, ’ ‘Prebiotic, ’ ‘Oligosaccha-
rides, ’ ‘Galacto-oligosaccharide, ’ ‘Fructooligosaccharide, ’ 
‘Synbiotic’) combined with terms related to obesity and 
overweight (‘Obesity, ’ ‘Overweight, ’ ‘ Adiposity, ’ ‘ Adipose 
Tissue Hyperplasia’) and the population (‘Child, ’ ‘ Adoles-
cent, ’ ‘Teen, ’ ‘Youth, ’ ‘Juvenile, ’ ‘Minors’), as well as study 
design filters (‘Randomized Controlled Trial, ’ ‘Placebo, ’ 
‘Blind, ’ ‘Controlled Trial’). The specific strategy of each 
database is provided in Supplementary materials . No 
language and date restrictions were applied to maxi -
mize the inclusion of eligible studies. In addition to peer-
reviewed publications, we included grey literature (e.g., 
conference abstracts, theses, and trial registries) to mini -
mize the publication bias.
Inclusion and exclusion criteria
Two reviewers independently screened RCTs for eligi -
bility based on the predefined inclusion and exclusion 
criteria. Reviewer disagreements were resolved through 
discussion, with a third reviewer consulted when neces -
sary. The population of interest was children and adoles -
cents aged 2–20 years with overweight or obesity, defined 
as BMI-for-age and sex at or above the 85th percentile. 
Eligible interventions were supplementation with prebi -
otics, probiotics, or synbiotics, and we only included pro-
biotic trials that clearly identified the organisms or strains 
administered. Studies that described interventions solely 
in generic terms, such as “fermented milk” without speci-
fication of microbial content, were excluded. Compara -
tors included placebo, usual care, or no intervention. The 
outcomes were body weight (BW), BMI, BMI-z score, 
fasting blood sugar (FBS), homeostatic model assessment 
for insulin resistance (HOMA-IR), insulin, total cho -
lesterol (TC), triglycerides (TG), low- and high-density 
lipoprotein-cholesterol (LDL-C and HDL-C), and high-
density lipoprotein-cholesterol (HDL-C). Only RCTs 
that reported mean changes with standard deviations 
or provided sufficient data to calculate effect sizes were 
included.
Studies were excluded if full-text data were unavail -
able, if they were published in non-English languages, 
if relevant outcomes were missing, if no control group 
was included, or if they were duplicates. Observational

studies, case reports, and other non-randomized designs, 
as well as trials conducted exclusively in adult popula -
tions, were also excluded.
Screenings and data extraction
Study selection and data extraction were performed inde-
pendently by two reviewers. Any disagreements were 
discussed and resolved with input from a third investi -
gator. A standardized data collection form was used to 
extract information from each eligible study, including: 
first author, year of publication, country, study design, 
sample size, age of participants, duration and dosage of 
probiotic/prebiotic/synbiotic supplementation, follow-
up period, type of intervention, and reported outcomes. 
Extracted outcomes encompassed anthropometric indi -
ces (weight, BMI, BMI-z score, FBG, insulin, HOMA-IR, 
TC, TG, HDL-C, and LDL-C).
Two reviewers independently screened titles/abstracts 
and full texts. Inter-rater reliability was assessed using 
Cohen’s kappa (κ), with discrepancies resolved by discus-
sion or adjudication by a third reviewer. The agreement 
between reviewers was substantial, with κ = 0.81 for title/
abstract screening and κ = 0.84 for full-text screening.
Risk of bias assessment and meta-evidence
We evaluate the quality of the included RCTs using Ver -
sion 2 of the Cochrane Risk-of-Bias tool (RoB 2) [ 25]. 
This tool looks at five domains where bias might occur: 
the randomization process, deviations from intended 
interventions, missing outcome data, outcome measure -
ment, and selection of reported results. After reviewing 
these domains, each study was given an overall rating of 
‘low risk, ’ ‘some concerns, ’ or ‘high risk’ of bias [26]. Dis-
crepancies were resolved through discussion or adjudica -
tion by a third reviewer.
The overall certainty of evidence for each outcome 
across RCTs was assessed using the GRADE framework 
[27]. Evidence from randomized controlled trials began 
as high certainty and was downgraded by one or two 
levels if concerns were identified regarding risk of bias, 
inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, or publication 
bias. Two reviewers conducted the GRADE assessments 
independently, and disagreements were resolved through 
discussion.
Statistical analysis
The meta-analysis was conducted using STATA ver -
sion 16 (Stata Corp LP , College Station, TX, USA). Effect 
sizes were expressed as standardized mean differences 
(Hedges’ g) with 95% confidence intervals. Hedges’ g 
was chosen over Cohen’s d to account for small sample 
sizes in several included trials [ 28], as it provides an 
unbiased estimate of standardized effect sizes in meta-
analyses with limited study populations. Effect sizes were 
calculated using the mean changes and standard devia -
tions (SDs) of lipid profiles, obesity, and glycemic indices 
from baseline to the final follow-up in both intervention 
and control groups. When trials did not report changes 
in mean and SD, we derived change scores by subtract -
ing baseline from final values, and calculated SDs using 
the formula recommended in the Cochrane Handbook, 
assuming a correlation coefficient of 0.5 between base -
line and follow-up values [ 29]. Between-study heteroge -
neity was assessed using the I² index, with values greater 
than 50% considered indicative of substantial heteroge -
neity. Subgroup analyses were pre-specified in the study 
protocol and included intervention type, study duration, 
sample size, and BMI to identify potential sources of het -
erogeneity. To examine the robustness of the results, sen-
sitivity analyses were carried out by removing one study 
at a time and observing its effect on the overall effect 
size. Publication bias was assessed using funnel plots only 
when at least 10 studies were available for an outcome, 
as well as Egger’s regression test and Begg’s rank corre -
lation test. Where evidence of publication bias was sug -
gested, the trim-and-fill method was applied to estimate 
the number of potentially missing studies and to provide 
adjusted pooled effect sizes. A p-value < 0.05 was deemed 
statistically significant for all analyses.
Results
Study selection
The initial database search yielded 1218 records. After 
eliminating 279 duplicates, 939 unique articles remained 
for screening by title and abstract. Of these, 911 were 
excluded because they did not align with the predefined 
objectives, population, or outcomes. Full texts of 28 
potentially relevant articles were reviewed in detail, lead -
ing to the exclusion of 11 studies. In total, 17 studies ful -
filled the eligibility criteria and were included in the final 
meta-analysis (Fig. 1).
Study characteristics
Among the included RCTs, weight was reported in ten 
RCTs [22, 24, 30– 37], BMI in eight RCTs [ 22– 24, 30, 32, 
35– 37], BMI-z score in ten RCTs [ 22, 31, 33– 35, 37– 41]. 
Glycemic outcomes were less consistently assessed: 
FBS was reported in eight RCTs [ 24, 31, 35, 37, 39– 42], 
HOMA-IR in four RCTs [ 31, 35, 37, 39], insulin in five 
RCTs [31, 35, 37, 39, 43], and Lipid profile parameters, 
including TC, LDL-C, HDL-C, and TG, were reported in 
seven studies [31, 32, 35, 39– 42]. Six studies investigated 
probiotics [ 22– 24, 31, 37, 39], six evaluated synbiot -
ics [30, 32– 35, 42], and five examined prebiotics [ 36, 38, 
40, 41, 43]. The included trials were published between 
2012 and 2023, with participant ages ranging from 6 to 
18 years and sample sizes varying from 9 to 103 partici -
pants. Intervention durations varied between 4 and 24

Risk of bias assessment and meta-evidence
Some RCTs had concerns related to outcome measure -
ment or reporting, and three were rated as high overall 
risk [ 22, 34, 36]. The methodological quality of the eli -
gible RCTs is detailed in Table 2. The quality of evidence

Biotic supplementation and obesity indices
The pooled analysis demonstrated a significant weight 
(SMD = −0.81; 95% CI: −1.44 to −0.19; p = 0.047) 
(Fig.  2A), and BMI reduction (SMD = −0.76; 95% CI: 
−1.38 to −0.14; p = 0.047) (Fig. 2B), but not BMI-z scores 
(SMD = 0.06; 95% CI: −0.63 to 0.74; p = 0.079) (Fig. 2C) 
following prebiotics supplementation. The pooled analy -
sis demonstrated that probiotics and synbiotics supple -
mentation did not significantly reduce weight, BMI, and 
BMI z-scores (Fig.  2A-C). Overall heterogeneity was 
substantial for weight (Q = 64.93, p < 0.001; I² = 90.0%), 
BMI (Q = 23.26, p = 0.002; I² = 70.67%), and BMI z-score 
(Q = 19.69, p = 0.020; I² = 55.62%). Subgroup analyses 
indicated that intervention type, study duration, sam -
ple size, and BMI contributed to heterogeneity. Obesity 
indices remained unchanged across all examined sub -
groups. No significant effects were observed according to 
BMI classification, other intervention types, duration of 
supplementation, or sample size, with all p-values > 0.05 
(Supplementary File).

Discussion
In this meta-analysis of 17 randomized controlled tri -
als ( n = 1,391) involving overweight and obese children 
and adolescents, we found no significant pooled effects 
of probiotic, prebiotic, or synbiotic supplementation on 
anthropometric indices (weight, BMI, BMI z-score), gly -
cemic outcomes (fasting glucose, insulin, HOMA-IR), or 
lipid parameters (TC, LDL-C, HDL-C, TG). Subgroup 
findings suggested possible benefits in isolated cases. 
For instance, one prebiotic trial reported reductions 
in weight and BMI, but overall results were consistent 
across sensitivity analyses. Based on our results, routine 
supplementation with prebiotics, probiotics, or synbiot -
ics for weight management in overweight or obese chil -
dren cannot be recommended. It remains possible that 
specific subgroups, for example, youths with pronounced 
metabolic syndrome or distinct gut dysbiosis, could ben -
efit more, but current evidence is insufficient to identify

these populations. Overall, the findings underscore the 
need for individualized approaches rather than universal 
recommendations.
Our conclusions are broadly aligned with prior pedi -
atric reviews. Mohammadi et al. reported no significant 
improvements in anthropometric or metabolic outcomes 
with probiotics/synbiotics over 4–16 weeks [ 44], while 
Li et al. found probiotic supplementation improved lipid 
and adipokine profiles but did not affect BMI [ 45]. How-
ever, they had limited number of studies; therefore, inter-
pretation must be performed with precaution. Duan et al. 
observed modest BMI and fasting glucose reductions but 
paradoxical increases in lipid markers [46]. Their analysis 
included only 8 probiotic trials (mostly from China) with 
approximately 390 adolescents [46], whereas our analysis 
included broader biotic interventions and participants. 
More recently, a Bayesian network meta-analysis sug -
gested that multi-strain probiotics could reduce BMI, 
waist circumference, lipids, leptin, and hs-CRP , whereas 
synbiotics offered smaller benefits and sometimes raised 
fasting glucose [ 47]. In contrast, synbiotic supplemen -
tation was associated with modest reductions in BMI 
z-score and LDL-C, but also with a slight increase in fast-
ing glucose [ 47]. Evidence for single-strain probiotics or 
prebiotics was weaker and less consistent [ 47]. Overall, 
multi-strain probiotic formulations appeared to show 
the greatest potential for improving obesity-related out -
comes in children and adolescents [ 47]. The differences 
between our results and those of Zhang et al. (2024) [ 47] 
may be partly explained by methodological approaches. 
Their Bayesian network meta-analysis incorporated 
both direct and indirect comparisons to rank multiple 
interventions, which can generate broader insights but 
depends on assumptions of transitivity and consistency 
that may not always be valid in pediatric populations 
where strains, dosages, and dietary contexts vary consid -
erably. By contrast, our frequentist pairwise meta-anal -
ysis relied solely on direct trial evidence, avoiding prior 
assumptions and yielding more conservative estimates 
that may better reflect actual trial data.
Findings in children and adolescents also diverge from 
those in adults. A large meta-analysis of more than 200 
RCTs involving over 12,600 adults reported that probi -
otics and synbiotics led to small but statistically signifi -
cant improvements, including modest weight loss (–0.91 
kg), BMI reduction (–0.28 kg/m²), and decreases in waist 
circumference and fat mass [ 48]. Such differences may 
reflect developmental factors: children and adolescents 
are still undergoing growth, with unique hormonal and 
metabolic changes, and their gut microbiota remain in 
the process of maturation. These features could limit or 
alter their responsiveness to microbiome-targeted thera -
pies compared with adults [49].
Mechanistically, biotics are thought to influence obe -
sity and metabolism primarily through modulation of the 
gut microbiota. Prebiotics promote growth of beneficial 
bacteria like Bifidobacteria and Lactobacilli, leading to 
increased production of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) 
including acetate, propionate, and butyrate [ 50]. SCFAs 
can activate gut epithelial receptors (GPR41/43) to stim -
ulate satiety hormones such as glucagon-like peptide-1 
(GLP-1) and peptide YY (PYY), potentially reducing food 
intake. They may also regulate lipid metabolism and adi -
pogenesis by upregulating peroxisome proliferator-acti -
vated receptors α and γ (PPARα/γ) and downregulating 
sterol regulatory element-binding protein-1c (SREBP-1c) 
[51]. Probiotics can enhance gut barrier integrity, reduce 
endotoxin translocation, lower systemic inflammation, 
and improve insulin sensitivity [ 51]. Biotics may further 
influence bile acid metabolism and energy harvest.
Despite these plausible pathways, the clinical impact 
in pediatric populations appears limited. Longer inter -
vention periods may be required to produce measurable 
anthropometric changes. Strain specificity is also impor -
tant; multi-strain formulations (as noted by Zhang et al. 
[47]) may be more effective than single-strain products. 
Host factors, including baseline diet, genetics, and gut 
microbiota composition, likely contribute to individual 
variability in response. In summary, although mechanis -
tic evidence supports potential metabolic benefits, our 
pooled data indicate that effects of biotics on weight, gly -
cemia, and lipids in children and adolescents are small 
and inconsistent.
This study has several limitations. The included trials 
differed considerably in participant age, baseline obe -
sity severity, probiotic strains, prebiotic types, dosages, 
and intervention durations (ranging from 4 to 24 weeks), 
which contributed to substantial statistical heterogeneity 
(e.g., I² >80% for weight), making pooled estimates less 
precise. Second, some outcomes, such as HOMA-IR and 
insulin, were reported in only a few studies, resulting in 
limited evidence for these measures. Third, since most 
of the included studies did not report results separately 
for children and adolescents, we were unable to perform 
subgroup analyses based on age groups. Fourth, low het -
erogeneity in FBS, HOMA-IR, insulin, TC, LDL-c, and 
TG values suggests consistency across the included stud -
ies. However, this should be interpreted with caution, as 
the number of studies for these outcomes was limited.
The strengths of this study include the comprehensive 
inclusion of all available RCTs evaluating prebiotics, pro -
biotics, and synbiotics in children and adolescents with 
obesity. To our knowledge, this represents the largest 
pediatric-focused meta-analysis on this topic. We inves -
tigated potential sources of heterogeneity by conducting 
stratified analyses based on intervention type, study dura-
tion, and sample size. The review adhered to PRISMA 
Page 11 of 12
Xie et al. BMC Pediatrics          (2025) 25:991 
guidelines, and the quality of evidence was assessed using 
the GRADE framework. Additionally, sensitivity analyses 
(leave-one-out) and tests for publication bias were per -
formed across all outcomes to ensure the robustness of 
our findings.
Conclusion
Biotic supplementation has not been shown to con -
sistently improve metabolic syndrome risk factors in 
overweight and obese children, except for a modest 
beneficial effect of prebiotics on weight and BMI (with 
very-low certainly of evidence); however, alternative 
probiotic organisms or formulations not tested to date 
may have different effects. Overall, these findings do not 
support the routine use of biotics for pediatric weight 
management, and future research should focus on lon -
ger-duration trials, specific subgroups with metabolic 
dysregulation, and standardized intervention protocols 
to clarify potential benefits.
